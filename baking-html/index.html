The science and art of baking — bread, pastry, and the oven's alchemy

Bread

Sourdough

Wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria give sourdough its tang and open crumb. A healthy starter is 100% hydration fed daily. Hydration levels range from 65% (firm) to 85%+ (open crumb). Scoring patterns control oven spring direction.

Wild yeastHigh hydrationLong ferment

Baguette

The French icon: flour, water, salt, yeast — nothing more. Achieved with a pre-ferment (poolish or biga), high-temperature baking (480°F+), and steam injection in the first 10 minutes to create the signature thin, crispy crust.

PoolishSteam bakingCrispy crust

Focaccia

Italian flatbread with abundant olive oil — both in the dough and pooled on top before baking. High hydration (80%+) creates a light, airy interior. Dimpling with fingers creates pockets for oil and toppings like rosemary, olives, or flaky salt.

Olive oilHigh hydrationDimpling

Whole Wheat

Bran and germ reduce gluten network strength, producing denser loaves. Typical approach: 30–50% whole wheat to white flour ratio. The bran cuts gluten strands — soaking bran in water before mixing (autolyse) helps. Richer, nuttier flavor than white bread.

BranDense crumbNutty

Ciabatta

Italian "slipper bread" with extremely high hydration (75–85%). The slack dough is never kneaded vigorously — stretch-and-fold develops structure. Results in an irregular, large-holed crumb perfect for absorbing olive oil. Baked on a hot stone or steel.

Very high hydrationOpen crumbStretch & fold

Brioche

Enriched dough with eggs and generous butter (up to 50% of flour weight). Butter is incorporated after gluten development in a stand mixer — too early and it blocks gluten formation. Rich golden color from egg wash; tender, almost cake-like crumb. Foundation for French toast.

Enriched50% butterEgg wash

Rye Bread

Rye flour contains pentosans rather than gluten-forming proteins, creating a dense, sticky dough. Sourdough leavening is traditional and ideal — the acidity activates amylase inhibitors that prevent a gummy crumb. Dark rye (pumpernickel) uses coarse-ground whole rye berries.

PentosansDenseSourdough preferred

Pretzel

The distinctive mahogany crust and chewy texture come from dipping dough in a lye (sodium hydroxide) or baking soda solution before baking. The alkaline bath raises surface pH, accelerating Maillard browning. Coarse salt adheres to the sticky surface before baking.

Lye bathMaillard browningChewy

Pastry

Croissant

Laminated dough with 27 or 54 butter layers. The process: make a détrempe (enriched dough), encase a beurrage (butter block), then fold and roll three times using the "letter fold." Cold rest between folds is critical. Steam from butter layers causes dramatic puffing in the oven.

Lamination27 layersDétrempe

Puff Pastry (Feuilletage)

Up to 729 paper-thin layers created by 6 book folds. Uses no yeast — lift comes entirely from steam between butter layers. The dough must stay cold throughout; warm butter absorbs into dough rather than staying separate. Rough puff uses less precise folding for a quicker result.

729 layersNo yeastSteam lift

Choux Pastry

Cooked on the stovetop, then eggs beaten in to create a glossy, pipeable paste. Steam from water content causes hollow centers — perfect for éclairs, profiteroles, and cream puffs. Cooking the panade (flour-water-butter base) gelatinizes starch, enabling it to absorb many eggs.

PanadeHollow interiorÉclairs

Shortcrust Pastry

Fat coats flour particles before any water is added, limiting gluten development. Ratio: 2:1 flour to fat. "Short" means tender and crumbly — the opposite of chewy. Overworking activates gluten and toughens the crust. Resting in the fridge relaxes any developed gluten. Used for tarts and quiches.

Fat-firstTenderTarts

Filo / Phyllo

Paper-thin unleavened sheets stretched until translucent. Each sheet is brushed with clarified butter or oil before layering. Used in baklava, spanakopita, börek. Commercial filo is made by machine; hand-stretching on a large table cloth is the traditional method. Dries out quickly — keep covered.

Paper-thinButter layeredBaklava

Danish Pastry

Yeast-leavened laminated dough — a hybrid of croissant technique and enriched dough. Softer and sweeter than croissant due to more sugar and sometimes milk in the détrempe. Typically filled with cream cheese, fruit, or almond paste. Fewer layers (16–27) than classic croissant.

Yeast + laminationSweetFilled

Cakes

Sponge Cake (Biscuit)

Whole eggs beaten with sugar until pale and tripled in volume (ribbon stage). Flour folded in gently — no fat, no leavening agent. Lift comes entirely from trapped air. Light, dry texture ideal for layered cakes soaked with simple syrup. Base of bûche de Noël and Swiss roll.

No fatAir-leavenedRibbon stage

Génoise

Similar to sponge but with melted butter folded in last, adding richness and moisture. Eggs and sugar are warmed over a bain-marie before whipping, helping achieve greater volume. The butter must be cooled and folded quickly to avoid deflating the foam.

Bain-marieMelted butterLayered

Pound Cake

Originally one pound each of flour, butter, eggs, and sugar. The creaming method traps air in the fat-sugar matrix. Dense, rich, buttery crumb. Modern recipes adjust ratios and add baking powder for more lift. Lemon or vanilla flavoring is classic. Excellent toasted.

1:1:1:1 ratioCreaming methodDense

Angel Food Cake

Uses only egg whites, beaten to stiff peaks, with no fat at all. Cream of tartar stabilizes the meringue. Cake flour (lower protein) keeps texture delicate. Baked in an ungreased tube pan so the batter can cling and rise — and inverted while cooling to prevent collapse.

Whites onlyNo fatTube pan

Chiffon Cake

Hybrid: uses oil (not solid fat) for moisture, plus beaten egg whites for lift. Oil doesn't solidify when cold, keeping the cake moist even from the fridge. Lighter than pound cake, richer than angel food. Invented in 1927, popularized by Betty Crocker.

Oil-basedBeaten whitesMoist

Red Velvet

Classic Southern cake with cocoa, buttermilk, and acidic vinegar. Originally the red color came from anthocyanins in natural cocoa reacting with buttermilk acid. Modern recipes add red food coloring. Cream cheese frosting is canonical. Subtle chocolate flavor with a tender crumb.

ButtermilkVinegarCream cheese frosting

Carrot Cake

Oil-based batter keeps this cake moist for days. Grated carrots add moisture, sweetness, and texture. Spices: cinnamon, nutmeg, ginger. Walnuts or pecans for crunch. Cream cheese frosting is the classic partner — its tang cuts the sweetness. Can include pineapple for additional moisture.

Oil-basedGrated carrotSpiced

Cookies

Chocolate Chip

Ruth Wakefield's 1938 invention. Brown butter amplifies Maillard flavors. More brown sugar = chewier (hygroscopic molasses). More white sugar = crispier, more spreading. Bread flour (higher protein) adds chew. Chilling the dough 24–72h concentrates flavor. The Maillard reaction creates complexity at 280°F+.

Brown sugar = chewyBrown butterChill dough

Shortbread

Just three ingredients: butter, sugar, flour. High butter ratio (1:2 fat to flour) creates a sandy, crumbly texture — the definition of "short." No egg, no leavening. Powdered sugar creates a finer texture than granulated. Scottish tradition calls for rice flour addition for extra crunch. Baked low and slow.

3 ingredientsHigh butterSandy texture

Snickerdoodle

Rolled in cinnamon-sugar before baking. Cream of tartar is the defining ingredient — it creates a distinctive tang and reacts with baking soda for lift. The result: crinkled tops, soft centers, slightly crisp edges. Some recipes use baking powder instead, losing the authentic tang.

Cream of tartarCinnamon-sugarTangy

French Macarons

Two almond meringue shells sandwiching ganache or buttercream. Success requires aged egg whites, precise mixing (macaronage — folding to lava consistency), proper drying time (30–60 min to form skin), and careful oven temperature. The "feet" (frilly bases) indicate correct technique. Humidity is the enemy.

Aged whitesMacaronageFeet formation

Biscotti

Italian twice-baked cookies — "bis" (twice) + "cotti" (cooked). First bake as a log; slice diagonally; second bake to dry out completely. Very low fat (some recipes use no butter). Dense, crunchy texture designed for dunking in espresso, vin santo, or coffee. Almonds are traditional.

Twice-bakedLow fatDunkable

Gingersnap

The snap comes from high sugar content that caramelizes and crisps as the cookie cools. Molasses provides depth and chew in softer versions. Triple ginger (ground, fresh, crystallized) maximizes flavor. Rolled in sugar before baking creates a crackled top as the cookie spreads.

MolassesTriple gingerCrackled top

The Science of Baking

Gluten Development

Glutenin and gliadin proteins in flour absorb water and align into gluten strands. Kneading cross-links strands into a network that traps CO₂ bubbles. Protein content determines strength: bread flour 12–14%, AP flour 10–12%, cake flour 7–9%. Salt strengthens gluten; fat inhibits it.

Yeast Leavening

Saccharomyces cerevisiae ferments sugars into CO₂ and ethanol. Active in 75–95°F range; killed above 140°F. Time and temperature affect flavor — longer, cooler fermentation produces more organic acids (lactic, acetic) for complexity. Wild yeast (sourdough) also includes lactobacillus bacteria for tang.

Baking Soda vs Baking Powder

Baking soda (pure NaHCO₃) needs an acid to react (buttermilk, vinegar, brown sugar, cocoa). Baking powder contains its own acid (cream of tartar or sodium aluminum sulfate) plus starch. Double-acting powder releases CO₂ twice: when wet and again when heated. Too much = metallic taste.

Maillard Reaction

At 280–330°F, amino acids and reducing sugars react to create hundreds of flavor compounds — the source of browning on bread crusts, cookie edges, and cake tops. Distinct from caramelization. Requires low moisture at the surface; steam inhibits it (which is why you remove the lid from Dutch oven late in bread baking).

Caramelization

Pure thermal degradation of sugar above 320°F (fructose caramelizes lower, sucrose higher). Creates 100+ flavor compounds including diacetyl (buttery), furan derivatives (nutty), and maltol (sweet). No protein needed — distinct from Maillard. Essential for tarte tatin and crème brûlée crust.

Role of Fat

Fat shortens gluten strands (hence "shortening"), coats starch granules, and carries fat-soluble flavor compounds. Butter (80% fat, 18% water) contributes flavor; the water steams and aids lift. Solid fats (butter, lard) cream with sugar to trap air. Liquid fats (oil) coat more evenly for tender, moist crumb.

Role of Eggs

Whole eggs provide structure (protein coagulation), richness (fat from yolk), leavening (foam), emulsification (lecithin in yolk binds fat and water), and color (carotenoids in yolk). Egg whites alone add structure and lift. Yolks alone add richness and color. Temperature matters: room-temperature eggs emulsify better.

Techniques

Kneading

Develops gluten by aligning and cross-linking protein strands. The push-fold-turn motion stretches the dough against the work surface. Properly kneaded dough passes the windowpane test: stretch a small piece thin enough to see light through without tearing. 8–12 minutes by hand, 5–8 in a stand mixer.

Stretch and Fold

Alternative to kneading for high-hydration doughs. Every 30 minutes during bulk fermentation, grab one side of dough, stretch upward, fold over to the opposite side. Rotate 90° and repeat 4 times. Builds gluten network without deflating gas. 4–6 sets replaces traditional kneading for ciabatta and sourdough.

Autolyse

Rest flour and water (no salt, no yeast) for 20–60 minutes before mixing. During rest, water fully hydrates flour and enzymes begin breaking down starch. This pre-hydration reduces required kneading time, improves extensibility, and creates a more open crumb. A French technique popularized by Raymond Calvel.

Proofing

Final rise after shaping. Properly proofed dough bounces back slowly when poked — springs back quickly means underproofed; doesn't spring back means overproofed. Cold proofing (12–18h in fridge) slows fermentation for more flavor development and makes scoring easier. A linen-lined banneton basket helps hold shape.

Scoring

Cuts made in the dough surface just before baking control where the loaf expands (oven spring). Use a sharp lame (curved razor blade) or serrated knife. Angle the blade 30–45° for an "ear." Patterns range from simple single slash to elaborate wheat stalks and leaves. Cuts must be decisive — hesitation drags dough.

Blind Baking

Pre-baking a tart or pie shell before adding filling. Line with parchment, fill with pie weights or dried beans to prevent puffing. Par-bake: 15 minutes with weights, 5–7 without for a partially baked shell (custard fillings). Full blind bake: 20–25 min total for crisp shell (no-bake fillings like lemon curd).

Tempering Chocolate

Cocoa butter crystallizes in 6 forms; Form V (beta crystals) gives glossy, snapping chocolate. Tempering: melt to 115°F (dark) to destroy all crystals, cool to 82°F on marble to seed Form V, rewarm to 88–90°F working temperature. Tabling, tabling with seed, and sous vide methods all achieve this.

Creaming Method

Beat butter and sugar together 3–5 minutes until pale and fluffy before adding eggs or flour. The mechanical action forces air into the fat matrix, creating tiny bubbles that expand in the oven. Room-temperature butter (65–68°F) is essential — cold butter won't aerate, warm butter collapses. Foundation of most butter cakes.
