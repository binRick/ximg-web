The Roman Legion — Military Structure
Legion (~5,400 men)
🦅 Legio — The Full Legion
10 cohorts of heavy infantry, 120 cavalry (equites legionis), and attached auxiliaries. Commanded by a Legate (legatus legionis), typically a senator. The eagle standard (aquila) was the legion's sacred symbol — losing it was catastrophic.
Cohort (~480 men)
⚔ Cohors — The Cohort
10 cohorts per legion. The First Cohort was double-strength (~800 men) and held the best veterans and the eagle. Commanded by a Tribune (tribuni militum) or Senior Centurion. 6 centuries per cohort.
Century (~80 men)
🛡 Centuria — The Century
Despite the name, a century held 80 soldiers (later). Commanded by a Centurion — the backbone of the Roman army. The most feared was the Primus Pilus, First Centurion of the First Cohort. Centurions carried a vine staff (vitis) as symbol of authority.
Contubernium (8 men)
🏕 Contubernium — The Tent Group
The smallest Roman unit — 8 soldiers who shared a tent, a mule, and a cooking pot. They fought together, marched together, and were often from the same town. This unit cohesion was a key factor in Roman battlefield effectiveness.
Soldier's Kit
🪖 Standard Legionary Equipment
Lorica segmentata (segmented armor), galea (helmet), scutum (large curved rectangular shield), gladius (short stabbing sword, ~50–60 cm), pilum (heavy javelin, ~2m). A full marching pack weighed ~40 kg and included entrenching tools, rations, and stakes for constructing camp walls each night.
Famous Legions
🌟 Notable Legions
Legio X Equestris — Caesar's favorite, crossed the Rubicon. Legio III Gallica — worshipped the sun god Sol. Legio XXII Deiotariana — destroyed in the Bar Kokhba revolt. Legio VI Ferrata — "Ironclad," served from Caesar to the 4th century. Legio IX Hispana — vanished from records after ~120 AD, inspiring legends of its disappearance.
