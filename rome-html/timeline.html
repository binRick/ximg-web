Timeline of Rome
753 BC
Founding of Rome
Traditional founding date by Romulus, who kills his twin Remus and names the city after himself. Rome begins as a small Latin settlement on the Seven Hills along the Tiber River. The early Romans mix with Sabines and Etruscans.
509 BC
Roman Republic Founded
The last king, Tarquinius Superbus ("the Proud"), is overthrown. Romans establish the Republic: two Consuls elected annually, a Senate, and popular assemblies. The motto: "No king shall rule Rome."
264–146 BC
Punic Wars — Rome vs. Carthage
Three wars against Carthage (modern Tunisia). Hannibal invades Italy in the Second Punic War, wins at Cannae, but never takes Rome. Scipio Africanus wins at Zama (202 BC). Carthage is finally razed in 146 BC. "Carthago delenda est."
73–71 BC
Spartacus Slave Revolt
The gladiator Spartacus leads 70,000–120,000 slaves in revolt across Italy. Rome mobilizes eight legions. Crassus defeats Spartacus; 6,000 slaves are crucified along the Appian Way from Capua to Rome as a warning.
49–44 BC
Caesar's Dictatorship
Caesar crosses the Rubicon ("the die is cast"), defeats Pompey, and is named Dictator Perpetuo. Reforms the calendar (Julian calendar), expands the Senate, and plans a campaign against Parthia. Assassinated by 23 senators on 15 March 44 BC.
27 BC
Roman Empire Begins — Augustus
Octavian receives the title "Augustus" from the Senate. The Republic gives way to the Principate — an empire in all but name. Begins a 200-year Pax Romana (Roman Peace) of relative stability and prosperity across the Mediterranean world.
64 AD
Great Fire of Rome
A fire burns for six days, destroying 10 of Rome's 14 districts. Nero reportedly played the lyre while Rome burned (likely myth). He blames Christians, launching the first official persecution. Uses the cleared land to build his enormous Domus Aurea (Golden House).
79 AD
Eruption of Vesuvius
Mount Vesuvius erupts on 24 August, burying Pompeii, Herculaneum, and Stabiae under 4–6 meters of ash and pumice. An estimated 16,000 people die. Pliny the Younger witnesses the eruption and writes the first eyewitness account of a volcanic eruption.
312 AD
Constantine's Vision — Milvian Bridge
Constantine sees a Christian symbol in the sky ("In this sign, conquer") before the Battle of Milvian Bridge. He defeats Maxentius and becomes sole western emperor. Issues the Edict of Milan (313 AD), granting religious tolerance. Christianity begins its rise to dominance.
380 AD
Christianity Becomes State Religion
Emperor Theodosius I issues the Edict of Thessalonica, making Nicene Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire. Other religions are suppressed. The ancient temples are closed and Roman paganism effectively ends after 1,200 years.
410 AD
Sack of Rome — Visigoths
Alaric's Visigoths sack Rome for three days — the first time in 800 years since the Gauls in 390 BC. The psychological shock is enormous. Augustine writes "The City of God" in response. The Western Empire begins its final collapse.
476 AD
Fall of the Western Roman Empire
The Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposes the last Roman emperor, Romulus Augustulus. He sends the imperial insignia to Constantinople rather than proclaiming a new emperor. The Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire continues for another 977 years until 1453 AD.
