Cocktails

Classic cocktails, modern twists, and the craft of mixing

Classic Cocktails

Old Fashioned Whiskey

2oz bourbon or rye, 1 sugar cube, 2 dashes Angostura bitters, orange peel. Muddle sugar with bitters and a splash of water, add spirit over ice, stir, express orange peel over glass.

Glass: Rocks — One of the oldest cocktail forms, documented from 1806. The "original cocktail."

Manhattan Whiskey

2oz rye or bourbon, 1oz sweet vermouth, 2 dashes Angostura bitters. Stir over ice until well chilled (30+ rotations), strain into chilled coupe, garnish with cherry.

Glass: Coupe — Originated ~1880s NYC. Stirred, never shaken — the spirit is the star.

Negroni Gin

1oz gin, 1oz Campari, 1oz sweet vermouth. Equal parts. Stir over ice, strain into rocks glass over large ice, orange peel garnish. Perfect balance of bitter, sweet, herbal.

Glass: Rocks — Count Negroni, Florence 1919, asked for an Americano strengthened with gin.

Martini Gin

2.5oz London Dry gin, 0.5oz dry vermouth. Stir over ice, strain into chilled martini glass, garnish with olive or lemon twist. Ratio varies from "5:1 wet" to the glacial "just wave the vermouth."

Glass: Martini/Coupe — Evolved from the Martinez in the 1880s. Shaken (Bond) is incorrect — it bruises gin.

Daiquiri Rum

2oz white rum, 0.75oz fresh lime juice, 0.75oz simple syrup. Shake hard with ice, double strain into chilled coupe. Fresh lime is non-negotiable — never bottled.

Glass: Coupe — Named after a Cuban beach town. Ernest Hemingway made the "Papa Doble" (no sugar, double rum) famous at El Floridita.

Mojito Rum

2oz white rum, 0.75oz lime juice, 0.75oz simple syrup, 8–10 mint leaves, soda water. Gently muddle mint (bruise, don't shred), add spirit, juice, syrup, shake lightly, pour over crushed ice, top soda.

Glass: Collins — Cuban in origin, popularized at La Bodeguita del Medio, Havana.

Margarita Tequila

2oz blanco tequila, 0.75oz Cointreau or triple sec, 0.75oz fresh lime juice. Shake with ice, strain into salt-rimmed glass. The salt rim cuts the tart and enhances sweetness — never skip it.

Glass: Rocks or coupe — Multiple origin claims from late 1930s–1940s Mexico and border Texas.

Whiskey Sour Whiskey

2oz bourbon, 0.75oz lemon juice, 0.75oz simple syrup, optional egg white for foam. Dry shake (no ice) if using egg white, then shake with ice. Strain into rocks glass.

Glass: Rocks — The "sour" template underpins dozens of cocktails. The egg white version is a "Boston Sour."

Cosmopolitan Vodka

1.5oz citrus vodka, 0.75oz Cointreau, 0.5oz fresh lime juice, 0.5oz cranberry juice. Shake hard, strain into chilled martini glass, flamed orange peel garnish.

Glass: Martini — Crafted by Toby Cecchini in NYC ca. 1988. Sex and the City made it iconic.

Tom Collins Gin

2oz London Dry gin, 1oz lemon juice, 0.75oz simple syrup, 2oz soda water. Shake gin, lemon, syrup; pour over ice in tall glass, top with soda, lemon wheel garnish.

Glass: Collins — Named for an 1870s hoax ("Tom Collins is talking about you in the bar next door").

Gimlet Gin

2oz gin, 0.75oz fresh lime juice, 0.75oz simple syrup. Shake vigorously with ice, strain into chilled coupe. Traditionalists use Rose's Lime Cordial (1:1 gin to cordial); fresh lime is superior.

Glass: Coupe — A Royal Navy staple; lime prevented scurvy on long voyages.

Sidecar Cognac

1.5oz cognac, 0.75oz Cointreau, 0.75oz lemon juice. Shake with ice, strain into sugar-rimmed coupe. The sugar rim should be half only — not all the way around — giving the drinker the choice.

Glass: Coupe — Post-WWI Paris, possibly Ritz Hotel. Named for motorcycle sidecar transport.

Sazerac Rye

2oz rye whiskey, 0.25oz absinthe (rinse), 1 sugar cube, 2 dashes Peychaud's bitters. Rinse chilled glass with absinthe, discard excess. Stir rye, bitters, muddled sugar over ice; strain into glass, lemon peel expressed over top.

Glass: Rocks (no ice) — Official cocktail of New Orleans. Peychaud's bitters are essential.

Last Word Gin

Equal parts: 0.75oz gin, 0.75oz green Chartreuse, 0.75oz maraschino liqueur, 0.75oz fresh lime juice. Shake with ice, double strain into coupe. The perfect equal-parts cocktail — herbal, bright, complex.

Glass: Coupe — Prohibition-era Detroit Athletic Club. Forgotten for decades, revived by Murray Stenson in Seattle ~2004.

French 75 Gin

1.5oz London Dry gin, 0.75oz fresh lemon juice, 0.5oz simple syrup, 2oz chilled champagne. Shake gin, lemon, syrup with ice; strain into flute, top with champagne, lemon twist garnish.

Glass: Champagne flute — Named after the WWI French 75mm artillery gun for its "kick." Can substitute cognac for gin.

Modern Classics (1970s–2000s)

Aperol Spritz Wine/Bitter

3oz Prosecco, 2oz Aperol, 1oz soda water. Build over ice in large wine glass, orange slice. The 3-2-1 formula is the official Aperol spec. Refreshing, bittersweet, low-ABV.

Venice origins, 1950s Italy, became a global phenomenon in the 2010s through brilliant marketing.

Dark & Stormy Rum

2oz Gosling's Black Seal rum, 4oz ginger beer, lime wedge. Build over ice in a highball. The dark rum floated on top creates the dramatic storm-cloud visual. Gosling's owns the trademark on the name.

Bermuda origin, early 20th century. The registered trademark cocktail — officially Gosling's or it's just a Dark & Rainy.

Espresso Martini Vodka

2oz vodka, 1oz Kahlúa, 1oz freshly brewed espresso (cooled slightly). Shake extremely hard with ice — the vigorous shake creates the signature foam. Double strain into chilled martini glass, 3 coffee beans garnish.

Dick Bradsell, London 1983. A customer asked for "something to wake me up and f*** me up."

Moscow Mule Vodka

2oz vodka, 0.5oz fresh lime juice, 4oz ginger beer. Build over ice in a copper mug, lime wedge. The copper mug isn't just aesthetic — it conducts cold and intensifies the experience.

1940s Los Angeles, a collaboration between a vodka importer and a ginger beer manufacturer to move product.

Piña Colada Rum

2oz white or gold rum, 1.5oz coconut cream, 3oz pineapple juice. Blend with cup of ice until smooth, or shake if you prefer "shaken style." Served in a hurricane or tall glass with a pineapple garnish and cherry.

Official drink of Puerto Rico. Created at the Caribe Hilton, San Juan, 1954. "Do you like piña coladas?"

Penicillin Scotch

2oz blended Scotch, 0.75oz fresh lemon juice, 0.75oz honey-ginger syrup, 0.25oz Islay Scotch (float). Shake blended Scotch, lemon, ginger-honey hard with ice; double strain, float peaty Islay Scotch on top.

Sam Ross, Milk & Honey NYC, ca. 2005. Instantly a modern classic — smoky, sweet, medicinal, perfect.

Paper Plane Whiskey

Equal parts: 0.75oz bourbon, 0.75oz Aperol, 0.75oz Amaro Nonino, 0.75oz fresh lemon juice. Shake with ice, double strain into chilled coupe. Another perfect equal-parts cocktail — bitter, sweet, boozy.

Sam Ross again, ca. 2008. Named after the M.I.A. song. Nonino is essential — other amari change the character entirely.

Naked & Famous Mezcal

Equal parts: 0.75oz mezcal, 0.75oz Aperol, 0.75oz yellow Chartreuse, 0.75oz fresh lime juice. Shake with ice, double strain. The smoky mezcal version of the Paper Plane template. Complex, herbal, smoky.

Joaquín Simó, Death & Co NYC, 2011. Proves the equal-parts formula is infinitely flexible.

Harvey Wallbanger Vodka

1.5oz vodka, 4oz fresh orange juice, 0.5oz Galliano (float). Build vodka and OJ over ice in a highball, float Galliano on top. The anise-vanilla liqueur float distinguishes it from a Screwdriver.

Donato "Duke" Antone, Hollywood 1952. Named after a legendary surfer who kept walking into walls after drinking them.

Lemon Drop Vodka

2oz citrus vodka, 0.75oz Cointreau, 1oz fresh lemon juice, 0.5oz simple syrup. Shake hard with ice, strain into sugar-rimmed martini glass. Bright, tart, refreshing — the most civilized of the "candy" cocktails.

Norman Jay Hobday, Henry Africa's bar, San Francisco, 1970s. Part of the first wave of modern citrus cocktails.

Sex on the Beach Vodka

1.5oz vodka, 0.5oz peach schnapps, 2oz cranberry juice, 2oz pineapple juice. Build over ice in a highball, orange slice. The layers of fruit juice make it accessible and visually appealing. Sweeter than most classics.

Florida bar contest, 1987. Ted Pizio of Confetti's bar in Orlando. Won by having the most scandalous name.

Cocktails by Spirit

🥃 Whiskey

Sazerac

New Orleans' own — rye, Peychaud's bitters, absinthe rinse, lemon peel. The oldest American cocktail. Never add ice to the finished drink.

Rob Roy

The Manhattan's Scottish cousin: 2oz Scotch, 1oz sweet vermouth, 2 dashes Angostura. Named after the Scottish folk hero in 1894 at the Waldorf Astoria.

Boulevardier

1.5oz bourbon, 1oz Campari, 1oz sweet vermouth. The whiskey Negroni. Stir over ice, strain into rocks glass. Harry McElhone, Harry's New York Bar, Paris 1927.

🌿 Gin

Negroni

Equal parts gin, Campari, sweet vermouth. The archetype of a balanced bitter-sweet cocktail. Use Plymouth or a juniper-forward London Dry for best results.

Gimlet

2oz gin, 0.75oz lime juice, 0.75oz simple syrup. Raymond Chandler gave this cocktail literary immortality in "The Long Goodbye": "a real gimlet is half gin and half Rose's Lime Juice."

Bee's Knees

2oz gin, 0.75oz fresh lemon juice, 0.75oz honey syrup (2:1 honey to water). Shake with ice, strain into coupe. Prohibition-era — honey masked cheap gin. Elegant and floral with quality gin.

🍹 Rum

El Presidente

1.5oz white rum, 0.75oz dry vermouth, 0.25oz orange curaçao, 0.25oz grenadine. Stir over ice, strain. Cuba in the 1920s, named for President Gerardo Machado. Sophisticated, spirit-forward.

Jungle Bird

1.5oz dark Jamaican rum, 0.75oz Campari, 1.5oz pineapple juice, 0.5oz lime juice, 0.5oz simple syrup. Shake, strain into rocks glass. 1970s Kuala Lumpur. Bittersweet tropical perfection.

Daiquiri

The Ur-rum cocktail: 2oz white rum, 0.75oz lime, 0.75oz simple syrup. Hemingway's favorite. The quality of the rum shines through — use a good agricole or Havana Club-style white.

🌵 Tequila & Mezcal

Paloma

2oz blanco tequila, 0.5oz lime juice, pinch salt, 4oz fresh grapefruit juice or Squirt. Build over ice in a highball with a salted rim. More popular in Mexico than the Margarita. Incredibly refreshing.

Tommy's Margarita

2oz blanco tequila, 1oz fresh lime juice, 0.5oz agave nectar. Shake, strain. Julio Bermejo, Tommy's Mexican Restaurant SF, 1990s. Replaced Cointreau with agave to honor the base spirit.

❄️ Vodka

Moscow Mule

Vodka, ginger beer, lime. The copper mug is central to the experience — the metal conducts cold intensely and the slightly metallic taste enhances the ginger. Always use fresh-squeezed lime.

White Russian

2oz vodka, 1oz Kahlúa, 1oz heavy cream (float). Build over ice in rocks glass, float cream on top (don't stir). The Dude abides. The Black Russian omits the cream.

🍇 Brandy & Cognac

Sidecar

1.5oz VSOP cognac, 0.75oz Cointreau, 0.75oz lemon juice. The definitive cognac cocktail. Use quality cognac — Rémy Martin 1738 or Hennessy VSOP. The sugar rim is optional but traditional.

Stinger

2oz brandy, 0.75oz white crème de menthe. Stir over ice, strain into rocks glass. Classic digestif cocktail from the early 1900s. The cooling mint tempers the warming brandy — an unlikely but elegant combination.

Vieux Carré

0.75oz rye, 0.75oz cognac, 0.75oz sweet vermouth, 1 tsp Bénédictine, Peychaud's and Angostura bitters. Stir, serve over ice. Created at the Carousel Bar at Hotel Monteleone, New Orleans, 1930s.

Cocktail Techniques

Shaking

Used for cocktails containing citrus juice, egg white, cream, or dairy. Shaking chills, dilutes, and aerates simultaneously — introducing micro-bubbles that change texture. Standard shake: 10–15 seconds hard. A vigorous shake dilutes ~15–20% and drops temperature to ~0°C. Use a Boston shaker (metal-on-metal) for best seal. Never shake spirit-only drinks — you'll over-dilute and make them "bruised."

Stirring

Used for spirit-forward cocktails: Martinis, Manhattans, Negronis, Old Fashioneds. Stirring chills and dilutes without aeration — preserving clarity, texture, and the spirit's character. Use a bar spoon in a mixing glass filled with ice. Stir 30–40 rotations (about 45 seconds) for proper dilution. The goal is crystal-clear, silky, properly cold.

Muddling

Pressing fruits, herbs, or sugar cubes in the bottom of a shaker or glass to release juice and essential oils. The key: gentle pressure for herbs (you want oils, not chlorophyll — grinding releases bitter compounds). Fruit can take more force. Use a flat-bottomed muddler, not a toothed one for delicate herbs. Mint in a Mojito should be lightly pressed — not obliterated.

Straining — Hawthorne vs Julep vs Fine

Hawthorne strainer: The spring-coil strainer used on shaker tins. Catches ice and large solids. Julep strainer: A perforated cup-shaped strainer used in a mixing glass (stirred drinks). Fine straining (double straining): Using a Hawthorne + a fine-mesh tea strainer together to remove ice shards and fruit pulp for a crystal-clear, smooth cocktail. Used for any shaken drink served up.

Building in Glass

Some cocktails are assembled directly in the serving glass — Negroni (stirred in rocks glass), Moscow Mule, Old Fashioned. Building preserves carbonation in highballs (ginger beer, soda water). Always add spirits before carbonated mixers. Stir gently to combine, not vigorously — you don't want to lose bubbles.

Floating

Pouring a liquid over the back of a bar spoon so it rests on top of a denser drink without mixing — creating visual layers. Density determines what floats: cream (lighter) floats on spirits; Islay Scotch can float on a lighter blended Scotch. Used in: Harvey Wallbanger (Galliano float), Penicillin (Islay float), White Russian (cream float). Pour very slowly down the back of the spoon.

Flaming (Expressing Citrus Peel)

A flamed orange or lemon peel releases and caramelizes the essential oils over the surface of a drink. Hold a coin-sized disk of peel skin-side toward a lit match, squeeze the peel sharply — the oils spray through the flame and atomize onto the cocktail. Used in Sazerac, Cosmopolitan, and many stirred cocktails. The smoke from a lit peel adds aromatic complexity.

Fat-Washing

Infusing a spirit with fat (bacon grease, sesame oil, peanut butter, coconut oil) to extract fat-soluble flavor compounds, then freezing the mixture so the fat solidifies and can be filtered out. The result: a spirit with savory, rich, nutty, or umami notes — without greasiness. Don Don Julio's bacon bourbon is the classic example. Requires 4–6 hours and a 24-hour freeze.

Infusing Spirits

Soaking fruits, herbs, spices, or other aromatics in a spirit to extract flavor. Cold infusion (room temperature, 24 hours to 2 weeks): vanilla vodka, cucumber gin, chili tequila. Sous vide rapid infusion (1–2 hours at 140°F): dramatically accelerates extraction. The key is tasting frequently — over-infusion can turn bitter, especially with spices and citrus pith.

Glassware — Why Shape Matters

Glass shape affects temperature retention, aroma concentration, carbonation preservation, and visual presentation. Always chill glasses before serving cold cocktails.

🥂

Coupe

Shallow, broad bowl on a long stem. Keeps drinks cold (stem prevents hand-warming), concentrates aroma. Used for: Daiquiri, Sidecar, Last Word, French 75.

🍸

Martini

Conical V-shape on long stem. Wide rim concentrates aromas, the angle looks elegant. Used for: Martini, Cosmopolitan, Lemon Drop. The coupe is generally preferred by professionals.

🥃

Rocks / Old Fashioned

Short, wide, heavy-bottomed tumbler. Accommodates large ice cube or sphere. Used for: Old Fashioned, Negroni, Sazerac, Boulevardier. The "double rocks" is slightly larger.

🥤

Highball

Tall, straight-sided glass (8–12 oz). Perfect for long drinks with carbonation. Used for: Moscow Mule (if not copper), Tom Collins, Paloma, Dark & Stormy.

🧋

Collins

Taller and narrower than a highball (10–14 oz). The extra height keeps carbonation longer. Used for: Tom Collins, Mojito (tall version), Singapore Sling.

☕

Copper Mug

The Moscow Mule's dedicated vessel. Copper conducts cold so efficiently that ice stays frozen longer and the drink stays colder than in glass. The slight metallic edge enhances ginger.

🥂

Champagne Flute

Tall, narrow bowl preserves carbonation longer than a coupe (smaller surface area). Used for: French 75, Bellini, Kir Royale, Aperol Spritz (though large wine glass is preferred for the Spritz).

🍷

Snifter

Wide bowl, narrow top — traps and concentrates aromas. Primarily for brandy, Cognac, aged rum, and whiskey served neat. The cupped hand warms the spirit gently from below.

🍹

Hurricane

Tall, curvy, tulip-shaped glass (20 oz+). Associated with New Orleans and tropical drinks. Used for: Piña Colada, Hurricane cocktail, Tiki drinks. The large volume accommodates blended or frozen drinks.

🍸

Nick & Nora

A smaller, more rounded coupe variant — holds about 4–5 oz. Elegant, understated, excellent for lower-ABV stirred drinks or small-format cocktails. Currently very fashionable in craft cocktail bars.

Non-Alcoholic Cocktails

Virgin Mojito

8–10 mint leaves, 0.75oz lime juice, 0.75oz simple syrup, 4oz sparkling water. Gently muddle mint with syrup and lime, top with sparkling water over crushed ice. Just as refreshing as the original — the mint and lime do the work.

Shirley Temple

4oz ginger ale, 4oz lemon-lime soda (Sprite/7-Up), 0.5oz grenadine, maraschino cherry. Build over ice in a highball. The sweet nostalgia cocktail named after the child actress, created at the Royal Hawaiian Hotel in the 1930s.

Arnold Palmer

Equal parts unsweetened iced tea and fresh lemonade. Build over ice. Named after golfer Arnold Palmer who habitually ordered this combination. Add ginger beer and mint for a more complex version. Simple but deeply satisfying.

Virgin Mary

4oz tomato juice, 0.5oz lemon juice, 2 dashes Worcestershire, 1 dash hot sauce, horseradish, celery salt, black pepper. Shake gently, pour over ice in a highball, celery stalk garnish. The Bloody Mary minus the vodka — just as complex and savory.

Seedlip-Style Botanical

Seedlip (or homemade distilled botanicals: juniper, cardamom, bay leaf, citrus peel), 0.5oz lemon juice, 0.5oz honey syrup, soda water. Shake Seedlip with lemon and honey, strain into ice-filled rocks glass, top with soda. Premium no-ABV spirit-forward approach.

Sparkling Cucumber Cooler

4–5 cucumber slices, 0.75oz elderflower cordial, 0.75oz fresh lime juice, 4oz sparkling water. Muddle cucumber, add cordial and lime, shake lightly, double strain over ice, top with sparkling water. Floral, clean, sophisticated.

Spicy Ginger Mule Mocktail

0.5oz fresh ginger juice (or ginger syrup), 0.75oz lime juice, 6oz premium ginger beer, lime wedge. Combine in a copper mug over ice. The ginger-forward flavor makes this one of the most satisfying zero-proof options. The copper mug keeps it cold and enhances the ginger bite.

Hibiscus Spritz

1.5oz hibiscus tea (brewed concentrated, cooled), 0.5oz lime juice, 0.5oz simple syrup, 3oz soda or tonic water. Build over ice in a wine glass, lime wheel. The deep crimson color is visually stunning, and hibiscus brings a natural tartness that mimics Campari without alcohol.
