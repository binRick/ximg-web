Doom — A History
1990
id Software Foundedcompany
John Carmack, John Romero, Adrian Carmack, and Tom Hall leave Softdisk to start id Software in Shreveport, Louisiana. Wolfenstein 3D ships in 1992 and proves the FPS market — but it's just the warmup act.
1993
Doom Released via Sharewarerelease
Doom 1.0 drops on December 10, 1993 — uploaded to an FTP server at the University of Wisconsin. The shareware model distributes Episode 1 free; the full game costs $40 mail-order. FTP servers crash under demand. An estimated 10 million people download it in two months. Nothing in gaming has ever spread this fast.
1994
Deathmatch & Network Playmilestone
Doom's IPX network multiplayer and deathmatch mode ignite the concept of competitive online gaming. Office networks grind to a halt as employees play at work. Corporate IT departments send memos banning "Doom parties." The term "deathmatch" enters gaming vocabulary permanently.
1994
Doom II: Hell on Earthrelease
Doom II ships as a retail-only release — no shareware — and sells 2 million copies in its first year. It introduces the Super Shotgun, the Icon of Sin final boss, and larger open maps. The commercial release model signals the FPS genre is mainstream.
1994
U.S. Congress Hearings — Video Game Violenceculture
Doom and Mortal Kombat are cited by name in U.S. Senate hearings on video game violence, led by Senators Lieberman and Kohl. The hearings directly lead to the creation of the ESRB ratings system in 1994. Doom becomes a political symbol for a decade.
1997
Source Code Releasedmilestone
id Software releases the Doom source code under a non-commercial license (later GPL in 1999). Within days, ports appear on Linux, BeOS, and dozens of other platforms. The source release spawns the modern source port ecosystem — ZDOOM, PrBoom, GZDoom — and keeps Doom alive for decades.
1999
Columbine & "Doom Clones"culture
Following the Columbine High School shooting, Doom is linked in media reports to the perpetrators, who were known players. President Clinton orders a federal study on media violence. The phrase "Doom clone" — already used dismissively for all FPS games — takes on a darker cultural meaning.
2004
Doom 3 — The Horror Rebootrelease
Doom 3 launches as a survival horror reboot with cutting-edge lighting, oppressive darkness, and scripted scares. It sells 3.5 million copies but divides fans — the slow pace and flashlight-or-gun mechanic feel un-Doom. The stencil shadow rendering engine (id Tech 4) is widely licensed and influential.
2011
Bethesda Acquires id Softwarecompany
ZeniMax Media (Bethesda's parent) acquires id Software. John Carmack remains as CTO — until 2013, when he departs to join Oculus VR. The loss of Carmack marks the end of an era. Bethesda takes over publishing Doom going forward.
2016
Doom (2016) — Rip and Tear Returnsrelease
After a troubled development that scrapped years of work, the 2016 Doom reboot ships to universal acclaim. id Software rediscovers the series' soul: relentless forward movement, glory kills, and Mick Gordon's thunderous heavy metal score. "Push forward combat" becomes a design philosophy studied across the industry.
2020
Doom Eternalrelease
Doom Eternal raises the mechanical complexity to its peak — resource management, platforming, and a weapon wheel that demands mastery. It sells over 3 million copies at launch. Mick Gordon's soundtrack dispute with id becomes a public controversy. Doom Eternal wins multiple Game of the Year awards.
2023
Doom's 30th Anniversary & "It Runs Doom"legacy
Doom turns 30. The "It Runs Doom" meme has documented the game running on pregnancy tests, ATMs, cash registers, MIDI keyboards, and a John Deere tractor. The original WAD format is still actively used for new community megawads. Doom: The Dark Ages is announced for 2025.
