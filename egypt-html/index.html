Great Pharaohs
Khufu (Cheops)
2589–2566 BC · Old Kingdom
Builder of the Great Pyramid of Giza — the only surviving Wonder of the Ancient World. Unified Egypt through central authority and monumental construction projects.
Builder
Ramesses II (the Great)
1279–1213 BC · New Kingdom
Egypt's longest-reigning pharaoh, renowned for the Battle of Kadesh against the Hittites and prolific temple construction including Abu Simbel. Signed history's first peace treaty.
Warrior-King
Hatshepsut
1478–1458 BC · New Kingdom
One of Egypt's most successful female pharaohs. Expanded trade routes to Punt, built the magnificent Deir el-Bahri mortuary temple, and promoted artistic achievement.
Builder
Tutankhamun
1332–1323 BC · New Kingdom
The "Boy King" whose nearly intact tomb, discovered in 1922 by Howard Carter, revealed unprecedented treasures and sparked global fascination with Egyptian archaeology.
Legacy
Akhenaten
1353–1336 BC · New Kingdom
The Heretic Pharaoh who revolutionized Egyptian religion by introducing monotheistic worship of Aten (the sun disk), relocating the capital to Amarna. His changes were reversed after death.
Reformer
Cleopatra VII
51–30 BC · Ptolemaic Period
Egypt's last active pharaoh, who forged alliances with Julius Caesar and Mark Antony. Fluent in 9 languages, a skilled diplomat and ruler who fought to preserve Egyptian independence.
Diplomat
Wonders & Monuments
🔺
Great Pyramid of Giza
Built for Pharaoh Khufu c. 2560 BC. Originally 146.5m tall. The only surviving Wonder of the Ancient World, built by ~20,000 workers over 20 years.
🗿
Great Sphinx
Limestone statue with lion body and human head, 73m long. Thought to depict Pharaoh Khafre, built c. 2500 BC and still guarding the Giza plateau.
🏛
Temple of Karnak
The largest ancient religious complex ever constructed. Built over 2,000 years by successive pharaohs, covering 100 hectares in ancient Thebes (modern Luxor).
⚱️
Valley of the Kings
Royal necropolis containing 63 known tombs of New Kingdom pharaohs (1539–1075 BC), including Tutankhamun's legendary unplundered burial chamber.
🌊
Abu Simbel Temples
Two massive rock temples carved for Ramesses II, featuring four 20m statues. Relocated in the 1960s to save them from the rising Lake Nasser.
📚
Library of Alexandria
The ancient world's greatest library, founded c. 300 BC. At its peak held 400,000–700,000 scrolls — a repository of all known knowledge.
Timeline of Ancient Egypt
3100 BC
Unification of Egypt
Pharaoh Narmer (or Menes) unites Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing the First Dynasty and beginning 3,000 years of pharaonic rule.
2560 BC
Great Pyramid Completed
Khufu's pyramid at Giza reaches completion after ~20 years of construction — the tallest man-made structure for 3,800 years.
1353 BC
Akhenaten's Religious Revolution
Pharaoh Akhenaten introduces sun-disk worship, moves capital to Amarna. The experiment ends with his death; temples are restored.
1274 BC
Battle of Kadesh
Ramesses II battles Hittite king Muwatalli II in history's first recorded peace treaty — the world's earliest known international agreement.
332 BC
Alexander the Great Conquers Egypt
Alexander is welcomed as a liberator from Persian rule, crowned pharaoh, founds Alexandria. The Ptolemaic Dynasty begins after his death.
196 BC
Rosetta Stone Carved
A decree inscribed in hieroglyphics, Demotic, and Greek — rediscovered in 1799, it unlocked the secrets of Egyptian hieroglyphic writing.
30 BC
End of Ancient Egypt
Cleopatra VII dies; Rome annexes Egypt as a province. Three millennia of pharaonic civilization comes to an end.

The Egyptian Pantheon
Ra
Sun God & King of the Gods
Ra sailed across the sky each day in his solar barque and descended into the underworld each night, battling the serpent Apep before rising again at dawn. Pharaohs were considered sons of Ra, their divine authority drawn directly from the solar deity.
Osiris
Lord of the Dead
Murdered by his brother Set and resurrected by his wife Isis, Osiris became the god of the afterlife and resurrection. The judgment of souls took place in his hall — the heart of the deceased was weighed against the feather of Ma'at (truth).
Isis
Goddess of Magic & Healing
The most powerful goddess in Egypt, Isis mastered all magic after learning the secret name of Ra. She gathered the pieces of Osiris' dismembered body and resurrected him through her sorcery, then conceived Horus to avenge his father's murder.
Anubis
Jackal-headed Guide of the Dead
Anubis guided souls through the Duat (underworld) and presided over the embalming ritual. Priests wore jackal masks during mummification ceremonies. His black color symbolized both death and the fertile black soil of the Nile valley — death and rebirth intertwined.
The Art of Mummification
70 Days
The Mummification Process
The complete mummification ritual took 70 days. First the body was purified, then the organs removed through a small incision and placed in canopic jars. The brain was extracted through the nose with a hook. The body was packed with natron salt for 40 days to desiccate it completely.
Canopic Jars
Protecting the Organs
The Four Sons of Horus each guarded a different organ: Imsety (human head) held the liver; Hapy (baboon) the lungs; Duamutef (jackal) the stomach; Qebehsenuef (falcon) the intestines. The heart alone remained in the body — it would be weighed at the final judgment.
Book of the Dead
The Spell Book of the Afterlife
A collection of 192 spells and instructions to help the deceased navigate the dangers of the Duat. Wealthy Egyptians commissioned personalized copies on papyrus, illustrated with their name inserted into the formulaic texts. The spell "I have not done wrong" lists 42 specific sins to deny.
Shabtis
Servant Figurines
Small figurines buried with the dead to serve as magical servants in the afterlife. A wealthy burial might include 365 shabtis — one for each day of the year — plus 36 overseers to supervise them. They would work the fields of the Field of Reeds so the deceased could rest.
