1300 – 1600 AD · Florence, Rome, Venice & Beyond
The Renaissance Masters
Leonardo da Vinci
1452–1519 · The Ultimate Renaissance Man
Painter, sculptor, architect, musician, mathematician, engineer, anatomist, geologist, cartographer, botanist. The Mona Lisa and The Last Supper are his surviving paintings; his notebooks contain thousands of inventions — flying machines, armored vehicles, solar power — centuries ahead of their time.
Michelangelo
1475–1564 · Divine Sculptor & Painter
Considered the greatest artist of his age — and perhaps any age. The Sistine Chapel ceiling (4 years lying on scaffolding), the Pietà, David, and the design of St. Peter's Basilica. At 72, he became chief architect of St. Peter's.
Raphael
1483–1520 · Master of Harmony
The third of the great Renaissance triumvirate. Famous for his Madonnas and the Vatican frescoes, especially "The School of Athens" — depicting Plato, Aristotle, and every major ancient philosopher in a single, harmonious composition.
Niccolò Machiavelli
1469–1527 · Father of Political Science
Florentine diplomat whose "The Prince" (1532) analyzed political power with brutal realism. "It is better to be feared than loved, if you cannot be both." His secular analysis of politics — separate from religion or morality — founded modern political science.
Galileo Galilei
1564–1642 · Father of Modern Science
Improved the telescope and turned it to the sky. Observed Jupiter's moons, lunar craters, and confirmed Copernicus's heliocentric model. Tried by the Inquisition for heresy; forced to recant. "And yet it moves." Founded observational, experimental science.
Erasmus of Rotterdam
1466–1536 · Prince of the Humanists
Northern Renaissance's greatest scholar. "In Praise of Folly" satirized Church corruption; his Greek New Testament translation exposed errors in the Latin Vulgate. A Catholic who criticized the Church without joining the Reformation — the most influential humanist of his age.
