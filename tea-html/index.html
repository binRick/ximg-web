Tea

From leaf to cup — the world's most consumed beverage across six varieties

The Six Types of Tea

All true tea comes from Camellia sinensis. Processing — how leaves are oxidized, heated, or aged — determines the type.

Green Tea 🟢

Minimal oxidation

Immediately heat-treated after picking (steaming in Japan, pan-firing in China) to stop oxidation. Flavor ranges from grassy and vegetal (Japanese style) to nutty and floral (Chinese style). Japanese teas: sencha, gyokuro (shade-grown, rich umami), matcha (stone-ground powder), hojicha (roasted). Chinese teas: Longjing (Dragon Well, pan-fired, nutty), Biluochun (fruity, floral).

Caffeine: 25–35mg per cup. Brew at 70–80°C (158–176°F).

White Tea ⚪

Barely processed

The most minimally processed tea — young buds and leaves simply dried and withered, with no oxidation step. Extremely delicate: subtle flavors of hay, honeysuckle, and melon. Silver Needle (Baihao Yinzhen) is buds only — the most prized and expensive. White Peony (Bai Mudan) includes leaves and is more accessible. Produced primarily in Fujian, China.

Caffeine: 15–25mg per cup. Brew at 75°C (167°F). Over-steeping makes it bitter.

Yellow Tea 💛

Rare, slight oxidation

Extremely rare — produced only in a few regions of China. Similar to green tea but with an additional "sealing yellow" step: damp tea is wrapped in cloth and allowed to yellow slightly, softening the grassy notes and adding a mellow, slightly sweet character. Often described as green tea's more sophisticated, less sharp cousin. Junshan Yinzhen is the most famous variety.

Caffeine: 20–30mg per cup. Brew at 70–80°C. Difficult to find outside China.

Oolong Tea 🟤

Partial oxidation 15–85%

The widest flavor range of any tea category — oxidation level determines everything. Lightly oxidized oolongs (15–30%): floral, creamy, close to green tea — Baozhong, Alishan high mountain. Medium oxidized (40–60%): complex stone fruit, honey, orchid — Dong Ding, Da Hong Pao (Wuyi rock oolong). Heavily oxidized (70–85%): caramel, toasty, malty — Oriental Beauty (Dong Fang Mei Ren). Taiwan is the premier oolong producer.

Caffeine: 30–50mg per cup. Brew at 85–90°C. Excellent for multiple re-steepings.

Black Tea ⚫

Fully oxidized

Fully oxidized — leaves turn deep brown/black, producing robust, bold, malty flavors. Assam (India): malty, full-bodied, the backbone of English Breakfast. Darjeeling (India): light, muscatel (grape-like) flavor — the "Champagne of teas," especially first flush. Ceylon (Sri Lanka): bright, citrusy, clean. Keemun (China): winey, slightly smoky, rose notes. Lapsang Souchong: intentionally smoke-dried over pinewood. Earl Grey is black tea with bergamot oil.

Caffeine: 40–70mg per cup. Brew at 95–100°C for 3–5 minutes.

Pu-erh Tea 🍂

Aged & fermented

A unique category — aged and microbially fermented. Two types: Sheng (raw) pu-erh ages naturally over years or decades, becoming smoother and more complex. Shou (ripe) pu-erh undergoes accelerated "wet piling" fermentation — dark, earthy, mushroomy, sometimes "fishy" when young. Often compressed into cakes or bricks. Quality aged pu-erh (10–30+ years) commands extraordinary prices. Traditionally from Yunnan, China — the birthplace of tea.

Caffeine: 30–70mg per cup. Brew at 95–100°C. The more water used per gram, the milder.

Tea Origins & Terroir

China 🇨🇳

The origin of all tea — cultivated for 5,000+ years. Key regions: Yunnan (pu-erh, ancient tea trees 800+ years old, large leaf variety), Fujian (white tea, oolong, Wuyi rock teas), Zhejiang (Longjing/Dragon Well, the emperor's green), Anhui (Keemun black, Huangshan Maofeng green), Guangdong (Phoenix Dan Cong oolong). Chinese tea culture is 5,000 years old and encompasses hundreds of named varieties.

Japan 🇯🇵

Tea arrived from China in the 9th century. Japan produces only green teas (no black or oolong). Key regions: Uji (Kyoto, premium matcha and gyokuro — shade-grown for 3–4 weeks to boost L-theanine and chlorophyll), Shizuoka (70% of Japan's tea — sencha, fukamushi deep-steamed), Kyushu (Kagoshima: Yabukita cultivar, Chiran sencha). Gyokuro (shade-grown), Genmaicha (green + roasted rice), Hojicha (roasted, low caffeine).

India 🇮🇳

Darjeeling: "The Champagne of teas." High altitude Himalayan gardens (2,000m). Three flushes: First (March–May): delicate, muscatel, most prized. Second (June–July): fuller muscatel. Autumn (Oct–Nov): robust. Assam: Low altitude, hot and humid, large-leaf CTC (cut-tear-curl) and orthodox black teas. Malty, strong, Irish Breakfast backbone. Nilgiri: South India, fragrant, brisk, used in iced tea. India is the world's second-largest tea producer.

Sri Lanka / Ceylon 🇱🇰

Ceylon tea is prized for brightness, briskness, and clean flavor. Altitude determines character: High-grown (Nuwara Eliya, 1,800m+): delicate, floral, golden liquor. Mid-grown (Kandy, 600–1,200m): medium body, good all-rounder. Low-grown (Ruhuna, Sabaragamuwa): full-bodied, dark, malty. Ceylon was once the world's largest coffee producer until a fungal blight in 1869 forced a switch to tea. James Taylor planted the first commercial tea estate in 1867.

Taiwan 🇹🇼

The undisputed master of oolong. High-mountain (gaoshan) teas from Ali Shan, Li Shan, and Shan Lin Xi at 1,000–2,600m are globally revered for their creamy, floral, complex character. The coolness and cloud cover slow leaf growth, concentrating flavor compounds. Dong Ding (medium oxidized, roasted), Baozhong (lightly twisted, floral, barely oxidized), Oriental Beauty (heavily oxidized, naturally sweet from leafhopper insect bites triggering defensive compounds).

Kenya 🇰🇪

Africa's largest tea producer, third globally. Kenya grows almost exclusively CTC black teas at high altitude (1,500–2,700m) in the Rift Valley — quick, consistent, low-cost. The perfect base for tea bags and blended breakfast teas. Bright red liquor, brisk and clean. Purple tea is a Kenyan innovation — a naturally purple-leaved cultivar containing anthocyanins, marketed for antioxidant properties. Kenya now also produces small amounts of specialty green and white tea.

Turkey 🇹🇷

Turkey is the world's largest per-capita tea consumer — Turks drink on average 3.5kg of dry tea per person per year. Tea (çay, pronounced "chai") is the lifeblood of Turkish culture and business. Grown in Rize on the Black Sea coast — a small, humid, steep coastal region. Turkish black tea is brewed strong in a double stacked teapot (çaydanlık): tea in the upper, boiling water in the lower. Served sweet in tulip-shaped small glasses.

Brewing Guide

Water temperature is the most important variable. Too hot destroys delicate compounds (green tea becomes bitter at 90°C+). Too cool and black tea won't extract properly.

Green
70–80°C / 158–176°F · 1–3 min
White
75°C / 167°F · 2–5 min
Yellow
75–80°C / 167–176°F · 2–3 min
Oolong
85–90°C / 185–194°F · 2–4 min
Black
95–100°C / 203–212°F · 3–5 min
Pu-erh
95–100°C / 203–212°F · 2–4 min (rinse first)

Brewing Methods

Gaiwan (盖碗)

The traditional Chinese lidded bowl (gaiwan = "lid-bowl"). Porcelain or ceramic, no handle. Pour water at correct temp, steep 30–45 seconds for the first infusion, extending each subsequent steep. The lid controls the pour. Quality tea can be steeped 5–10+ times, evolving with each infusion. The gaiwan is the most versatile brewing vessel — suitable for all tea types.

Gongfu Cha (工夫茶)

Chinese "kung fu tea" — the art of careful, attentive brewing. Uses a small clay Yixing teapot (unglazed, absorbs tea oils over time, improving with age), a tea tray to catch overflow, a fairness pitcher, and small cups. High leaf-to-water ratio, short steeps (15–30 seconds). Produces concentrated, complex tea. The ritual is as important as the result.

Japanese Kyusu

A side-handled teapot designed specifically for green tea. The strainer basket prevents leaf fragments from pouring through. Unlike Chinese gaiwans, the kyusu is designed for a single person's enjoyment. Hohin (handless) and shiboridashi (flat, lid-less) are used for high-grade gyokuro.

Cold Brew

Cold water extraction (8–24 hours in the refrigerator) produces a naturally sweet, lower-caffeine, lower-astringency tea. The cold temperature slows extraction of tannins and caffeine while allowing aromatic compounds to dissolve. Excellent for green and white teas. Use 2x the leaf amount, cold filtered water, steep 8–12 hours. The result is silky and incredibly clean.

Water Quality

Water is 99.5% of the finished cup. Ideal water: slightly soft, 50–150ppm TDS (total dissolved solids), neutral pH (7.0), no chlorine or fluoride taste. Over-mineralized water (hard tap water) mutes delicate flavors and causes scum on the surface. Chlorinated water makes green tea taste metallic. Reverse osmosis water (0 ppm) produces flat tea — add a tiny pinch of Himalayan salt to re-mineralize. Spring water from known-clean sources is ideal.

Health & Nutrition

L-Theanine

An amino acid unique to tea (and some mushrooms). L-theanine promotes calm alertness — it increases alpha brain waves associated with relaxed focus. Crucially, it works synergistically with caffeine: the combination produces "calm energy" without the jitteriness of coffee. Highest concentration in shade-grown teas (matcha, gyokuro) where the plant accumulates L-theanine in preparation for photosynthesis. A typical cup of matcha: 25–46mg L-theanine, 70mg caffeine.

Catechins & EGCG

Polyphenol antioxidants are the dominant phytocompounds in tea. Green tea is richest in catechins — especially EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate), the most studied tea compound. EGCG has shown anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer (in vitro), cardiovascular protective, and metabolic benefits in research. Green tea has 100–200mg catechins per cup; black tea converts catechins to theaflavins and thearubigins during oxidation (different antioxidants, equally beneficial).

Caffeine Content

Tea caffeine varies enormously by type, brewing time, and temperature. Rough hierarchy per 8oz cup: Matcha (70–90mg, all leaf consumed) > Black (40–70mg) > Oolong (30–50mg) > Green (25–35mg) > White (15–25mg) > Hojicha (5–10mg, roasting destroys caffeine) > Herbal/Tisane (0mg). Compare: drip coffee = 80–200mg per cup. Tea's caffeine is released more slowly than coffee's, contributing to its smoother energy profile.

Cardiovascular Effects

Large-scale epidemiological studies (Japan, China, UK Biobank with 500,000+ participants) consistently associate regular tea consumption (3–5 cups/day) with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, and all-cause mortality. Mechanisms: catechins improve endothelial function, reduce LDL oxidation, lower blood pressure. The association is strongest for green tea in Asian populations, but black tea shows similar trends in Western studies.

Anti-inflammatory Properties

Both green and black tea polyphenols inhibit inflammatory pathways (NF-κB signaling, COX enzymes). Pu-erh's fermentation produces additional compounds including statins (naturally occurring lovastatin-like compounds). EGCG in green tea has shown inhibitory effects on inflammatory cytokines in cell studies. Chronic low-grade inflammation underlies most modern diseases — tea may modulate this, though human trial evidence is ongoing.

Gut Microbiome

Tea polyphenols act as prebiotics — selectively feeding beneficial bacteria (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium) while inhibiting harmful species. Pu-erh tea, fermented by microbes, acts as a probiotic as well. Studies show regular tea drinkers have notably different gut microbiome profiles. The tannins in black tea are partially responsible for its binding effect on pathogens. Green tea polyphenols survive digestion largely intact to reach the colon.

Tea Ceremonies & Traditions

Japanese Tea Ceremony — Chanoyu (茶の湯)

Developed in the 15th–16th centuries by tea masters including Sen no Rikyū, chanoyu is a ritualized preparation and presentation of matcha in a formal setting. Rooted in the Zen Buddhist principles of wabi (rustic simplicity), sabi (appreciation of imperfection), and ichigo ichie (this meeting will never happen again — treasure it). The ceremony involves prescribed movements for every action: entering the tearoom, cleansing utensils (chakin cloth, chasen whisk, chawan bowl), whisking matcha (usucha thin or koicha thick), and receiving the bowl with proper rotation. A full formal ceremony can last 4 hours. The tearoom (chashitsu) is deliberately humble — low doorway requiring a bow, minimal decoration, seasonal tokonoma alcove.

Gongfu Cha — Chinese Kung Fu Tea (工夫茶)

The practice of brewing tea with skill and intention. Using a small Yixing clay teapot, a gongfu tea set uses high leaf-to-water ratios and very short steeping times (15–45 seconds). The first steep is often discarded as a "rinse" — it awakens the leaves and removes any dust. Subsequent steeps (5–12 or more) each reveal different facets of the tea's character. The host pours for guests in a continuous motion. Widely practiced in Fujian, Guangdong, and Taiwan. Tea tasting (like wine tasting) is taken seriously — evaluating color, aroma, and the lingering aftertaste (huigan).

British Afternoon Tea

Anna, the 7th Duchess of Bedford, is credited with creating afternoon tea around 1840 to fill the long gap between lunch and the fashionable late dinner. Traditionally served at 4pm: a three-tiered stand with finger sandwiches (cucumber, smoked salmon, egg-and-cress) on the bottom, scones with clotted cream and jam in the middle, and miniature pastries and cakes on top. Served with a pot of black tea (usually Assam or Darjeeling blend) with milk. The "milk in first" debate has never been settled — scientifically, milk-first prevents the hot tea from scalding the milk proteins, but the middle class added milk after to prove their china was high-quality enough to withstand hot water.

Moroccan Mint Tea — Atay

The ultimate expression of hospitality. Gunpowder green tea (Chinese, packed in pellets) is brewed strong with fresh spearmint leaves and a large amount of sugar (often 3–4 tablespoons per small glass). The host pours the tea from a great height — sometimes 18 inches above the glass — both to aerate and create foam, and to demonstrate skill. The ritual of three glasses: "The first glass is as gentle as life, the second is as strong as love, the third is as bitter as death." Refusing tea is a serious social offense. The teapot is always a silver-colored metal pot with a curved spout.

Russian Samovar Tradition

The samovar (from Russian "self-boiler") is a large heated metal urn that keeps water at a rolling boil continuously. A small teapot of very strong tea concentrate (zavarka) sits on top, warming in the steam. Tea is served by pouring a small amount of zavarka into a glass cup (often held in a metal holder — podstakannik) and diluting with hot samovar water to taste. The samovar became the center of Russian social life in the 18th–19th centuries — an icon of warmth and hospitality. The tea glass is often accompanied by sugar to hold between the teeth while drinking (priglaskut).

Tisanes — Herbal Infusions

Technically, "tea" only comes from Camellia sinensis. Everything else is a tisane (herbal infusion) — though "herbal tea" is used colloquially. All tisanes are naturally caffeine-free unless blended with true tea.

Chamomile

Dried flowers of Matricaria chamomilla. Apple-like aroma, mildly sweet, honey-like flavor. The most popular herbal tea in the world. Traditional use as a sleep aid and digestive soother — contains apigenin, a flavonoid that binds to benzodiazepine receptors (mild sedative effect). Brew at 90°C for 5 minutes with a covered cup to trap volatile aromatics.

Peppermint

Mentha × piperita — a natural hybrid of watermint and spearmint. The menthol gives a powerful cooling sensation without any actual temperature change. Supports digestion, relieves IBS symptoms (menthol relaxes gut smooth muscle), and opens airways. One of the most versatile tisanes — excellent hot or cold, morning or evening. Brew at 95°C for 5–7 minutes.

Hibiscus

Dried calyces of Hibiscus sabdariffa. Vivid crimson color, intensely tart, cranberry-like flavor. Rich in vitamin C, anthocyanins, and quercetin. Multiple studies show it modestly lowers blood pressure. Served as Jamaica in Mexico, Bissap in West Africa, Karkade in Egypt — one of the most globally beloved tisanes. Excellent cold-brewed. Can stain — handle with care.

Rooibos

From Aspalathus linearis, a leguminous shrub from the Cederberg mountains of South Africa. Naturally sweet, earthy, vanilla-like. Completely caffeine-free. Unlike herbal infusions, it can be brewed strong without bitterness. Rich in aspalathin (unique to rooibos), a powerful antioxidant. Green rooibos (unfermented) is lighter and grassier; red (fermented) is the classic. Excellent with milk as a caffeine-free alternative to black tea.

Ginger

Fresh ginger root sliced thin and steeped, or dried ginger powder. Pungent, warming, spicy — the gingerols and shogaols create heat via TRPV1 receptor activation (same pathway as capsaicin). Effective anti-nausea (motion sickness, morning sickness), anti-inflammatory, digestive aid. Best fresh: 6–8 thin slices of unpeeled ginger, steep in near-boiling water for 10 minutes. Add honey and lemon for a classic cold remedy.

Lavender

Dried Lavandula angustifolia flowers. Intensely floral, slightly sweet, with woody undertones. Contains linalool (anxiety-reducing, studied for its GABA-modulatory effects). Often blended with chamomile for a sleep-support tisane. Important: use culinary lavender (not ornamental, which may be sprayed). A little goes a long way — too much is soapy. Excellent combined with lemon verbena and honey.

Elderflower

Fragrant white blossoms of Sambucus nigra. Delicately floral, honey-like, muscat-grape aroma. The flower heads must be used fresh or dried quickly — they ferment rapidly. Elderflower cordial is the quintessential British summer drink. The elder tree has a deep folklore history — European cultures believed it warded off evil spirits and possessed powerful healing properties. The berries (elderberry) make a different, more medicinal tisane.

Rose Hip

The fruit of Rosa canina, left on the bush after the rose petals fall. Tart, fruity, slightly earthy. Extraordinarily rich in vitamin C — dried rose hips contain up to 400–800mg per 100g (20x more than oranges). During WWII, Britain collected wild rose hips to make syrup for children when citrus imports were blocked. Also contains vitamin A, bioflavonoids, and lycopene. Brew at 95°C for 10–15 minutes.

Valerian

Root of Valeriana officinalis. Strong, earthy, almost unpleasant smell (isovaleric acid). One of the most research-backed herbal sleep aids — multiple RCTs show improved sleep quality and reduced time to fall asleep. Contains valerenic acid which modulates GABA receptors. Typically blended with hops and passionflower to mask flavor. Best consumed 1 hour before bed. Long-term use not recommended — the sedative effect may diminish.
