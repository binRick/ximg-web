Ancient Babylon

Mesopotamia — Where Writing, Law & Astronomy Were Born
3500 BC – 539 BC · The Fertile Crescent
Great Kings of Mesopotamia
Sargon of Akkad
2334–2279 BC · First Empire Builder
Created the world's first empire by unifying the Sumerian city-states under Akkadian rule. Built the city of Akkad. His empire stretched from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean — the first known empire in history.
Hammurabi
1792–1750 BC · Babylon's Greatest King
Sixth king of Babylon, who unified Mesopotamia under his rule. Authored the Code of Hammurabi — 282 laws carved into a massive black stele, the most complete ancient legal code. "An eye for an eye" originates here.
Nebuchadnezzar II
605–562 BC · Neo-Babylonian Empire
The most celebrated Neo-Babylonian king. Built the Hanging Gardens (one of the Seven Wonders), destroyed Jerusalem's First Temple in 586 BC, and created the Ishtar Gate — covered in brilliant blue glazed tiles.
Ur-Nammu
2112–2095 BC · Third Dynasty of Ur
Founded the Third Dynasty of Ur. Author of the world's oldest known law code (predating Hammurabi). Built massive ziggurats including the Great Ziggurat of Ur, remarkably well-preserved today.
Gilgamesh
c. 2700 BC · King of Uruk
Two-thirds divine, one-third mortal — the legendary king of Uruk. The Epic of Gilgamesh is humanity's oldest written story, exploring themes of friendship, mortality, and the search for eternal life.
Ashurbanipal
668–627 BC · Assyrian Library Builder
Last great Assyrian king, who assembled the Library of Nineveh — the ancient world's most comprehensive collection of cuneiform tablets. Preserved the Epic of Gilgamesh and thousands of other texts for posterity.
