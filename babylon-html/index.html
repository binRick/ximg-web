Ancient Babylon

Mesopotamia — Where Writing, Law & Astronomy Were Born
3500 BC – 539 BC · The Fertile Crescent
Great Kings of Mesopotamia
Sargon of Akkad
2334–2279 BC · First Empire Builder
Created the world's first empire by unifying the Sumerian city-states under Akkadian rule. Built the city of Akkad. His empire stretched from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean — the first known empire in history.
Hammurabi
1792–1750 BC · Babylon's Greatest King
Sixth king of Babylon, who unified Mesopotamia under his rule. Authored the Code of Hammurabi — 282 laws carved into a massive black stele, the most complete ancient legal code. "An eye for an eye" originates here.
Nebuchadnezzar II
605–562 BC · Neo-Babylonian Empire
The most celebrated Neo-Babylonian king. Built the Hanging Gardens (one of the Seven Wonders), destroyed Jerusalem's First Temple in 586 BC, and created the Ishtar Gate — covered in brilliant blue glazed tiles.
Ur-Nammu
2112–2095 BC · Third Dynasty of Ur
Founded the Third Dynasty of Ur. Author of the world's oldest known law code (predating Hammurabi). Built massive ziggurats including the Great Ziggurat of Ur, remarkably well-preserved today.
Gilgamesh
c. 2700 BC · King of Uruk
Two-thirds divine, one-third mortal — the legendary king of Uruk. The Epic of Gilgamesh is humanity's oldest written story, exploring themes of friendship, mortality, and the search for eternal life.
Ashurbanipal
668–627 BC · Assyrian Library Builder
Last great Assyrian king, who assembled the Library of Nineveh — the ancient world's most comprehensive collection of cuneiform tablets. Preserved the Epic of Gilgamesh and thousands of other texts for posterity.
Hammurabi's Code — Selected Laws
Law 1 — False Accusation of Murder
"If a man accuses another of a capital crime but cannot prove it, the accuser shall be put to death."
Introduced the presumption of evidence — accusers bore the burden of proof.
Law 8 — Theft of Property
"If a man steals an ox, sheep, donkey, pig or boat, he shall pay 30-fold if from a god or palace, or 10-fold if from a commoner. If he cannot pay, he shall be put to death."
Distinguished between theft from institutions and individuals; penalties scaled to social harm.
Law 22 — Highway Robbery
"If a man commits a robbery and is caught, that man shall be put to death."
Severe deterrence for crimes that disrupted trade — commerce was vital to Babylonian prosperity.
Law 196 — Bodily Injury
"If a man destroys the eye of another man, they shall destroy his eye."
The famous "lex talionis" — proportional punishment that influenced Mosaic law and beyond.
Law 229 — Builder Negligence
"If a builder builds a house and it collapses and kills the owner, that builder shall be put to death."
Established professional liability — one of the earliest concepts of legal responsibility for craftsmanship.
Law 282 — Freedom of Slaves
"If a slave says to his master 'You are not my master,' if they convict him his master shall cut off his ear."
The final law addressed slave relations — the code covered all aspects of Babylonian society, from trade to family.
Timeline of Mesopotamia
3500 BC
Invention of Writing
Sumerians develop cuneiform — the world's first writing system. Clay tablets record trade, laws, and stories. The word "cuneiform" means "wedge-shaped."
2334 BC
Sargon Creates First Empire
Sargon of Akkad conquers all Sumerian city-states, establishing humanity's first multiethnic empire covering modern-day Iraq, Syria, and parts of Turkey and Iran.
1792 BC
Hammurabi Ascends to Throne
Hammurabi begins his 42-year reign over Babylon. He will unify Mesopotamia and inscribe his famous law code on a 2.25m diorite stele.
605 BC
Neo-Babylonian Empire Peaks
Nebuchadnezzar II begins his reign. Babylon becomes the world's largest city (c. 200,000 people). Construction of the Ishtar Gate and the legendary Hanging Gardens.
539 BC
Cyrus the Great Conquers Babylon
Persian king Cyrus II enters Babylon without a battle. He frees the Jewish exiles (as recorded in the Hebrew Bible) and issues history's first human rights charter — the Cyrus Cylinder.

Gallery — Wonders of Ancient Babylon
Ishtar Gate
The magnificent blue-glazed brick gate of Babylon, decorated with dragons and bulls. Built by Nebuchadnezzar II, now reconstructed in Berlin's Pergamon Museum.
Code of Hammurabi
The 2.25-metre black diorite stele inscribed with 282 laws. Discovered in Susa in 1901, now in the Louvre, Paris. One of the oldest legal texts ever found.
Hammurabi & Shamash
Bas-relief from the top of the stele showing Hammurabi receiving the laws from Shamash, the sun god of justice. Divine authority underpins earthly law.
The Hanging Gardens
One of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, attributed to Nebuchadnezzar II. Terraced gardens rising above the Euphrates plain — a marvel of ancient engineering.
Cuneiform Chronicles
Clay tablets inscribed with wedge-shaped cuneiform script — the world's first writing system. Used to record trade, law, astronomy, and epic literature.
Babylon Today
The ruins of ancient Babylon near modern Hillah, Iraq. Once the world's largest city, these bricks still bear the stamp of Nebuchadnezzar II's name.
The Gardens of Semiramis
Romantic 19th-century depiction of the legendary Hanging Gardens. Some historians believe they may have actually been in Nineveh rather than Babylon.
