The Great Khans
Genghis Khan (Temüjin)
1162–1227 · Founder of the Empire
Born Temüjin in poverty, became the Great Khan in 1206 after uniting all Mongol tribes. A brilliant military strategist who introduced meritocracy, religious tolerance, and the Yasa legal code. Conquered from China to Persia. Ancestor to possibly 0.5% of all humans alive today.
Ögedei Khan
1229–1241 · The Expander
Third son of Genghis, who continued his father's conquests. Completed the conquest of Jin Dynasty China, conquered much of Eastern Europe (Russia, Poland, Hungary), and established the Mongol capital at Karakorum. His sudden death likely saved Western Europe from invasion.
Kublai Khan
1260–1294 · Founder of the Yuan Dynasty
Grandson of Genghis Khan who completed the conquest of Song Dynasty China and founded the Yuan Dynasty. Built a new capital at Khanbaliq (Beijing). Hosted Marco Polo. Twice tried to invade Japan — both fleets destroyed by storms (kamikaze — "divine wind").
Möngke Khan
1251–1259 · The Organizer
Reorganized the empire's administration and conducted a great census. Launched major campaigns against the Abbasid Caliphate and Song China. His death during the siege of Diaoyu Castle halted the Mongol advance into the Middle East and possibly spared Egypt.
Key Conquests & the Silk Road
Siege of Zhongdu (1215)
Jin Dynasty Capital Falls
Genghis Khan captures the Jin Dynasty capital (modern Beijing) after a prolonged siege. The sacking of Zhongdu shook the Chinese world and opened the way for Mongol control of northern China.
Destruction of the Khwarezmian Empire (1219–1221)
The Cities of the East Shattered
After the Shah insulted Mongol merchants, Genghis launched a devastating campaign. Samarkand, Bukhara, and Merv — great cities of the Islamic world — were destroyed. Millions died; the Aral Sea's river tributaries were diverted as an act of vengeance.
Battle of Mohi (1241)
Hungary Falls to the Golden Horde
Batu Khan and Subutai destroy the Hungarian army in two days, killing King Béla IV's force. Europe lay open — only Ögedei's death stopped the advance. The terror caused mass migrations and fortification across Central Europe.
Sack of Baghdad (1258)
End of the Abbasid Caliphate
Hülegü Khan's forces destroy the Abbasid Caliphate — one of the Islamic world's greatest powers. The Grand Library of Baghdad is destroyed; books thrown into the Tigris turned the river black with ink. The Caliph is executed, ending 500 years of Islamic golden age.
Pax Mongolica & the Silk Road
A United Eurasia
After conquest came the Pax Mongolica — a period of stability enabling unprecedented east-west trade. Marco Polo traveled the Silk Road under Mongol protection. Goods, ideas, and the Black Death traveled freely from China to Europe for the first time.
Battle of Ain Jalut (1260)
The First Mongol Defeat
The Egyptian Mamluk Sultanate defeats a Mongol force in Palestine — the first significant Mongol military loss. This battle halted Mongol expansion into Africa and marked the beginning of the empire's fragmentation.
Timeline of the Mongol Empire
1162
Birth of Temüjin
Born into a minor noble family on the Mongolian steppe. His father is poisoned when he is 9; he survives poverty, capture, and slavery before uniting the tribes.
1206
Genghis Khan Proclaimed
At a great kurultai (assembly), Temüjin is proclaimed Genghis Khan — "Universal Ruler" — over all Mongol tribes. He introduces the Yasa law code, meritocracy, and religious freedom.
1227
Death of Genghis Khan
Genghis dies during the Xi Xia campaign. His empire stretches from the Pacific Ocean to the Caspian Sea — the world's largest land empire to date. His location of burial remains unknown.
1241
Mongols Reach the Adriatic
Mongol cavalry reach the Adriatic Sea after crushing Poland and Hungary. Ögedei Khan's sudden death orders a withdrawal — Europe is saved by dynastic succession politics.
1271
Kublai Khan Founds Yuan Dynasty
Kublai establishes the Yuan Dynasty in China, blending Mongol and Chinese governance. Marco Polo arrives at his court at Khanbaliq (Beijing) 10 years later.
1368
End of the Yuan Dynasty
Chinese rebels drive the Mongols out of China, founding the Ming Dynasty. The Mongol Empire fragments into successor khanates — the Golden Horde, Ilkhanate, and Chagatai Khanate.

Mongol Warfare & Tactics
Feigned Retreat
The Deadly Withdrawal
The Mongols' most devastating tactic: retreat in apparent disorder, drawing the pursuing enemy out of formation, then wheel about and annihilate them with arrows. European knights, bred on the doctrine of pursuing a fleeing enemy, fell for this trick repeatedly from Mohi to Legnica.
Horse Archers
Composite Bow Mastery
The Mongol composite bow — made from horn, sinew, and wood — had a draw weight of 160 lbs and a range of 350 meters. Mongol warriors trained from childhood to fire accurately at a full gallop, delivering 10 aimed arrows per minute. A Mongol army was essentially a mobile artillery platform.
Psychological War
Terror as a Weapon
The Mongols deliberately spread stories of total destruction for cities that resisted, while offering lenient terms to those that surrendered. Cities that opened their gates received protection; those that fought faced massacre. Entire populations often fled before the army arrived, spreading panic ahead of the advance.
Siege Warfare
Mastering the City
Initially lacking siege capability, the Mongols recruited Chinese and Persian engineers to operate trebuchets, mangonels, and naphtha throwers. At Xiangyang (1267-1273), they maintained a five-year siege, constructing counterweight trebuchets from Persian designs to finally crack the city's defenses.
The Silk Road & Pax Mongolica
Yam System
The World's First Postal Network
Genghis Khan established a relay post system (Yam) with stations every 25-30 miles across the empire. Imperial messengers could travel 200 miles per day, carrying letters, goods, and intelligence across Asia. Marco Polo described riding the system in the 1270s from China to Persia in days rather than months.
Trade Safety
Pax Mongolica
The Mongol Peace (1250-1350) made the Silk Road safe for the first time in centuries. A merchant with a golden paiza (passport tablet) could travel from the Pacific to the Danube without fear of banditry. The Mongols executed anyone who robbed traveling merchants — trade meant tax revenue.
Black Death
The Unintended Legacy
The same trade routes that carried silk and spice also carried the bubonic plague westward. The bacterium Yersinia pestis traveled from Central Asia along Mongol supply lines, reaching Crimea by 1346 and Western Europe by 1347. The Black Death killed one-third of Europe's population — a devastating side effect of connectivity.
