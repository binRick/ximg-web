Key Conquests & the Silk Road
Siege of Zhongdu (1215)
Jin Dynasty Capital Falls
Genghis Khan captures the Jin Dynasty capital (modern Beijing) after a prolonged siege. The sacking of Zhongdu shook the Chinese world and opened the way for Mongol control of northern China.
Destruction of the Khwarezmian Empire (1219–1221)
The Cities of the East Shattered
After the Shah insulted Mongol merchants, Genghis launched a devastating campaign. Samarkand, Bukhara, and Merv — great cities of the Islamic world — were destroyed. Millions died; the Aral Sea's river tributaries were diverted as an act of vengeance.
Battle of Mohi (1241)
Hungary Falls to the Golden Horde
Batu Khan and Subutai destroy the Hungarian army in two days, killing King Béla IV's force. Europe lay open — only Ögedei's death stopped the advance. The terror caused mass migrations and fortification across Central Europe.
Sack of Baghdad (1258)
End of the Abbasid Caliphate
Hülegü Khan's forces destroy the Abbasid Caliphate — one of the Islamic world's greatest powers. The Grand Library of Baghdad is destroyed; books thrown into the Tigris turned the river black with ink. The Caliph is executed, ending 500 years of Islamic golden age.
Pax Mongolica & the Silk Road
A United Eurasia
After conquest came the Pax Mongolica — a period of stability enabling unprecedented east-west trade. Marco Polo traveled the Silk Road under Mongol protection. Goods, ideas, and the Black Death traveled freely from China to Europe for the first time.
Battle of Ain Jalut (1260)
The First Mongol Defeat
The Egyptian Mamluk Sultanate defeats a Mongol force in Palestine — the first significant Mongol military loss. This battle halted Mongol expansion into Africa and marked the beginning of the empire's fragmentation.
