The ISP Era
📀
AOL
America Online mailed 1 billion free trial CDs. At peak (2002), 35 million subscribers. "You've Got Mail." The training wheels of the internet. $21.95/month for unlimited access. Buying Time Warner for $164B in 2000 became history's worst acquisition.
☎
CompuServe
The original online service (1969). CIS addresses like 71234,567 preceded email. GIF format invented here (1987). Forums for every interest. Acquired by AOL in 1998. Beloved by technically-minded users.
🌩
Prodigy
IBM & Sears joint venture (1988). Graphical interface over dial-up. 2 million subscribers by 1992. Famous for family-friendly content filtering and bright, blocky graphics. Killed by the web.
🔌
The World (TIAC)
First commercial internet dial-up ISP in the US (1989). Based in Brookline, MA. Shell accounts with UNIX commands. Dial in at 9600 baud, use Gopher, Lynx, IRC, and early email. For the technically adventurous.
📞
NetZero / Juno
Free dial-up in exchange for displaying ads. Popular in late 1990s for budget internet access. NetZero boasted 10 million users in 2002. Ad-supported model eventually collapsed; pivoted to low-cost $9.95/month service.
🏃
The Busy Signal
The most hated sound of the 1990s. ISPs needed enough phone lines (POPs) to serve all users — miscalculate and every dial attempt hit a busy signal. "AOL busy signal" was a national meme. Drove demand for broadband.
