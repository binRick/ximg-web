Baud Rate History

"Baud" (named for Émile Baudot) measures symbols per second. At low speeds, baud = bits per second. Above 2400 baud, multiple bits per symbol made bps > baud. The race from 300 to 56,000 bps took 25 years of relentless innovation.

300 bps
Bell 103 / V.21 — 1962–1981
The acoustic coupler era. AT&T Bell 103 used frequency-shift keying (FSK). 300 bps = ~30 characters per second — you could read it as fast as it arrived. Modems fit in a desk drawer, with rubber cups for the handset.
1,200 bps
Bell 212A / V.22 — 1980
First major speed jump. Phase-shift keying (PSK) encoded 2 bits per symbol. Transformed BBS usage — text scrolled faster than you could read. The Hayes Smartmodem 1200 ($699 in 1981) became the standard.
2,400 bps
V.22bis — 1984
QAM encoding. Became the dominant BBS speed through the late 1980s. Most BBS software was designed around 2400 bps. A 1.44MB floppy took ~80 minutes to download. External modems blinked reassuringly.
9,600 bps
V.32 — 1984 (affordable by ~1991)
Echo cancellation made full-duplex 9600 possible on ordinary phone lines. Trellis-coded modulation (TCM). Originally cost $1,000+; fell to $200 by 1991. The speed that made large file transfers actually practical.
14,400 bps
V.32bis — 1991
Added higher modulation to V.32. Combined with V.42bis compression (4:1 ratio), effective throughput could reach ~57,600 bps on compressible data. The sweet spot for early internet dialup. A 1.44MB floppy: ~14 minutes.
28,800 bps
V.34 — 1994
Full V.34 spec pushed to 28.8 then 33.6 kbps. Near the theoretical Shannon limit for analog phone lines (~35 kbps). Trellis coding, precoding, and line probing. The ISP era's workhorse modem speed.
56,000 bps
V.90 / K56flex / X2 — 1998
Exploited the digital-to-analog conversion at the ISP end. Downloads could reach ~53 kbps (FCC limited to 53 kbps); uploads still limited to 33.6 kbps. X2 (US Robotics) and K56flex (Rockwell) warred until V.90 standardized in 1998.
∞ DSL / Cable
Broadband — 1999 onward
DSL used the high-frequency range of phone lines (above voice). Cable used TV coax. Both were always-on — no dialing, no busy signals, no tying up the phone. By 2002, broadband exceeded dialup subscribers in the US. The modem era ended quietly.
