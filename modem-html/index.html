The Dial-Up Era

Acoustic couplers, screeching handshakes, and the world at 56,000 bits per second
1958 – 2013
The AT Command Set

Hayes Microcomputer Products introduced the AT command set with the Smartmodem 300 in 1981. "AT" means "attention" — every command begins with it. The standard was so successful it became universal, used by virtually every dial-up modem ever made.

ATD
Dial
Dial a phone number. ATDT for tone dialing, ATDP for pulse. The most-used command of the dial-up era.
ATDT5551234 → dials 555-1234
ATH
Hook control
ATH0 hangs up the phone line. ATH1 goes off-hook (picks up). Parents used ATH0 to kick kids offline.
ATH0 → disconnect
ATA
Answer
Manually answer an incoming call. Usually handled automatically by auto-answer register S0.
ATA → answer ringing line
ATZ
Reset
Reset modem to stored profile. First command in almost every init string. Clears any previous state.
ATZ → OK
AT&F
Factory defaults
Load factory default settings. Used when modem is behaving strangely. Clears all custom S-register values.
AT&F → OK
ATI
Identify
Report modem information. ATI0=product code, ATI3=firmware version, ATI4=current settings. Useful for diagnostics.
ATI3 → V1.6 Rockwell
ATE
Echo
ATE1 echoes typed characters back (default). ATE0 disables echo. Important when terminal software handles its own display.
ATE0 → no local echo
ATM
Speaker mode
ATM0 = speaker always off. ATM1 = on until connected (default). ATM2 = always on. M0 was used at night to avoid waking family.
ATM0 → silent modem
ATL
Speaker volume
ATL0=low, ATL1=low, ATL2=medium, ATL3=high. Combined with ATM1 to control the infamous connection screech.
ATL0M1 → quiet connect
ATS0
Auto-answer rings
S0=0 disables auto-answer. S0=1 answers on first ring. BBS sysops set ATS0=1 so the modem picked up automatically.
ATS0=1 → auto-answer
AT+MS
Modulation select
Modern command to force specific modulation standard (V.34, V.90, etc.). Used to troubleshoot slow connections.
AT+MS=V90 → 56K only
AT&W
Write profile
Save current settings to non-volatile RAM. Survives power off. AT&W0 saves to profile 0, AT&W1 to profile 1.
AT&W0 → saved
Baud Rate History

"Baud" (named for Émile Baudot) measures symbols per second. At low speeds, baud = bits per second. Above 2400 baud, multiple bits per symbol made bps > baud. The race from 300 to 56,000 bps took 25 years of relentless innovation.

300 bps
Bell 103 / V.21 — 1962–1981
The acoustic coupler era. AT&T Bell 103 used frequency-shift keying (FSK). 300 bps = ~30 characters per second — you could read it as fast as it arrived. Modems fit in a desk drawer, with rubber cups for the handset.
1,200 bps
Bell 212A / V.22 — 1980
First major speed jump. Phase-shift keying (PSK) encoded 2 bits per symbol. Transformed BBS usage — text scrolled faster than you could read. The Hayes Smartmodem 1200 ($699 in 1981) became the standard.
2,400 bps
V.22bis — 1984
QAM encoding. Became the dominant BBS speed through the late 1980s. Most BBS software was designed around 2400 bps. A 1.44MB floppy took ~80 minutes to download. External modems blinked reassuringly.
9,600 bps
V.32 — 1984 (affordable by ~1991)
Echo cancellation made full-duplex 9600 possible on ordinary phone lines. Trellis-coded modulation (TCM). Originally cost $1,000+; fell to $200 by 1991. The speed that made large file transfers actually practical.
14,400 bps
V.32bis — 1991
Added higher modulation to V.32. Combined with V.42bis compression (4:1 ratio), effective throughput could reach ~57,600 bps on compressible data. The sweet spot for early internet dialup. A 1.44MB floppy: ~14 minutes.
28,800 bps
V.34 — 1994
Full V.34 spec pushed to 28.8 then 33.6 kbps. Near the theoretical Shannon limit for analog phone lines (~35 kbps). Trellis coding, precoding, and line probing. The ISP era's workhorse modem speed.
56,000 bps
V.90 / K56flex / X2 — 1998
Exploited the digital-to-analog conversion at the ISP end. Downloads could reach ~53 kbps (FCC limited to 53 kbps); uploads still limited to 33.6 kbps. X2 (US Robotics) and K56flex (Rockwell) warred until V.90 standardized in 1998.
∞ DSL / Cable
Broadband — 1999 onward
DSL used the high-frequency range of phone lines (above voice). Cable used TV coax. Both were always-on — no dialing, no busy signals, no tying up the phone. By 2002, broadband exceeded dialup subscribers in the US. The modem era ended quietly.
ITU Modem Standards
V.21
1964 · 300 bps · FSK
First international modem standard. Frequency-shift keying — two audio tones represent 0 and 1. Used by early acoustic coupler modems. Still used today for fax preambles.
V.22 / V.22bis
1980 · 1200 / 2400 bps · PSK / QAM
Phase-shift keying at 1200; V.22bis added QAM for 2400 bps. The BBS golden age standards. Backward compatible — two modems negotiated the fastest common speed.
V.32 / V.32bis
1984 · 9600 / 14400 bps · QAM + TCM
Full-duplex using echo cancellation. Trellis-coded modulation added error detection into the modulation itself. V.32bis extended to 14.4 kbps in 1991. Enabled practical internet dial-up.
V.34
1994 · 28800–33600 bps
The most sophisticated analog standard. Line probing (training sequence) at connection to measure line quality. Shaped constellation encoding adapts to line conditions. Near theoretical maximum for analog POTS.
V.42 / V.42bis
1989 · Error correction + Compression
V.42 added LAPM error correction (retransmit corrupt packets). V.42bis added LZW compression up to 4:1 ratio. Combined, a 14.4k modem could push ~57.6 kbps of compressible text. Critical for BBS file transfers.
V.90 / V.92
1998 / 2000 · 53 kbps down / 48 kbps up
V.90 exploited digital ISP connections for asymmetric speed. V.92 added Modem-on-Hold (pause dial-up for incoming calls) and faster handshake. The final chapter of analog modem evolution.
MNP Classes
1984 · Microcom Networking Protocol
Proprietary Microcom standard, later adopted widely. MNP 4 = error correction. MNP 5 = 2:1 compression. MNP 10 = adverse channel enhancements for noisy lines. Competed with V.42 until V.42 won.
V.8 / V.8bis
1994 · Handshake negotiation
The protocol for negotiating which data standard to use during the connection handshake. The screeching tones you heard were modems exchanging V.8 capability advertisements before settling on a speed.
The ISP Era
📀
AOL
America Online mailed 1 billion free trial CDs. At peak (2002), 35 million subscribers. "You've Got Mail." The training wheels of the internet. $21.95/month for unlimited access. Buying Time Warner for $164B in 2000 became history's worst acquisition.
☎
CompuServe
The original online service (1969). CIS addresses like 71234,567 preceded email. GIF format invented here (1987). Forums for every interest. Acquired by AOL in 1998. Beloved by technically-minded users.
🌩
Prodigy
IBM & Sears joint venture (1988). Graphical interface over dial-up. 2 million subscribers by 1992. Famous for family-friendly content filtering and bright, blocky graphics. Killed by the web.
🔌
The World (TIAC)
First commercial internet dial-up ISP in the US (1989). Based in Brookline, MA. Shell accounts with UNIX commands. Dial in at 9600 baud, use Gopher, Lynx, IRC, and early email. For the technically adventurous.
📞
NetZero / Juno
Free dial-up in exchange for displaying ads. Popular in late 1990s for budget internet access. NetZero boasted 10 million users in 2002. Ad-supported model eventually collapsed; pivoted to low-cost $9.95/month service.
🏃
The Busy Signal
The most hated sound of the 1990s. ISPs needed enough phone lines (POPs) to serve all users — miscalculate and every dial attempt hit a busy signal. "AOL busy signal" was a national meme. Drove demand for broadband.
The Sound of Connecting

The handshake sequence between two modems was a precise choreography of tones, each phase serving a technical purpose. Generation X and Millennials can identify it instantly.

Phase 1 — Dial Tone & Dialing
────────────────── DTMF tones ──────────────────
Modem goes off-hook and detects dial tone. ATDT command sends dual-tone multi-frequency (DTMF) signals — the same tones as pressing digits on a touchtone phone. A phone on the same line would ring once.
Phase 2 — Remote Ringing & Answer
ring... ring... BONG (answer tone 2100 Hz)
The remote modem (at your ISP or BBS) detects the incoming call. It answers and sends a 2100 Hz answer tone (the distinctive single high-pitched "bong"). This tells the calling modem it's talking to another modem, not a person.
Phase 3 — V.8 Capability Exchange
~~~~~ ci tone ~~~~~ answer sequence ~~~~~
The screech begins. Both modems broadcast their V.8 capability lists — which standards they support (V.34, V.42, MNP, etc.). This sounds like rapid alternating tones. The modems are introducing themselves and negotiating protocols.
Phase 4 — Training & Line Probing
SCREEEEEEEEEEEE ← carrier tones → WHEEEEEEE
The loudest phase. Modems exchange known training sequences to measure the phone line's characteristics — noise floor, signal attenuation, frequency response. The modem uses this to select optimal symbol rate, constellation size, and precoding.
Phase 5 — Rate Negotiation & Sync
▓▓░░▓░▓▓░░▓░▓▓░░▓░ (bit sync pattern)
Final speed negotiation. Both modems agree on the fastest mutually-supported speed the line can sustain. Bit synchronization is established. Error correction layer (V.42) performs its own handshake.
Phase 6 — CONNECT
CONNECT 33600 → speaker off → silence
The modem reports "CONNECT 33600" (or whatever speed was negotiated) to the terminal software. Speaker cuts out. The data session begins. That distinctive silence after the screech was the sound of the internet arriving.
Timeline of the Modem
1958
SAGE — First Modem
The US Air Force Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) air defense system uses modems to transmit radar data over phone lines. IBM builds the first commercial modem. Not intended for consumers — each unit is the size of a refrigerator.
1962
Bell 103 — 300 bps for the Masses
AT&T introduces the Bell 103 modem. 300 bps full-duplex over standard phone lines. Acoustic couplers allow any phone to be used. University researchers start connecting computers to the ARPANET. The era of personal data communication begins.
1978
Ward Christensen's MODEM.ASM
Ward Christensen writes MODEM.ASM, the first public-domain file transfer program. Simultaneously creates CBBS, the first BBS. XMODEM protocol allows reliable file transfers. The foundation of hobbyist modem culture is laid.
1981
Hayes Smartmodem — AT Commands Born
Hayes Microcomputer Products releases the Smartmodem 300. A microprocessor inside the modem interprets AT commands from software — no manual switches needed. Priced at $299. The AT command set becomes the universal standard for the next 30 years.
1991
14.4K — Internet Becomes Usable
V.32bis modems at 14,400 bps become affordable (~$200). Combined with V.42bis compression, throughput reaches ~57 kbps for text. Graphical web browsers like Mosaic (1993) become practical. The ISP industry explodes. AOL begins mass-mailing floppy disks (later CDs).
1996
56K Wars — X2 vs K56flex
US Robotics launches X2 (57.6 kbps). Rockwell counters with K56flex. Both proprietary, incompatible. ISPs must support both. Consumers must choose sides. The FCC caps downloads at 53 kbps (to protect phone network integrity).
1998
V.90 — 56K Standard Agreed
ITU ratifies V.90, ending the 56K format war. Most X2 and K56flex modems receive firmware updates. 56K modems become the universal standard. ADSL DSL and cable internet exist but cost $50–80/month — still premium.
2001
Broadband Crosses 10 Million US Subscribers
DSL and cable internet prices drop below $30/month. Always-on connections eliminate the ritual of dialing in. "Disconnecting mom from the internet" becomes impossible. Teenage phone lines across America finally ring again.
2013
Dial-Up's Last Stand
AOL still reports 2.5 million dial-up subscribers in 2013. Rural areas without cable or DSL infrastructure keep dial-up alive. NetZero and AOL continue selling service. The final holdouts are in areas where broadband still hasn't arrived — some 20 million Americans as of 2020.
Modem Gallery

Hardware that defined the dial-up era — acoustic couplers, internal ISA cards, external 56K boxes, and the modems that brought millions online.

Acoustic Coupler (1960s–70s)
The original modem interface: telephone handset cradled in rubber cups. Used before direct-connect modems were legal in the US. Operated at 300 baud via audio tones transmitted through the handset.
Acoustic Coupler — Field Use
Portable acoustic couplers let reporters and travelers connect to mainframes from any payphone. The AT&T Carterfone decision (1968) eventually opened the door for direct-connect modems, making these obsolete.
Hayes Smartmodem 300 (1981)
The modem that defined an era. Dale Heatherington and Dennis Hayes invented the AT command set here. Every PC modem from 1981 to the end of dial-up spoke Hayes-compatible AT commands. "ATDT" and "ATH0" became common knowledge.
External Analogue Modem (1980s–90s)
The external desktop modem with its array of status LEDs — CD, RD, SD, TR, MR, AA — gave users a visual window into the handshake process. The blinking lights became the icon of the dial-up era.
ISA Internal Modem (late 1980s–90s)
Internal modems on ISA expansion cards were cheaper than external units and took no desk space. They used the host CPU for some signal processing, a tradeoff that became the basis for the controversial "Winmodem" design.
AT&T DataPhone Modem
AT&T's Bell System modems dominated enterprise and government computing before the personal computer era. The Bell 103 (1962) established 300 baud full-duplex as the first dial-up standard, lasting nearly two decades.
Winmodem / HSP Modem (mid-1990s)
Host Signal Processing modems offloaded DSP work to the CPU, cutting hardware costs drastically. Nicknamed "Winmodems" for their Windows-only drivers, they were widely despised — particularly by Linux users who spent years reverse-engineering proprietary driver blobs.
Cable Modem (late 1990s–2000s)
DOCSIS-standard cable modems ended the dial-up era for millions. Always-on connections at 512 Kbps–10 Mbps made the screeching handshake a memory. By 2003, cable and DSL broadband subscribers outnumbered dial-up in the US.
