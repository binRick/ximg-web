The Sound of Connecting

The handshake sequence between two modems was a precise choreography of tones, each phase serving a technical purpose. Generation X and Millennials can identify it instantly.

Phase 1 — Dial Tone & Dialing
────────────────── DTMF tones ──────────────────
Modem goes off-hook and detects dial tone. ATDT command sends dual-tone multi-frequency (DTMF) signals — the same tones as pressing digits on a touchtone phone. A phone on the same line would ring once.
Phase 2 — Remote Ringing & Answer
ring... ring... BONG (answer tone 2100 Hz)
The remote modem (at your ISP or BBS) detects the incoming call. It answers and sends a 2100 Hz answer tone (the distinctive single high-pitched "bong"). This tells the calling modem it's talking to another modem, not a person.
Phase 3 — V.8 Capability Exchange
~~~~~ ci tone ~~~~~ answer sequence ~~~~~
The screech begins. Both modems broadcast their V.8 capability lists — which standards they support (V.34, V.42, MNP, etc.). This sounds like rapid alternating tones. The modems are introducing themselves and negotiating protocols.
Phase 4 — Training & Line Probing
SCREEEEEEEEEEEE ← carrier tones → WHEEEEEEE
The loudest phase. Modems exchange known training sequences to measure the phone line's characteristics — noise floor, signal attenuation, frequency response. The modem uses this to select optimal symbol rate, constellation size, and precoding.
Phase 5 — Rate Negotiation & Sync
▓▓░░▓░▓▓░░▓░▓▓░░▓░ (bit sync pattern)
Final speed negotiation. Both modems agree on the fastest mutually-supported speed the line can sustain. Bit synchronization is established. Error correction layer (V.42) performs its own handshake.
Phase 6 — CONNECT
CONNECT 33600 → speaker off → silence
The modem reports "CONNECT 33600" (or whatever speed was negotiated) to the terminal software. Speaker cuts out. The data session begins. That distinctive silence after the screech was the sound of the internet arriving.
