Protein

The building block of muscle, enzymes, hormones, and immune function. Made of 20 amino acids, 9 of which are essential (must come from diet). Complete proteins contain all 9 essential amino acids: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, quinoa, soy. Incomplete proteins (most plants) are missing one or more — combine beans + rice, or hummus + pita for a complete profile. Protein has the highest thermic effect of food (25–30% of calories burned in digestion).

Carbohydrates

Simple carbs (sugars): mono- and disaccharides — glucose, fructose, sucrose. Rapidly digested, quick energy spike. Complex carbs: long chains of glucose (starch, glycogen). Slower digestion, more sustained energy. Fiber: non-digestible complex carbs. Soluble fiber (oats, beans) forms a gel, slows digestion, lowers LDL. Insoluble fiber (wheat bran, vegetables) adds bulk, speeds gut transit. Recommended: 25–38g fiber/day; most Americans get ~15g.

Fats

Saturated fats (solid at room temp — butter, coconut oil, animal fat): no double bonds in the carbon chain. Raise LDL cholesterol; some debate on cardiovascular impact. Unsaturated fats: monounsaturated (olive oil, avocado, almonds) and polyunsaturated (omega-3 and omega-6). Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils): raise LDL, lower HDL — banned or heavily restricted in most countries since 2015+. Essential fatty acids: omega-3 (EPA, DHA from fish; ALA from flaxseed) and omega-6 cannot be synthesized.

Fiber — Deep Dive

Soluble fiber: dissolves in water, forms viscous gel in gut. Slows gastric emptying, reduces post-meal blood glucose spikes, feeds beneficial gut bacteria (prebiotic effect), lowers LDL by binding bile acids. Sources: oats, barley, legumes, apples, psyllium. Insoluble fiber: doesn't dissolve, adds bulk, softens stool, speeds transit time. Prevents constipation and diverticular disease. Sources: wheat bran, vegetables, nuts. Resistant starch (cooked-then-cooled rice and potatoes) acts as both fiber and prebiotic.

Calories by Food Category

Fruits

Generally low-calorie (50–100kcal per 100g), high water content, rich in vitamin C, potassium, and antioxidants. High in fructose — a simple sugar. The fiber in whole fruit slows sugar absorption significantly (vs juice, which has no fiber). Berries (50–60kcal/100g) are the most nutrient-dense. Tropical fruits (mango, banana) are higher calorie. Dried fruit is calorie-dense — raisins are ~300kcal/100g.

Best choices: berries, citrus, apple, kiwi. Highest sugar: dates, grapes, mangoes, figs.

Vegetables

Non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, spinach, lettuce, peppers, cucumber): 15–35kcal/100g, the most nutrient-dense foods in existence. Eat as much as you want. Cruciferous (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, kale): rich in glucosinolates, vitamin K, folate. Leafy greens: exceptional micronutrient density. Starchy vegetables (potato, corn, peas, winter squash): 70–100kcal/100g — eat mindfully.

Potatoes: 77kcal/100g boiled. Sweet potato: 86kcal/100g. Corn: 86kcal/100g.

Proteins

Animal protein: typically 100–250kcal/100g cooked, high bioavailability. Chicken breast (165kcal, 31g protein) is the dieter's staple. Fatty fish (salmon, 208kcal) adds beneficial omega-3s. Plant protein: legumes (130–230kcal/100g cooked, 7–15g protein) have significant carbohydrate; tofu (76kcal, 8g protein) is extremely versatile. Protein quality (PDCAAS/DIAAS scoring) ranks egg and whey highest, followed by meat, then soy, then other legumes.

Grains

Whole grains: retain bran and germ — more fiber, vitamins, minerals, and phytonutrients than refined. Brown rice, whole wheat bread, oats, quinoa, barley. Refined grains: bran and germ removed — white rice, white flour, white bread. Higher glycemic index, lower micronutrient density. Quinoa is notable for being a complete protein (8g protein/cup). Oats contain beta-glucan, a soluble fiber shown to lower LDL and improve insulin sensitivity.

Nuts & Seeds

Calorie-dense (550–700kcal/100g) but extraordinarily nutrient-rich. Dominated by healthy unsaturated fats, with good protein, fiber, magnesium, vitamin E. Almonds: 164kcal/oz, 6g protein. Walnuts: 185kcal/oz, rich in ALA omega-3. Cashews: 157kcal/oz, highest carb of common nuts. Chia seeds: 138kcal/oz, 9g omega-3 ALA, 10g fiber. Flaxseeds: 150kcal/oz, best plant source of omega-3. Portion control matters — a small handful is 150–200 calories.

Oils & Fats

All oils are 100% fat — approximately 120kcal/tbsp (14g). Olive oil: 73% monounsaturated oleic acid, rich in polyphenols (extra virgin). Smoke point ~375°F (refined: 465°F). Coconut oil: 90% saturated fat, medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs). Avocado oil: high smoke point (520°F), excellent for high-heat cooking. Butter: 80% fat, 102kcal/tbsp, contains butyrate (gut-healthy). Ghee: clarified butter, lactose-free, higher smoke point (485°F).

EPOC — Exercise Post-Oxygen Consumption

High-intensity exercise (HIIT, heavy lifting) creates an "afterburn effect" where elevated oxygen consumption continues for 12–48 hours post-workout. This increases total energy expenditure beyond what's burned during exercise itself. HIIT and resistance training generate more EPOC than steady-state cardio. However, the magnitude is often overstated — EPOC from a single HIIT session adds perhaps 50–100 extra calories.

NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis

The calories burned from all movement that isn't formal exercise — fidgeting, standing, walking to the fridge, doing laundry. NEAT varies enormously between individuals (200–2,000+ kcal/day) and is a major driver of metabolic differences. A person with high NEAT (naturally active, fidgets, walks everywhere) can burn 500–1,000 more calories per day than a sedentary person of the same size. Getting a standing desk, parking farther away, or taking stairs makes a genuine difference over weeks and months.

Metabolism & Energy

BMR — Basal Metabolic Rate

The calories burned at complete rest — keeping your heart beating, lungs breathing, organs functioning, and body temperature maintained. Typically 60–75% of total daily energy expenditure. The Mifflin-St Jeor equation (most accurate): Men: (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) − (5 × age) + 5. Women: (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) − (5 × age) − 161. Example: 35-year-old 70kg 175cm man: BMR ≈ 1,716 kcal/day.

TDEE — Total Daily Energy Expenditure

Your true daily calorie need = BMR + three components: NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — all daily movement), EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — formal workouts), TEF (Thermic Effect of Food — energy cost of digestion). Activity multipliers: Sedentary (×1.2), Light activity 1-3x/week (×1.375), Moderate 3-5x/week (×1.55), Very active 6-7x/week (×1.725), Athlete/physical job (×1.9).

Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)

The energy cost of digesting, absorbing, and processing nutrients. Varies significantly by macronutrient: Protein: 25–30% of calories are used in processing (eat 100kcal of protein, net ~72–75kcal). Carbohydrates: 6–8% (100kcal → ~92–94 net). Fat: 2–3% (most efficient to store — 100kcal → ~97–98 net). Alcohol: ~15–20%. High-protein diets have a meaningful metabolic advantage over isocaloric high-fat diets.

Muscle Mass & Metabolism

Skeletal muscle is metabolically active tissue — it burns approximately 6 calories per pound per day at rest (vs fat tissue: ~2 kcal/lb/day). A person with significantly more muscle mass has a meaningfully higher BMR. Resistance training builds muscle, which permanently elevates resting metabolism. After age 30, adults lose 3–5% muscle mass per decade (sarcopenia) unless they resistance train — a key reason metabolism slows with age.

Hormonal Regulation

Insulin: released by pancreas in response to blood glucose — promotes glucose uptake and fat storage. Chronically elevated insulin (from high-sugar diets) promotes fat accumulation. Leptin (satiety hormone): secreted by fat cells, signals "enough" to the brain. Obesity → leptin resistance → satiety signal ignored. Ghrelin (hunger hormone): rises before meals, drops after eating. Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin — explaining why tired people eat more. Cortisol: chronic stress raises cortisol, promoting abdominal fat storage.

Adaptive Thermogenesis

The body actively resists significant weight loss by reducing metabolic rate beyond what's expected from lost mass alone. During caloric restriction, the body reduces non-exercise movement (NEAT), lowers body temperature, decreases thyroid activity, and reduces leptin. This "metabolic adaptation" can reduce TDEE by 100–400 kcal/day beyond expected. This is why weight loss plateaus occur and why large deficits become less effective over time — the body fights back. Diet breaks and refeeds may mitigate this partially.

Nutrition Myths — Fact vs Fiction

"Calories In = Calories Out" Is Too Simple

Verdict: Directionally correct but mechanistically incomplete. Energy balance is real — sustained calorie deficit produces weight loss. However, not all calories are metabolically equivalent: protein has higher TEF, fiber content affects absorption, food matrix matters. Ultra-processed foods (high palatability, fast digestion) produce different hormonal responses than whole foods with identical calories. The CICO model is correct for physics; nutrition science adds important nuance.

"Starvation Mode" Stops Weight Loss

Verdict: Partially true, dramatically overstated. Metabolic adaptation is real (see Metabolism tab) — extreme restriction slows metabolism. But the body cannot defy thermodynamics. Severe restriction (500–1,000 kcal/day) does produce weight loss, but can cause muscle loss, hormonal disruption, and nutrient deficiency. The "starvation mode" myth implies metabolism can drop enough to prevent all loss — it cannot. Sustainable 300–500 kcal deficits minimize adaptation while producing steady loss.

"Metabolism Booster" Foods

Verdict: Largely marketing fiction. Green tea extract adds 80–100 kcal/day of extra burn at best. Capsaicin (chili) adds ~20–30 kcal. Coffee/caffeine: 100mg caffeine ≈ 100 kcal/day (fades quickly with tolerance). Apple cider vinegar, coconut oil, "fat burners" — effect sizes are tiny or nonexistent. The most effective "metabolism booster" is building muscle mass through resistance training — the effect is permanent and substantial (hundreds of calories/day over years).

Negative Calorie Foods (Celery)

Verdict: False. No food requires more calories to digest than it provides. Celery is ~6 kcal per stalk and requires ~0.5 kcal to digest. It's extremely low-calorie and high-water, which makes it useful for volume eating — but it's not "negative calorie." Foods with high water content (cucumber, lettuce, celery) are valuable tools for managing hunger through volume without significant caloric cost, but the negative calorie claim is biologically impossible.

Breakfast Is the Most Important Meal

Verdict: Context-dependent, not universally true. Breakfast is not mandatory. Intermittent fasting research shows skipping breakfast doesn't slow metabolism or impair muscle. For some people, breakfast reduces total daily intake; for others, it increases it. Athletes and growing children generally benefit from breakfast. The "breakfast boosts metabolism" claim comes from industry-funded cereal research. Total daily intake matters far more than meal timing for most people.

Fat Makes You Fat

Verdict: False. Dietary fat does not directly become body fat — all macronutrients can be stored as fat when consumed in excess. The low-fat diet era (1980s–2000s) was driven by flawed research and industry lobbying (sugar industry paid scientists to shift blame to fat). Dietary fat is essential for hormone production, fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), cell membrane integrity, and brain function. Avocados, olive oil, and nuts are among the healthiest foods despite being high in fat.

Meal Timing and "Metabolism Windows"

Verdict: Modest effect, often overstated. The post-workout "anabolic window" for protein consumption is real but longer than commonly believed — muscle protein synthesis is elevated for 24–48 hours after training, not just 30 minutes. Eating protein near training is beneficial, but the exact timing is flexible. Eating late at night doesn't inherently cause fat gain — total daily calories drive body composition. However, circadian rhythm research suggests metabolic efficiency is somewhat higher in the morning.
