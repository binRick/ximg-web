Origins of Coffee

Roast Levels

Coffee roasting transforms green beans into the aromatic brown beans we know. Heat causes the Maillard reaction and caramelization — developing hundreds of flavor compounds while reducing acidity and increasing body at darker levels.

Green Light Medium Medium-Dark Dark Extra Dark

Light Roast

No oil on surface. Origin flavors preserved. High acidity. Fruity, floral, tea-like. First crack only.

Examples: Cinnamon Roast, New England, City Roast

Medium Roast

Balanced acidity and body. Caramel and nut notes develop. Most popular in the US. Between first and second crack.

Examples: American Roast, City+, Breakfast Blend

Medium-Dark

Rich, dark flavor. Slight bittersweet aftertaste. Oil begins to surface on bean. End of second crack.

Examples: Full City+, After Dinner, Vienna Roast

Dark Roast

Oil visible on surface. Bold, smoky, low acid. Roast flavor dominates origin character. Carbon notes emerge.

Examples: French Roast, Espresso Roast

Extra Dark

Charred, bittersweet, intense body. Almost all origin flavor lost. Bean surface heavily oiled. Very low caffeine per volume.

Examples: Italian Roast, Spanish Roast

The Roasting Process

Drying Phase

150–200°C. Green beans lose moisture (10–12% water content). Beans turn yellow, smell grassy. Endothermic phase — beans absorb heat.

Maillard Reaction

~150–180°C. Amino acids and sugars react. Brown compounds (melanoidins) form. Hundreds of aromatic compounds develop. The core of coffee flavor.

First Crack

~196°C. Steam pressure builds inside. Cell walls rupture — audible crack. Bean doubles in size. Exothermic. Light roasts pulled here.

Second Crack

~224°C. CO2 escapes from bean structure again. Beans become brittle. Oil migrates to surface. Dark roasts pulled at or just before second crack.

Coffee Varieties

The coffee plant belongs to the genus Coffea, with over 100 species. Just three dominate commercial production. Within those species, hundreds of varietals offer extraordinary flavor diversity.

Coffea Arabica

The premium species. Accounts for ~60% of world production. Grown at high altitude, requires careful cultivation. Lower caffeine, higher sugar content, complex acidity. Origin: Ethiopian highlands.

Caffeine: ~1.2–1.5% | Chromosomes: 44 (tetraploid)

Coffea Canephora (Robusta)

The workhorse species. ~40% of production. Hardy, disease-resistant, high-yield. Grown at lower altitudes. Higher caffeine, earthier flavor. Key to espresso blends and instant coffee.

Caffeine: ~2.7% | Chromosomes: 22 (diploid)

Coffea Liberica

The rare third. <1% of production. Large beans, asymmetric shape. Distinctive smoky, woody, floral notes. Popular in Philippines (Barako). Resistant to leaf rust disease.

Caffeine: ~1.2% | Native to West Africa

Notable Varietals

Typica
The original Arabica. All modern varietals descend from Typica or Bourbon. Clean, sweet, low yield. Parent of Jamaican Blue Mountain, Kona, Maragogipe.
Bourbon
Mutation of Typica from Réunion Island (formerly Bourbon). Caramel sweetness, balanced acidity. Foundation of Colombian and Central American coffees.
Geisha (Gesha)
From Ethiopia via Panama. The world's most expensive varietal. Extraordinary jasmine, bergamot, peach, mango flavors. Hacienda La Esmeralda made it famous in 2004.
SL28 / SL34
Scott Laboratories selections from Kenya. Drought-resistant with intense blackcurrant and tomato juice acidity. The defining character of Kenyan AA coffee.
Caturra
Dwarf mutation of Bourbon. High yield, easy to harvest. Bright acidity, medium body. Widely grown in Colombia and Central America. Susceptible to leaf rust.
Catuai
Cross of Mundo Novo × Caturra. Wind-resistant, high yield. Brazil's most planted varietal. Pleasant sweetness, medium body, mild acidity.
Pacamara
Cross of Pacas × Maragogipe. El Salvador specialty. Very large beans. Complex fruit, floral, herbal, and chocolate notes. Exceptional cup quality.
Kenyan AA
A grade classification (not a varietal) referring to large-screen Kenyan beans. Typically SL28/SL34. Consistently one of the world's highest-scoring coffees.

Coffee House History

1450s — Yemen
Sufi Monasteries
Yemeni Sufi monks use qahwa (coffee) during night devotions to stay awake. Coffee cultivation begins in the Yemeni highlands at Mocha. The port city of Mokha becomes the world's first major coffee trading hub.
1500s — Ottoman
Qahvehane — First Coffee Houses
Constantinople (Istanbul) sees the rise of qahvehane — public houses where men gather to drink coffee, play chess, discuss politics, and hear music. Called "schools of the wise." Sultan Murad IV bans coffee in 1623 (unsuccessfully).
1650 — Oxford
Britain's Coffee House Revolution
The Queen's Lane Coffee House opens in Oxford — one of the first in England. By 1700, London has 2,000+ coffee houses. Lloyd's of London starts as a coffee house. The London Stock Exchange emerges from Jonathan's Coffee House. Called "penny universities" (price of admission).
1683 — Vienna
Viennese Kaffeehaus
After the Battle of Vienna, Franz Georg Kolschitzky uses captured Ottoman coffee to open Vienna's first coffee house. The Viennese café becomes a literary and philosophical institution — Freud, Klimt, Mahler, and Trotsky all regular patrons. Added milk and sugar to the recipe.
1700s — France
French Café Culture
Café de Procope (1686, Paris) hosts Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, and Napoleon Bonaparte. Coffee houses fuel Enlightenment discourse and, some argue, the French Revolution. French café culture becomes synonymous with intellectual life.
1900s — Italy
The Espresso Bar
Angelo Moriondo patents the first espresso machine in 1884. Luigi Bezzera refines it in 1901. Standing at the bar, drinking a 25-second shot becomes Italian ritual. The bar as a social hub — quick, efficient, deeply communal.
1990s–now — Seattle
Third Wave Coffee Movement
Starbucks (1971) leads the second wave — coffee as premium product. The third wave treats coffee as artisan craft, like fine wine. Single-origin sourcing, transparent farm relationships, precision roasting, skilled baristas. Counter Culture, Intelligentsia, Stumptown lead the charge.

History of Coffee

From a legendary goat herder in Ethiopia to a $100 billion global industry, coffee has shaped empires, fueled revolutions, and changed the way humanity thinks, works, and socializes.

~850 CE — Ethiopia
Kaldi & the Dancing Goats
Ethiopian legend tells of goat herder Kaldi, who notices his goats dancing after eating red berries from a certain tree. He brings the berries to a monastery; the abbot makes a drink and finds it keeps him alert during evening prayers. The legend of coffee begins.
15th century — Yemen
Sufi Monks & the Port of Mocha
Sufi monks in Yemen become the first documented coffee drinkers, using it to stay awake for night prayers. Cultivation begins in the Yemeni port city of Mocha (Al-Makha), which becomes the world's first major coffee trading hub — and gives "mocha" its name.
1475 — Constantinople
Kiva Han — World's First Coffee House
Kiva Han opens in Constantinople (Istanbul) — believed to be the world's first coffee house (qahveh khaneh, "wine of Islam"). Coffee becomes central to social and intellectual life across the Ottoman Empire.
1600 — Europe
Coffee Reaches Europe
Coffee reaches Europe via Venetian traders. Initially called "the bitter invention of Satan" by some clergy. Pope Clement VIII tastes it, approves it, and baptizes it — clearing the way for European coffee culture.
1652 — England
"Penny Universities" — English Coffee Houses
The first English coffee house opens in Oxford. London soon has over 300. Called "penny universities" — for the price of a penny (cost of coffee) anyone could sit and debate with anyone. Lloyd's of London and the London Stock Exchange both begin as coffee houses.
1683 — Vienna
The Viennese Café
After the Battle of Vienna, the Ottomans leave behind sacks of coffee beans. Franz Georg Kolschitzky opens Central Europe's first café — adding milk and honey, creating the modern café style. The Viennese kaffeehaus becomes a literary institution.
1714 — France
One Plant, 90% of the World
Amsterdam's mayor gives Louis XIV a coffee plant. The French take it to Martinique. From that single plant, an estimated 90% of the world's coffee plants are eventually descended — one of history's most consequential botanical gifts.
1773 — America
The Boston Tea Party
The Boston Tea Party makes coffee the patriotic American drink. Tea becomes politically undesirable. Americans switch to coffee en masse — a cultural shift that endures to this day.
1822 — France
The Espresso Machine
The first espresso machine prototype is invented in France. Italian Luigi Bezzera patents a commercial espresso machine in 1901, enabling the fast, concentrated shot that defines Italian café culture.
1864 — USA
Industrial Roasting
Jabez Burns invents the industrial coffee roaster, enabling consistent roasting at scale. The modern coffee industry is born — mass-market packaged coffee becomes possible for the first time.
1901 — Instant coffee
Instant Coffee & WWII
Satori Kato (Japanese-American) patents instant coffee. Nescafé perfects and commercializes it in 1938, supplying millions of cans to Allied troops in WWII. Instant coffee wires an entire generation to caffeine.
1940 — Home brewing
The Drip Machine Era
Mr. Coffee and similar automatic drip machines (popularized in 1972) transform American home brewing. Coffee becomes the fuel of the workplace and the morning ritual of a nation.
1971 — Seattle
Starbucks Opens
Starbucks opens its first store at Seattle's Pike Place Market selling roasted beans. Howard Schultz joins in 1982, visits Milan's espresso bars, and transforms Starbucks into a café chain by 1987 — sparking the second wave of coffee culture worldwide.
1980s — Second wave
Espresso Goes Mainstream
The second wave: specialty coffee and espresso drinks — lattes, cappuccinos, macchiatos — become mainstream in the US. Espresso bars spread globally. Coffee is no longer just fuel; it's an experience.
2000s — Third wave
Coffee as Artisan Craft
The third wave treats coffee as artisan craft. Single-origin beans, pour-over, AeroPress (2005), cold brew. Baristas become skilled professionals. Direct trade with farmers. Counter Culture, Intelligentsia, and Stumptown lead the charge.
2020s — Now
$100B Market & Beyond
Specialty coffee market worth $100B+. Nitro cold brew, canned espresso, AI-optimized roasting, and precision fermentation push coffee into new territory. Coffee remains the world's second most traded commodity after oil.

Green Coffee Beans
Unroasted (green) coffee beans ready for the roaster — dense, grassy-scented, and pale. At this stage they can be stored for months without flavor loss; roasting starts the clock.
Freshly Roasted Beans
Freshly roasted coffee beans cooling on the drum — the Maillard reaction has transformed pale green seeds into aromatic, oil-sheened brown beans full of complex flavor compounds.

Latte Art
