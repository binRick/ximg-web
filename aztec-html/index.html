The Aztec Empire
Tenochtitlan
Founded 1325 · Island Capital
Built on an island in Lake Texcoco (modern Mexico City), Tenochtitlan was connected to the mainland by causeways. With 200,000+ people, it was larger than any European city of the time, featuring aqueducts, markets, and great temples.
Triple Alliance
1428 · Political Foundation
The Aztec Empire was actually a Triple Alliance of three city-states: Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tlacopan. Together they dominated central Mexico, collecting tribute from over 500 conquered cities.
Flower Wars
Ritual Conflict · Xochiyaoyotl
Staged battles between the Aztecs and neighboring states to capture (not kill) enemies for ritual sacrifice. Sacrificial offerings were believed to nourish the sun god Huitzilopochtli and prevent the destruction of the world.
Moctezuma II
1502–1520 · Last Great Emperor
Ruled at the height of Aztec power, controlling an empire of 5–6 million people. Met Hernán Cortés in 1519, initially mistaking him for the returning god Quetzalcoatl. Died during the Spanish conquest under disputed circumstances.
Chocolate & Agriculture
Cacao · Chinampas · Innovation
The Aztecs cultivated cacao for a bitter ceremonial drink (xocolatl). Their chinampas — floating garden islands in lakes — were engineering marvels producing multiple annual harvests of maize, beans, squash, and tomatoes.
Spanish Conquest
1519–1521 · Hernán Cortés
Cortés arrived with 600 men, but allied with the Aztecs' many enemies — Tlaxcalans and others. Combined with smallpox epidemics that killed millions, the alliance besieged and destroyed Tenochtitlan in 1521.
The Aztec Pantheon
Huitzilopochtli
God of Sun & War
The patron god of the Aztecs. Required daily nourishment through human sacrifice — the sun's movement across the sky depended on blood offerings. The Great Pyramid of Tenochtitlan was built in his honor.
Quetzalcoatl
The Feathered Serpent
God of wind, air, and learning. A benevolent deity associated with creation and knowledge. The legend of his return as a light-skinned god may have contributed to initial Aztec confusion about the arriving Spanish conquistadors.
Tlaloc
God of Rain & Agriculture
Worshipped since at least 200 BC across Mesoamerica. Required the sacrifice of children (whose tears would bring rain). Shared the Great Pyramid's summit shrine with Huitzilopochtli — agriculture and war equally sacred.
Coatlicue
Earth Mother Goddess
Goddess of life, death, and rebirth. Depicted wearing a skirt of writhing serpents and a necklace of hands and hearts. Mother of Huitzilopochtli, she represents the terrifying power of the natural world.
Xipe Totec
God of Seasons & Renewal
"Our Lord the Flayed One" — associated with the agricultural cycle. Priests wore the flayed skins of sacrificial victims to symbolize the earth shedding its husk for new growth in spring.
Tezcatlipoca
Smoking Mirror · God of Night
The dark counterpart to Quetzalcoatl. God of the night sky, sorcery, and destiny. Carried a smoking obsidian mirror that could reveal hidden truths — and the fates of the guilty.
Aztec Timeline
1111 AD
The Mexica Begin Their Migration
The Mexica people leave their mythical homeland of Aztlán on a divinely ordered migration, guided by their patron deity Huitzilopochtli, searching for the promised land.
1325 AD
Tenochtitlan Founded
The Mexica see the prophesied sign — an eagle eating a serpent on a cactus — on an island in Lake Texcoco. They found Tenochtitlan; this image becomes the Mexican national symbol.
1428 AD
Triple Alliance Formed
Itzcoatl forms the Triple Alliance with Texcoco and Tlacopan after defeating the dominant Tepanecs. The Aztec Empire begins rapid expansion through conquest and tribute collection.
1487 AD
Dedication of the Great Temple
The Templo Mayor in Tenochtitlan is rededicated with a massive sacrificial ceremony. Estimates suggest 20,000 prisoners of war were sacrificed over four days.
1519 AD
Cortés Arrives in Mexico
Hernán Cortés lands on the Mexican coast with 600 soldiers. He forms alliances with Aztec enemies and marches to Tenochtitlan, where Moctezuma II receives him with gifts.
1521 AD
Fall of Tenochtitlan
After an 80-day siege, Tenochtitlan falls on August 13, 1521. The last emperor Cuauhtémoc is captured. The Spanish raze the city and build Mexico City on its ruins.

Aztec Warriors
Jaguar
Jaguar Warriors (Ocēlōtl)
Elite warriors who wore jaguar skins and helmets shaped like the big cat's head. They captured enemies for ritual sacrifice rather than killing them outright — a prisoner's life had far greater spiritual value than a corpse on the battlefield.
Eagle
Eagle Warriors (Cuāuhtli)
The other great warrior society, wearing eagle feather costumes. Eagle warriors and Jaguar warriors were the two highest military orders, open only to those who had captured four or more enemy combatants in battle.
Cuachicqueh
Shorn Ones
The most feared warriors in the Aztec army — they shaved their heads except for a scalp-lock on the left side, and vowed never to retreat in battle. They fought at the front of every advance and were the last to leave a battlefield.
Weapons
Macuahuitl & Atlatl
The macuahuitl — a wooden sword lined with obsidian blades — could decapitate a horse. The atlatl dart-thrower gave warriors a missile weapon with devastating range. Obsidian, volcanic glass, was sharper than modern surgical steel.
Tenochtitlan — City of the Sun
Founded
Island City, 1325 AD
Founded on an island in Lake Texcoco after an eagle perched on a cactus eating a serpent — the sign promised by the god Huitzilopochtli. The Aztecs built causeways, aqueducts, and floating gardens (chinampas) to sustain a city of 200,000.
Templo Mayor
The Great Temple
The twin-pyramid temple dominated the city's ceremonial precinct. The left side honored Tlaloc (rain, agriculture) and the right side Huitzilopochtli (sun, war). Rebuilt seven times, each new layer entombing the last, its ruins lie beneath Mexico City Cathedral today.
Market
Tlatelolco Market
The adjacent city of Tlatelolco hosted the greatest market in the Americas. Spanish conquistadors marveled at its order — dedicated sections for gold, cloth, food, slaves, and medicines, overseen by judges who resolved disputes on the spot.
Aqueducts
Freshwater Engineering
Twin aqueducts ran from Chapultepec Hill to the island city, carrying fresh water to every neighborhood. While one was cleaned and repaired, the other continued flowing — a redundancy system that ensured the city never ran dry.
