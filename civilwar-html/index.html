American Civil War

A Nation Divided — Union vs. Confederacy, Slavery vs. Freedom
April 12, 1861 – April 9, 1865 · 620,000 American Dead
The Two Sides
🔵 Union (The North)
President: Abraham Lincoln
Commander: Ulysses S. Grant (from 1864)
Population: 22 million
Industry: 92% of US industrial output
Navy: Dominant naval force
Cause: Preserve the Union; end slavery
Soldiers: 2.1 million (including 180,000 Black troops)
Losses: ~360,000 killed
🔴 Confederacy (The South)
President: Jefferson Davis
Commander: Robert E. Lee (Army of Northern Virginia)
Population: 9 million (3.5M enslaved)
Economy: Agricultural, cotton-dependent
Strategy: Defensive war, foreign recognition
Cause: States' rights; preservation of slavery
Soldiers: ~750,000
Losses: ~260,000 killed
Key Battles
First Battle of Bull Run (1861)
Northern Virginia · Confederate Victory
The North expected a quick victory; civilians brought picnics to watch. Confederate forces (including Jackson "Stonewall") routed Union troops. The shock ended illusions of a short war. Both sides began serious military buildup.
Battle of Antietam (1862)
Bloodiest Single Day in American History
September 17, 1862: 22,717 casualties in one day — more than in the entire War of 1812. Technically a Union victory (Lee retreated). Lincoln used it to issue the Emancipation Proclamation, changing the war's moral dimension and preventing European intervention.
Battle of Gettysburg (1863)
The High Watermark of the Confederacy
The war's turning point in the East. Three days (July 1-3), 51,000 casualties. Pickett's Charge — 12,000 Confederates marching 400m across open field — was decimated. Lee never invaded the North again. Lincoln's Gettysburg Address followed in November.
Siege of Vicksburg (1863)
Grant Controls the Mississippi
Grant's 47-day siege captured the last Confederate stronghold on the Mississippi. The Confederacy was split in two; Grant emerged as the North's best general. Coming the day after Gettysburg (July 4), it was the war's true turning point.
Sherman's March to the Sea (1864)
Total War
General Sherman marched 60,000 troops from Atlanta to Savannah, deliberately destroying Confederate infrastructure and civilian morale. A 60-mile-wide swath of destruction. The first application of modern "total war" doctrine.
Appomattox (1865)
April 9, 1865 · The War Ends
Lee surrenders to Grant at Appomattox Court House. Grant's terms were generous: Confederate officers kept their sidearms; soldiers could keep horses. "The war is over. The rebels are our countrymen again." Lincoln was assassinated 6 days later.
Civil War Timeline
Nov 1860
Lincoln Elected
Abraham Lincoln wins the presidency without a single Southern electoral vote. South Carolina secedes within weeks. By February 1861, seven states have formed the Confederacy.
Apr 1861
Fort Sumter — War Begins
Confederate forces bombard Fort Sumter in Charleston Harbor. No one is killed in the bombardment, but the nation is at war. Lincoln calls for 75,000 volunteers; four more states secede.
Sep 1862
Emancipation Proclamation Issued
After Antietam, Lincoln issues the Emancipation Proclamation: as of January 1, 1863, enslaved people in Confederate states are declared free. The war becomes explicitly about slavery — preventing British/French recognition of the Confederacy.
Jul 1863
Gettysburg & Vicksburg
The double turning point. Gettysburg ends Confederate offensive capability in the East; Vicksburg splits the Confederacy and gives the Union control of the Mississippi. The South can no longer win — only fight on.
Nov 1863
Gettysburg Address
Lincoln dedicates the Gettysburg cemetery in 272 words. "Government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth." Redefines the war's purpose and America's democratic promise.
Apr 1865
Lee Surrenders at Appomattox
After Grant's relentless Overland Campaign, Lee has no option but surrender. The war ends. Lincoln is assassinated on April 14. Reconstruction begins — 13th Amendment abolishes slavery everywhere in the United States.

Gallery
Abraham Lincoln, 1863
Photograph by Anthony Berger. Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation transformed the Civil War into a crusade against slavery, preventing European intervention and cementing the Union's moral purpose.
Ulysses S. Grant, c. 1876
The Union's most effective general, Grant's relentless Overland Campaign and siege of Petersburg finally forced Lee's surrender. His generous terms at Appomattox helped begin national reconciliation.
Robert E. Lee
Commander of the Army of Northern Virginia, Lee's tactical genius kept the Confederacy fighting for four years against overwhelming Union odds. His surrender at Appomattox, April 9, 1865, effectively ended the war.
Battle of Gettysburg, July 1863
The Louisiana Tigers charging at Gettysburg. The three-day battle was the war's turning point — 51,000 casualties, Pickett's Charge repulsed, and Lee never invaded the North again.
Battle of Antietam, September 1862
The bloodiest single day in American history — 22,717 casualties. Lincoln used the Union's strategic victory to issue the Emancipation Proclamation, transforming the war's moral dimension.
Battle of Hampton Roads, 1862
The duel between USS Monitor and CSS Virginia (Merrimack) was the first battle between ironclad warships, rendering wooden warships obsolete and launching a new era of naval warfare.
Sherman's March to the Sea, 1864
Union troops foraging through Georgia. General Sherman's 60-mile-wide swath of destruction from Atlanta to Savannah was the first modern application of "total war," targeting civilian infrastructure and morale.
Captured Confederate Battle Flags
Battle flags captured by Union forces during the war. Each flag represented a Confederate regiment — their capture in battle was a significant honor, and their recovery after the war was a symbol of reunification.
