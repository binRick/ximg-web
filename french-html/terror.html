Months of Terror
Sep 1792
September Massacres
As Prussian armies advanced on Paris, panicked mobs stormed the city's prisons and killed between 1,100 and 1,400 prisoners over four days, fearing they would be freed to join the enemy. The massacres shocked Europe and revealed the violent potential coiled beneath the revolutionary surface.
Jan 1793
Louis XVI Executed
Louis Capet — stripped of his royal title — was guillotined in the Place de la Révolution before a silent crowd. His final words: "I die innocent of all the crimes attributed to me. I forgive those who are guilty of my death." The execution of a king horrified the monarchies of Europe and widened the war.
Jul 1793
Committee of Public Safety
The Committee of Public Safety was reorganized as a twelve-man war cabinet effectively governing France. Robespierre joined in late July and soon dominated it. The Law of Suspects (September 1793) allowed arrest of anyone whose "conduct, associations, talk, or writings" showed them to be enemies of liberty — creating the legal machinery of the Terror.
Oct 1793
Marie Antoinette Executed
After a show trial in which she was accused of treason, counter-revolutionary conspiracy, and worse, the former queen was guillotined on October 16, 1793. She faced her death with remarkable composure, apologizing to the executioner after accidentally stepping on his foot: "Monsieur, I beg your pardon. I did not do it on purpose."
Apr 1794
Danton Executed
Georges Danton, having called publicly for an end to the Terror's excesses, was arrested and accused of treason. His trial was a farce — he was refused the right to call witnesses and the tribunal voted to silence him mid-defense. Guillotined April 5, 1794. His last words to the executioner: "Show my head to the people — it is worth seeing." Even the revolution's founders were not safe.
Jul 1794
Thermidorian Reaction — The Terror Ends
On 9 Thermidor Year II (July 27, 1794), members of the National Convention, fearing they were next, arrested Robespierre during his own speech. The Convention shouted him down as he tried to speak. He was guillotined the next day along with Saint-Just and other allies. The Terror was over. The survivors who ended it were not idealists — they were men who knew their own names were on Robespierre's list.
