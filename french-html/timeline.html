Timeline of the French Revolution
May 1789
Estates-General Convenes
For the first time since 1614, Louis XVI summoned the Estates-General to Versailles to address the financial crisis. The Third Estate immediately disputed voting procedures — each estate had one vote, meaning the privileged two could always outvote the ninety-seven percent. The confrontation over voting rules became a confrontation over sovereignty itself.
Jun 1789
Third Estate Forms National Assembly
Locked out of their meeting hall, the Third Estate deputies moved to the royal tennis court and swore not to disperse until France had a constitution. The Tennis Court Oath was an act of open defiance against the crown. The king capitulated, ordering all three estates to meet together — the Old Regime had cracked.
Jul 14, 1789
Storming of the Bastille
Parisians, fearing a royal crackdown, stormed the Bastille fortress — symbol of royal tyranny — finding only seven prisoners inside. The governor's head was paraded through the streets on a pike. Louis wrote in his diary that day: "Nothing." The date became the symbolic beginning of the Revolution and is still celebrated as France's national day.
Aug 1789
Declaration of the Rights of Man
The National Assembly adopted the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen — seventeen articles proclaiming that sovereignty resided in the nation, not the king; that men were born free and equal; and that the purpose of government was to protect liberty, property, and security. Inspired by Enlightenment philosophy and the American Declaration of Independence, it became one of history's most influential documents.
1791
Constitutional Monarchy Established
France adopted its first written constitution, transforming the absolute monarchy into a constitutional one. The king retained executive power but the Legislative Assembly controlled laws and finances. The experiment lasted barely a year — Louis's flight to Varennes and France's declaration of war on Austria destroyed trust in the constitutional order.
Sep 1792
First French Republic Declared
The newly elected National Convention abolished the monarchy and declared France a Republic. The revolutionary calendar was adopted — Year I began September 22, 1792. France was now a republic surrounded by hostile monarchies, fighting for its survival on multiple fronts simultaneously.
Jan 1793
Louis XVI Executed
The King of France was guillotined in the Place de la Révolution. The vote to execute him passed by a single vote in the Convention — 361 to 360. His execution shocked every monarchy in Europe and broadened the war coalition against France, but it was irreversible: France could not go back to monarchy without convicting itself of murder.
1793–94
Reign of Terror
The Committee of Public Safety under Robespierre's effective leadership conducted a mass purge of perceived enemies of the Revolution. The Revolutionary Tribunal sent thousands to the guillotine. The Terror's victims were not only aristocrats — they included Girondin deputies, priests, moderate republicans, and eventually the Terror's own architects. France was in a state of emergency, and emergency justified everything.
Jul 27, 1794
Thermidorian Reaction Ends Terror
Convention members who feared Robespierre's purges were coming for them organized a coup. Robespierre was arrested on the Convention floor and guillotined the next day. The surviving Thermidorians dismantled the Terror machinery, released political prisoners, and prosecuted the most extreme sans-culottes. The Revolution entered a more conservative phase.
1795
Directory Takes Power
A new constitution created the Directory — a five-man executive body paired with a bicameral legislature. It was deeply corrupt, chronically unstable, and lurched from crisis to crisis while fighting wars across Europe. Its one consistent achievement was producing capable military commanders. Its most brilliant general was a 26-year-old Corsican named Bonaparte.
Nov 9, 1799
Napoleon's Coup — 18 Brumaire
Napoleon Bonaparte, the most celebrated general in France, returned from Egypt and staged a coup against the Directory with his brother Lucien and the Abbé Sieyès. On 18 Brumaire Year VIII, he dissolved the councils at bayonet-point and declared himself First Consul. Most historians mark this as the end of the Revolution and the beginning of the Napoleonic era.
1804
Napoleon Crowned Emperor
Napoleon crowned himself Emperor of the French in Notre-Dame Cathedral, famously taking the crown from Pope Pius VII and placing it on his own head — making clear that his power came from himself, not the Church. The Revolution had begun by abolishing monarchy; it ended by creating a new one. But the ideas it unleashed — popular sovereignty, legal equality, nationalism — could not be uncrowned.
