Monostable Operation:
Triggered by a LOW pulse on pin 2 (TRIGGER). Output goes HIGH for t = 1.1×R×C seconds, then returns LOW. Useful for debouncing, pulse stretching, and time delays.

History of the 555 Timer

Hans Camenzind (1934–2012), inventor of the 555 timer — "Wizard of Analog"

Hans R. Camenzind was a Swiss-American electronics engineer who designed the 555 timer while working as a contractor for Signetics in 1971. His goal was to create a general-purpose, inexpensive timing IC. The design took about a year and was done entirely by hand — no CAD tools existed at the time. He later wrote the book Designing Analog Chips, available free online, which became a classic reference.

NE555 in DIP-8 (left) and SOIC-8 (right) packages — both still in production today

The 555 timer was introduced as the Signetics NE555 in 1972. The name comes from the three 5 kΩ resistors that form the internal voltage divider. It was an instant success: cheap, robust, and able to run from 5V to 15V. Today it is produced by dozens of manufacturers and over a billion units are sold annually.

Timeline

  • 1971Hans Camenzind designs the 555 timer under contract for Signetics. The entire IC is laid out by hand on a large sheet of mylar film.
  • 1972Signetics introduces the NE555 commercially. Initial price: ~$0.75. The "555" name reflects three internal 5 kΩ resistors setting voltage comparator thresholds at ⅓ Vcc and ⅔ Vcc.
  • 1973The NE556 (dual 555) debuts, placing two independent timers in a single 14-pin DIP package.
  • 1975The NE558 quad timer is released. Multiple manufacturers begin second-sourcing the NE555, including Texas Instruments (LM555) and National Semiconductor.
  • 1978Intersil releases the ICM7555, the first CMOS version. It operates from as little as 2V and draws only 60 μA quiescent current vs. ~3 mA for bipolar, enabling battery-powered applications.
  • 1987Signetics is acquired by Philips Semiconductors. The NE555 line continues unchanged. By this point the chip costs under $0.20 in quantity.
  • 1994Texas Instruments releases the TLC555, a LinCMOS variant with rail-to-rail output swing and operation down to 1V, targeting low-voltage embedded systems.
  • 2000sThe 555 becomes a staple of hobbyist electronics. Popularised by Arduino culture and maker communities, it appears in countless tutorials, kits, and introductory circuits.
  • 2011On the chip's 40th anniversary, Maker Faire holds "555 Fest" — a competition to build the largest array of 555 timers. The winning entry used 10,000 chips wired in parallel.
  • 2012Hans Camenzind passes away at age 78. The IEEE hails the 555 as one of the most influential integrated circuits ever designed. Annual production exceeds one billion units.
  • 2024The NE555 is still in full production at Texas Instruments, STMicroelectronics, ON Semiconductor, and many others — over 50 years after its introduction.
