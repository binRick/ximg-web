Ales, lagers, stouts, and IPAs — a guide to the world's favorite fermented grain

Ales (Top-Fermented)

Ales use Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast that ferments at warmer temperatures (60–75°F) at the top of the fermentation vessel. Generally ready faster than lagers. Wide flavor diversity.

Pale Ale

The foundation of the ale world. Balanced malt and hop character. American Pale Ale (Sierra Nevada Pale Ale is the archetype): citrusy Cascade hops, clean bitterness, amber color. English Pale Ale: more malt-forward, lower bitterness, biscuity. ABV: 4.5–6.2%. Approachable and food-friendly. The base style from which IPA evolved.

4.5–6.2% ABVBalancedCascade hops

Amber Ale

Malt-forward with caramel and toasty notes from crystal and caramel malts. Reddish-amber color from those specialty malts. Moderate hop bitterness for balance. American versions: more hop-forward. Irish Red Ale: very malt-dominated, low bitterness, creamy. Fat Tire (New Belgium) is the American craft benchmark. ABV: 4.5–6.2%.

4.5–6.2% ABVCaramel maltAmber color

Brown Ale

English origin. Roasted malt flavors: chocolate, toffee, nutty, coffee. Two sub-styles: Northern English (Newcastle Brown Ale) — lighter, drier, nuttier. Southern English — sweeter, more chocolate. American Brown Ale: hoppier and stronger than English versions. Low to medium bitterness. ABV: 4–5.5%. Approachable, sessionable, and deeply malty.

4–5.5% ABVChocolate/nuttyNewcastle style

Barleywine

The biggest of the ales — named for its wine-like ABV (8–14%). English Barleywine: rich, caramel, dried fruit, toffee, moderate hops. American Barleywine: aggressively hopped, piney or citrusy. Strong enough to age 5–10 years like wine. Sip slowly; often served in 8oz portions. Sierra Nevada Bigfoot (American Barleywine) and Fuller's Vintage Ale (English) are benchmarks. Winter seasonal.

8–14% ABVAges like wineRich + complex

Saison / Farmhouse Ale

Belgian farmhouse ale historically brewed in winter for summer farm laborers. Dry, refreshing, spicy (from phenolic yeast character), often fruity, moderate bitterness. Complex yeast character — pepper, citrus, earthiness. Belgian saison yeast is the key. Dupont Saison is the classic reference. American craft saisons often add adjuncts (spices, local ingredients). ABV: 5–8%.

5–8% ABVSpicy phenolsFarmhouse yeast

Trappist & Abbey Ales

Brewed at Trappist monasteries (or in abbey style). Belgian Trappist producers: Westvleteren (arguably world's best beer), Rochefort, Westmalle, Chimay, Orval. Styles: Dubbel (7–8%, dark fruit, caramel), Tripel (8–10%, golden, spicy, dry), Quadrupel (10–12%+, darkest, richest, raisin/plum). Yeast character from abbey strains is defining. Non-Trappist versions use the style.

7–12% ABVDubbel/Tripel/QuadWestvleteren

Sour / Wild Ales

Fermented with wild yeast and bacteria: Brettanomyces (barnyard, leather, funky), Lactobacillus (lactic sourness), Pediococcus (intense sourness), Acetobacter (acidic). Belgian Lambic (spontaneous fermentation): Gueuze (blended, champagne-like), Kriek (cherry), Framboise (raspberry). Flanders Red Ale (Rodenbach): complex sour-sweet oak-aged. Berliner Weisse: light and sour. American Wild Ales explore this space.

BrettanomycesLambic/GueuzeSpontaneous ferment

Kölsch

Only brewed in Cologne, Germany — legally protected like Champagne. Fermented with ale yeast but conditioned cold like a lager (hence "hybrid"). Pale golden, very clean, delicate hop bitterness, subtle fruitiness from yeast. Light and supremely drinkable. Served in slim 200ml Stange glasses that require constant refilling by the Köbes (waiter). ABV: 4.4–5.2%. Summer's perfect beer.

4.4–5.2% ABVCologne-onlyHybrid style

Lagers (Bottom-Fermented)

Lagers use Saccharomyces pastorianus yeast that ferments cold (34–50°F) at the bottom of the vessel. Cleaner flavor profile than ales — no fruity or spicy yeast esters. Conditioning at cold temperature for weeks to months ("lagering") is required. Account for ~90% of all beer sold globally.

Czech Pilsner (Bohemian)

The original pilsner, invented in Plzeň (Pilsen) in 1842 — the first clear, pale, golden lager. Pilsner Urquell is the benchmark and still uses open fermenters and traditional triple decoction mashing. Spicy, herbal Saaz hop character, rich soft-water malt, substantial bitterness with a smooth finish. ABV: 4.4–5.1%. Served with a thick creamy foam (mlíko — "milk" pour). Extraordinary depth for a "simple" beer.

4.4–5.1% ABVSaaz hopsPilsner Urquell

German Pils

Drier and more bitter than Czech Pilsner, influenced by harder water. Noble hop aroma and firm bitterness with a clean, dry finish. Light body. Bitburger and Warsteiner are mass-market examples; craft German Pils are more flavorful. IBUs 25–45 — noticeable bitterness. ABV: 4.4–5.2%. North German Pils tends to be drier and more bitter; South German slightly softer.

Dry + bitterNoble hopsBitburger

Märzen / Oktoberfest

Traditionally brewed in March (März), lagered through summer, and released at Oktoberfest in September. Amber to deep copper color. Rich, toasty malt, clean, medium-high ABV (5.8–6.3%). The original Oktoberfest style before modern "festbiers" (pale lagers) replaced it at the Munich fest. Spaten Märzen is the traditional reference. American craft versions embrace the traditional amber style.

5.8–6.3% ABVAmber/toastySpaten Märzen

Bock & Doppelbock

Bock: strong lager (6–7.5% ABV). Malty, toasty, low bitterness. Originally from Einbeck, Germany — Bavarian mispronunciation gave the name. Maibock (Helles Bock): pale spring release. Doppelbock: even stronger (7–12% ABV), intensely malty, rich, dark fruit. Munich monastery Paulaner's Salvator ("savior") gave Doppelbocks their -ator suffix tradition. Eisbock: frozen to concentrate further — 9–14% ABV. Liquid bread.

Bock 6–7.5%Doppelbock 7–12%Salvator

Munich Helles

Bavaria's response to the Czech Pilsner — a soft, malt-forward pale lager with restrained hops. "Helles" means bright/light in German. Clean, slightly sweet malt character with soft floral hop aroma. The everyday beer of Munich's beer halls and gardens. Spaten Hell and Augustiner Helles are revered examples. ABV: 4.7–5.4%. Approachable but surprisingly nuanced. The default at the Hofbräuhaus.

4.7–5.4% ABVSoft maltBeer garden beer

Dunkel & Schwarzbier

Dunkel (dark lager): Munich-style dark lager. Chocolate, caramel, bread crust malt with clean lager fermentation. No roastiness — uses Munich malt, not black malt. ABV: 4.5–5.6%. Augustiner Dunkel is the gold standard. Schwarzbier (black lager): even darker, uses a small amount of black malt for subtle roast — like a lager-version of a dark beer. Köstritzer Schwarzbier is the classic. Both are dangerously drinkable.

4.5–5.6% ABVClean dark maltNo harsh roast

Stouts & Porters

Irish Dry Stout

Guinness Draught is the iconic example. Roasted barley (unmalted) gives the characteristic coffee/chocolate bitterness and almost black color. Nitrogen dispense (mix of N₂ and CO₂) creates the famous creamy, tight-bubbled head and cascading pour. Dry, bitter finish. Surprisingly low ABV: 4.2%. The "perfect pint" takes 119.5 seconds according to Guinness — two-stage pour with a 3/4 settle. Medium body despite the intimidating color.

4.2% ABVNitrogen pourRoasted barley

Milk Stout

Lactose (milk sugar) added to the boil — yeast cannot ferment lactose, leaving residual sweetness and creamy body. Chocolate, coffee, and vanilla notes with a gentle sweetness that smooths the roasted bitterness. Left Hand Milk Stout (with nitro) is the American benchmark. Original Mackeson's from England is the classic. ABV: 4.0–6.0%. The sweetness makes it more approachable for those who find dry stout too bitter.

Lactose = sweet4–6% ABVLeft Hand benchmark

Oatmeal Stout

Oats (typically 5–15% of grain bill) add silky, full body and smooth creaminess without much sweetness. The beta-glucans in oats create a velvety mouthfeel. Less sweet than milk stout. Chocolate, coffee, and earthy oat flavors. Samuel Smith's Oatmeal Stout is the classic English example. A style that nearly disappeared before craft beer revival. ABV: 4.2–5.9%. Rich without being heavy.

Oat smoothness4.2–5.9% ABVSamuel Smith

Imperial / Russian Imperial Stout

The biggest stout — historically brewed for export to the Russian Imperial court (hence the name). High gravity gives 8–12%+ ABV. Intense roasted malt, dark chocolate, espresso, molasses, dried fruit. Ages remarkably well — 5–10 years in bourbon barrels (bourbon barrel-aged RIS is a craft staple). Founders KBS, Goose Island Bourbon County Brand Stout, and Parabola are annual highly-anticipated releases. Sip like wine.

8–12%+ ABVBourbon barrel agedIntense + complex

Porter

London's working-class beer — historically England's most popular beer from 1720s through early 1800s. Made with brown malt and later pale malt + black patent malt. Chocolate, caramel, light roast, toffee. Less bitter and lighter-bodied than stout. Fuller's London Porter is the benchmark. Baltic Porter uses lager yeast at cold temperatures for a smooth, rich take. ABV: 4.5–7.5%. The ancestor of stout.

4.5–7.5% ABVFuller's London PorterLighter than stout

Baltic Porter

A strong, smooth porter fermented with lager yeast at cold temperatures. Developed in Baltic countries — Poland, Finland, Estonia — that received English porter exports in the 18th century and started making their own. ABV: 6.5–9.5%. Rich dark fruit, chocolate, molasses, smooth and creamy. No harsh roastiness. Sinebrychoff Porter (Finland) and Żywiec Porter (Poland) are classic examples. An underappreciated style.

6.5–9.5% ABVLager yeastDark fruit + smooth

IPAs — India Pale Ales

Originally brewed extra-hoppy for preservation on the voyage to British India. The dominant American craft beer style since the 2000s. Hops provide bitterness (alpha acids), aroma (essential oils), and can create dramatically different flavor profiles depending on variety, hopping method, and timing.

West Coast IPA

The original American IPA style. Clear, dry, bitter, and resinous. High IBU (International Bitterness Units) — 55–80+. Pine, citrus, resin from classic American hops (Centennial, Simcoe, Chinook). Dry hopping adds aroma. The bitterness is clean and assertive. Ballast Point Sculpin, Stone IPA, Pliny the Elder (Russian River) are benchmarks. Dry finish from low final gravity. Refreshing despite the bitterness.

60–80 IBUPine + resinPliny the Elder

New England / Hazy IPA (NEIPA)

The 21st century's most influential beer innovation. Soft, pillowy mouthfeel from oats and wheat in the grain bill. Intentionally hazy/unfiltered. Hop-forward but low bitterness (IBU 30–50) — all about tropical aroma: mango, passionfruit, papaya, pineapple, stone fruit. Massive dry-hop charges post-fermentation. The Alchemist Heady Topper and Bissell Brothers are the archetypes. Drink fresh — deteriorates rapidly.

30–50 IBUTropical aromaDrink fresh

Session IPA

IPA character at session strength — under 4.5% ABV. The challenge: keep the hop aroma and flavor while reducing alcohol and the accompanying malt body. Dry hopping and hop-forward techniques achieve IPA character without the weight. Founders All Day IPA (4.7%) was the pioneer. Oskar Blues Pinner (4.9%) is another benchmark. Great for multiple pints without impairment. IBU 40–60.

Under 4.5% ABVSession strengthAll Day IPA

Double / Imperial IPA (DIPA)

Everything dialed up: more hops (bitterness and aroma), more malt to balance, more alcohol (7.5–10%+ ABV). West Coast DIPA: piney, resinous, brutally bitter. NEIPA DIPA: softer with intense tropical fruit. Russian River Pliny the Elder (8% — technically a DIPA) inspired a generation of craft brewers. Often dry-hopped multiple times. Complex and potent — typically served in 10oz pours. Avoid with food; savor alone.

7.5–10%+ ABVPliny the ElderIntense hops

Black IPA

Oxymoronic but real: dark color from de-husked black malt (removes harsh roastiness) with IPA hop character. The roast and dark malt adds background coffee/chocolate notes that meld surprisingly well with citrus and pine hops. Born in Vermont (Greg Noonan at Vermont Pub and Brewery) in the 1990s. Cascazilla (Ithaca Beer Co.) is a classic. ABV: 6–8.5%. A niche but satisfying style for those who love both stout and IPA.

6–8.5% ABVDe-husked black maltRoast + hops

Brut IPA

Born in San Francisco in 2018. Uses amyloglucosidase enzyme (the same enzyme in Champagne production) during fermentation to break down all fermentable sugars to near zero — creating bone-dry, effervescent, Champagne-like carbonation. Very dry, very aromatic, very low sweetness. Light body. IBU often low (20–30) — all aroma, no bitterness. A style that rose and fell quickly but pioneered the "dry and aromatic" direction in craft beer.

Bone dryChampagne-likeEnzyme fermentation

Wheat Beers

Hefeweizen (German)

Bavarian wheat beer — unfiltered ("Hefe" = yeast, "Weizen" = wheat). 50%+ wheat in the grain bill. The Weizen yeast strain produces isoamyl acetate (banana) and 4-vinyl guaiacol (clove) — signature aromas. Fermentation temperature determines ratio: cooler = more clove, warmer = more banana. Light, hazy, refreshing. ABV: 4.5–5.5%. Serve in a tall, curved Weizen glass. Paulaner and Weihenstephaner are the standard-bearers. Never served with lemon (a German pub faux pas).

Banana + clove yeast4.5–5.5% ABVWeihenstephaner

Kristallweizen

Filtered version of Hefeweizen — the yeast is removed, producing a clear, golden beer. The filtration removes most of the yeast character (banana, clove) and body. Crisper, cleaner, and lighter than Hefeweizen. Less popular than Hefe in Germany but appreciated by those who prefer clarity. Also called Kristall Weizen. The same base beer as Hefeweizen with dramatically different presentation and flavor.

FilteredClear / cleanLighter than Hefe

Dunkelweizen

Dark wheat beer — Hefeweizen made with Munich and/or chocolate malts. Deep amber to dark brown. The banana and clove yeast character persists alongside caramel, chocolate, and bread crust malt flavors. A beautiful and underappreciated style. Ayinger Ur-Weisse and Schneider Weisse Original are excellent examples. ABV: 4.5–5.6%. Richer and more complex than standard Hefeweizen; still refreshingly drinkable.

Dark wheatBanana + chocolateAyinger Ur-Weisse

Witbier / Bière Blanche (Belgian)

Belgian white beer spiced with coriander and Curaçao orange peel — the defining characteristic that separates it from German Hefeweizen. Unfiltered, hazy pale straw color. Light, refreshing, dry. Pierre Celis revived the style in the 1960s; Hoegaarden remains the accessible reference. Blue Moon (USA) is a witbier with orange slice garnish. ABV: 4.5–5.5%. Quintessential summer beer. Fruity, spicy, refreshing.

Coriander + orange peel4.5–5.5% ABVHoegaarden

Berliner Weisse

Berlin's "White Beer of the North" — a historically significant, very sour, very low-alcohol wheat beer (2.5–3.5% ABV). Lactic acid sourness from Lactobacillus fermentation. Traditionally served with a shot of raspberry (rot) or woodruff (grün) syrup to balance the sourness. Nearly extinct in Berlin itself but widely brewed by American craft brewers. A refreshing, sessionable sour. Berliner Kindl Weisse is the surviving commercial example.

2.5–3.5% ABVVery sourSyrup served alongside

Gose

Ancient German wheat beer from Goslar with a unique character: brewed with coriander and a significant addition of salt (sodium chloride). The salt enhances hop aromas, rounds out the flavor, and gives a distinctive saline finish. Low ABV (4–5%), tart (Lactobacillus fermentation), and refreshing. Nearly extinct by the 20th century; revived by craft brewers and now widely available. Often flavored with fruit in American craft versions — mango gose, tart cherry gose.

Salted + sour4–5% ABVCoriander

The Brewing Process

1

Malting

Barley (or other grains) is steeped in water, allowed to germinate (which activates enzymes), then kilned (dried). Kilning temperature determines malt color and flavor: pale malt (170°F), crystal/caramel malt (240°F), chocolate malt (375°F), black patent malt (480°F). The Maillard reaction during kilning creates the flavor compounds. Brewers purchase pre-malted grain; maltsters do this step.

2

Mashing

Crushed malt (grist) is mixed with hot water (148–158°F) in the mash tun for 60 minutes. Alpha and beta amylase enzymes (developed during malting) convert starches to fermentable sugars (maltose, glucose) and unfermentable dextrins. Mash temperature affects body: lower temp (148°F) = more fermentable, drier, thinner beer; higher temp (158°F) = more dextrins, fuller body. The result is the "mash" — grain porridge in sweet liquid.

3

Lautering

Separating the sweet wort from spent grain. The grain bed itself acts as a filter. Sparging (rinsing with hot water) extracts remaining sugars from the grain. The lauter tun has a false bottom with slotted plates. Runoff wort is collected in the kettle. Efficiency: how much sugar you extract from grain. 65–75% efficiency is typical for homebrewers. Commercial breweries achieve 95%+. Spent grain goes to animal feed or composting.

4

Boiling & Hopping

Wort boiled 60–90 minutes. Serves multiple purposes: sterilization, alpha acid isomerization (bittering), evaporation of DMS (cooked corn off-flavor), protein coagulation (hot break). Hop additions: bittering hops (60 min remaining) — alpha acids isomerize to iso-alpha acids for bitterness. Flavor hops (15–20 min): aromatic oils that survive partially. Aroma hops (0 min / whirlpool): essential oils preserved at lower temp. Dry hops: added cold post-fermentation — maximum aroma.

5

Fermentation

Cooled wort transferred to fermenter, yeast pitched. Yeast consumes sugars, producing ethanol + CO₂. Ales: 60–75°F, 5–14 days. Lagers: 34–50°F, 2–6 weeks. Primary fermentation (most vigorous, 3–5 days) is followed by secondary conditioning (residual sugars consumed, flavors clean up). Closed fermentation vessel; airlock allows CO₂ escape. Pitching rate (amount of yeast) and yeast health dramatically affect flavor.

6

Conditioning & Packaging

After primary fermentation, beer is cold-conditioned (lagering for lagers; crash cooling for ales) to improve clarity and clean up off-flavors. Dry hopping happens here. Finings (Irish moss, gelatin) may be added to clear the beer. Filtering or centrifugation for commercial clarity. Packaging: bottles (primed with sugar for natural carbonation), kegs (force-carbonated with CO₂), or cans. Pasteurization or sterile filtration extends shelf life for distribution.

7

Water Chemistry

Water minerals dramatically affect beer style. Sulfate (SO₄²⁻): enhances hop bitterness and dryness — Burton-on-Trent's sulfate-rich water made it ideal for pale ales. Chloride (Cl⁻): enhances malt sweetness and fullness — ideal for stouts, porters, NEIPA. Calcium improves enzyme activity and yeast health. Carbonate: buffers pH, historically used for dark beers. Modern brewers adjust water chemistry from scratch using RO water, adding minerals for each style.

Beer & Food Pairing

Cheese

Imperial Stout + aged Cheddar: roasted bitterness and caramel malt complement the sharpness. Witbier + Brie or Camembert: the fruity, spiced wheat beer cuts through creamy fat. Hefeweizen + fresh goat cheese: banana/clove yeast with acidic chèvre. IPA + blue cheese: bitterness tames the funk. Dubbel + Gouda: the dried fruit and caramel complexity match. Beer's carbonation scrubs fat from the palate better than most wines.

Stout + CheddarWit + BrieIPA + blue

Burgers & Grilled Meat

IPA: the hop bitterness cuts through fat, and citrus notes complement charred meat. Amber Ale: malt caramel mirrors browned meat. Märzen: the toasty, malt-forward character is why Oktoberfest (and by extension, bratwurst) exists. Imperial Stout with a slow-smoked brisket — intensity matches intensity. For pulled pork: smoked porter or a robust brown ale. The Maillard reaction in cooked meat mirrors that in roasted malt.

IPA with burgersMärzen = bratwurstStout + brisket

Pizza

Amber Ale's malt caramel complements tomato sauce sweetness. IPA cuts through cheese richness. Spicy pepperoni pizza: hefeweizen's banana and low bitterness cools the heat. Margherita: pilsner's clean crispness. White pizza (no tomato): saison's spice and dryness. The hop oils in IPA have antibacterial properties that explain why hoppy beer and fatty food seem evolutionarily well-matched. Almost any beer works with pizza.

Amber + tomato sauceSpicy = hefeweizenMargherita = pilsner

Seafood & Fish

Oysters: dry Irish Stout (Guinness) — the classic Irish pub combination. The stout's roasted dryness and the oyster's brine are legendary together. Pilsner with fried fish or fish tacos — crisp carbonation and light bitterness. Witbier with mussels steamed in white wine: Belgian beer, Belgian moules-frites tradition. Saison with grilled salmon. Berliner Weisse with ceviche — the sourness enhances citrus in the dish.

Stout + oystersPilsner + fried fishWit + mussels

Chocolate & Dessert

Imperial Stout with dark chocolate cake or chocolate truffles — chocolate with chocolate, amplifying both. Milk Stout with vanilla ice cream: pour the stout over ice cream (a "Black Cow" float). Oatmeal Stout with brownies. Raspberry Lambic or Framboise with a fruit tart. Cherry Kriek with dark chocolate. Banana desserts with Hefeweizen (banana + banana). Doppelbock with tiramisu — both rich, both featuring coffee/chocolate.

Stout + chocolateMilk stout floatKriek + fruit tart

Spicy Food

Hefeweizen is the classic spicy food pairing — the low bitterness and fruity wheat character doesn't amplify heat, while CO₂ provides refreshment. American Wheat Ale. Lager (Mexican lager with lime): the clean, light body and gentle carbonation complement heat without adding bitterness intensity. Avoid: hop-forward IPAs and high-alcohol barleywines with very spicy food — both amplify the burn. Mango Gose has the acidic, saline, fruity character for Thai and Mexican cuisine.

Hefeweizen = bestLager + limeAvoid IPAs
