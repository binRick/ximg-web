Beer

Ales, lagers, stouts, and IPAs — a guide to the world's most popular fermented grain beverage

Ales — Top-Fermented Beers

Pale Ale
ABV: 4–6% · Origin: England
Hoppy, light-to-medium body. Pioneered the hop-forward style. American pale ales use more citrus-forward Pacific Northwest hops.
HoppyAmber
Amber Ale
ABV: 4.5–6% · Origin: USA
Caramel malt sweetness balanced with moderate hops. Medium body, reddish-amber color. Fat Tire is the classic example.
MaltyBalanced
Brown Ale
ABV: 4–6% · Origin: England
Nutty, chocolaty malt character. Newcastle Brown Ale is the iconic example. American versions trend hoppier.
NuttyMalty
Barleywine
ABV: 8–12% · Origin: England
Very strong ale. Rich caramel, toffee, dried fruit. English style is sweeter; American style is more hop-bitter.
StrongRich
Saison / Farmhouse
ABV: 5–8% · Origin: Belgium
Dry, spicy, fruity from Belgian yeast. Originally brewed for farm workers in spring. Refreshing and complex.
SpicyFruity
Trappist / Abbey Ale
ABV: 6–12% · Origin: Belgium
Brewed by or under license from Trappist monasteries. Rochefort, Westmalle, Chimay, Orval. Complex and bottle-conditioned.
ComplexBelgian
Sour / Wild Ale
ABV: 3–8% · Origin: Belgium
Tart and funky from wild yeast (Brettanomyces) or bacteria. Lambic, Gueuze, Kriek (cherry lambic), Flanders Red Ale.
TartFunky
Kölsch
ABV: 4.4–5% · Origin: Cologne, Germany
Light, crisp, subtly fruity ale fermented cold — like a lager. Protected designation; officially only brewed in Cologne.
CrispLight

Lagers — Bottom-Fermented Beers

Czech Pilsner
ABV: 4–5% · Origin: Plzeň, Czech Republic
Pilsner Urquell invented the style in 1842. Soft water, Saaz hops, rich golden color, dense white head. Hoppy for a lager.
HoppyGolden
German Pilsner
ABV: 4.5–5% · Origin: Germany
Drier, crisper than Czech. More bitter. Bitburger, Beck's, Warsteiner. Uses harder water which accentuates bitterness.
CrispBitter
Munich Helles
ABV: 4.7–5.4% · Origin: Munich, Germany
Soft, malty, low bitterness. Created in 1894 by Spaten to compete with pilsner. The everyday beer of Bavaria.
MaltySoft
Märzen / Oktoberfest
ABV: 5–6% · Origin: Bavaria, Germany
Traditional Oktoberfest beer. Amber, toasty malt, moderate hops. Brewed in March, lagered through summer, released in fall.
ToastyAmber
Bock
ABV: 6–7% · Origin: Einbeck, Germany
Strong, malty lager. Dark amber color, minimal hops. Maibock (pale, spring), Doppelbock (very strong, 7–12%), Eisbock.
StrongMalty
Dunkel
ABV: 4.5–5.6% · Origin: Bavaria, Germany
Dark lager. Chocolate and bread malt character, low hops. The traditional Bavarian lager before pilsner took over.
DarkChocolaty
Schwarzbier
ABV: 4.4–5.4% · Origin: Thuringia, Germany
Black lager. Roasted malt but stays crisp and dry — not heavy. Less roasty than stout. The dark side of lager.
DarkCrisp
American Adjunct Lager
ABV: 4–5% · Origin: USA
Budweiser, Coors, Miller. Uses corn or rice alongside barley for a lighter body and clean finish. Globally the bestselling style.
LightCrisp

Stouts & Porters

Irish Dry Stout
ABV: 4–5% · Icon: Guinness
Roasted barley gives coffee/dark chocolate flavor. Dry finish. Surprisingly light body. Nitrogen dispense creates the famous creamy cascade.
Milk Stout
ABV: 4–6%
Lactose (milk sugar) added for sweetness and body. Creamy, chocolaty, less bitter. Left Hand Milk Stout is a classic.
Oatmeal Stout
ABV: 4.5–6%
Oats add silky, smooth body. Coffee and chocolate flavors. Richer mouthfeel than dry stout.
Imperial / Russian Imperial Stout
ABV: 8–14%
Originally brewed for the Russian Imperial Court. Very strong, rich, complex — dark fruit, chocolate, roasted coffee. Great for aging.
Porter
ABV: 4–6.5% · Origin: London, 1700s
Precursor to stout. Brown malt base, chocolate and caramel notes. Robust Porter is hoppier and darker.
Baltic Porter
ABV: 6.5–9.5%
Strong porter, lagered cold. Dark fruit, licorice, chocolate. Popular in Poland, Finland, and the Baltic states.
Pastry Stout
ABV: 8–14%
Modern style loaded with adjuncts — vanilla, chocolate, coconut, coffee, caramel. Very sweet and dessert-like.

IPA — India Pale Ale

West Coast IPA
Origin: California, 1990s
Clear, dry, bitter. Pine and citrus from West Coast hops (Cascade, Centennial, Chinook). Firm malt backbone. Ballast Point Sculpin is iconic.
New England / Hazy IPA
Origin: Vermont, 2010s
Opaque, juicy, soft. Low bitterness despite high hop content. Tropical and stone fruit flavors. Treehouse Julius is the benchmark.
Session IPA
ABV: 3–5%
IPA character at lower alcohol. Good drinkability. Sacrifices some body and complexity but keeps hop flavor.
Double / Imperial IPA
ABV: 7.5–10%
More malt to balance intense hops. Big and bold. Pliny the Elder (Russian River) essentially invented the modern DIPA.
Black IPA
ABV: 6–9%
Dark roasted malts with IPA hop character. Coffee and chocolate with citrus. Controversial name — some call it Cascadian Dark Ale.
Brut IPA
ABV: 6–8%
Bone dry, champagne-like finish. Uses amylase enzyme to convert all sugars. Clear, light-bodied, effervescent.
English IPA
Origin: England, 1800s
The original. Marmalade and earthy hops, moderate bitterness. Historically brewed strong for export to India. Fuller's London Pride descends from this lineage.

Wheat Beers

Hefeweizen
ABV: 4.5–5.5% · Origin: Bavaria
Unfiltered German wheat. Iconic banana (isoamyl acetate) and clove (4-vinyl guaiacol) esters from the distinctive yeast. Paulaner and Weihenstephaner are the standards.
Kristallweizen
ABV: 4.5–5.5% · Origin: Bavaria
Filtered hefeweizen. Clear, crisp, loses some yeast character but more approachable for lager drinkers.
Dunkelweizen
ABV: 4.5–5.6% · Origin: Bavaria
Dark wheat beer. Banana and clove from yeast plus chocolate and caramel from dark malts. Richer, more complex.
Weizenbock
ABV: 7–9%
Strong wheat beer. Fruity esters (banana, plum), rich maltiness. Schneider Aventinus is the classic.
Witbier / Belgian White
ABV: 4.5–5.5% · Origin: Belgium
Spiced with coriander and bitter orange peel. Hazy, pale, refreshing. Hoegaarden revived the style in the 1960s.
American Wheat
ABV: 4–5.5% · Origin: USA
Cleaner than German wheat, less yeast character. Used as a base for fruit additions. Widely approachable.
Berliner Weisse
ABV: 2.8–3.8% · Origin: Berlin
Very tart, low alcohol wheat beer. Traditionally served with raspberry or woodruff syrup. "The champagne of the North."
Gose
ABV: 4–5% · Origin: Goslar, Germany
Salted and soured wheat beer, often with coriander. Tart, salty, lemony. Saw a massive American craft revival in 2010s.

How Beer is Brewed

1
Malting

Barley (or wheat) is soaked, germinated to activate enzymes, then kilned to stop germination. Kilning temperature and duration determines malt color and flavor — pale, crystal, chocolate, black patent.

2
Mashing

Crushed malt mixed with hot water (~65–68°C / 149–155°F) for 60–90 minutes. Enzymes convert starches to fermentable sugars, producing sweet wort.

3
Lautering

The grain bed acts as a natural filter. Sweet wort is drained from the grain. Sparge water rinses remaining sugars from the grain bed.

4
Boiling

Wort is boiled for 60–90 minutes. Sterilizes, concentrates, caramelizes, and drives off unwanted compounds (DMS). Hops are added at different times.

5
Hopping

Bittering hops added early (60+ min) for bitterness. Flavor hops added later (15–30 min). Aroma hops at knockout or dry-hopped after fermentation for hop aroma without bitterness.

6
Fermenting

Cooled wort is pitched with yeast. Ales ferment warm (18–22°C) for 1–2 weeks. Lagers ferment cold (8–12°C) for 4–6 weeks. Yeast converts sugars to alcohol and CO₂.

7
Conditioning & Packaging

Beer is conditioned (lagered, dry-hopped, or filtered), carbonated, and packaged in kegs, cans, or bottles. Some are bottle-conditioned with residual yeast.

Beer & Food Pairing

Burgers & IPA
West Coast IPA's bitterness cuts through beef fat. The hop resin complements char from the grill. The rule: bold food, bold beer.
Fish & Pilsner
Crisp pilsner won't overwhelm delicate fish. The clean finish refreshes the palate between bites. Avoid hoppy ales with delicate white fish.
Pizza & Amber Ale
Caramel malt complements tomato sauce sweetness. Medium body matches pizza's richness. Very versatile pairing.
Oysters & Dry Stout
Classic Irish pairing. Roasted coffee bitterness provides a counterpoint to the briny, mineral oyster. Guinness and oysters is a tradition.
Spicy Food & Hefeweizen
Low bitterness, fruity esters, and carbonation cool the palate. Avoid IPAs with spicy food — hops amplify capsaicin heat.
Chocolate & Imperial Stout
Match intensity. The dark roasted malts mirror chocolate's bitterness. Bourbon barrel-aged imperial stout with chocolate cake is exceptional.
Cheddar & Pale Ale
Hop bitterness cuts through fat. Carbonation cleanses. Aged sharp cheddar with American pale ale is one of the great cheese and beer combinations.
Roast Chicken & Witbier
Coriander and orange peel in witbier complement herb-roasted chicken. The spice harmony is intentional and delicious.
