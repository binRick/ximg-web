Sushi

The art of sushi — types, fish, rice, and etiquette

Nigiri — Hand-pressed sushi rice topped with fish

Nigiri (握り寿司) is the cornerstone of sushi: a palm-sized oval of seasoned rice with a thin slice of fish or shellfish draped on top, often with a tiny smear of wasabi between them.

Tuna (Maguro)
マグロ
Rich, meaty, mild iron flavor. Bluefin is the gold standard; yellowfin and bigeye also common. Melts on the tongue.
Salmon (Sake)
サーモン
Buttery and sweet with delicate orange flesh. Introduced to Japan by Norwegian exporters in the 1980s. Now a global favorite.
Yellowtail (Hamachi)
ハマチ
Silky, slightly fatty, mildly sweet. Young yellowtail farmed in Japan. Prized for its clean finish and high oil content.
Shrimp (Ebi)
エビ
Sweet and firm with a slight snap. Usually poached and butterflied. One of the few cooked nigiri options.
Eel (Unagi)
うなぎ
Freshwater eel, always grilled and glazed with sweet tare sauce. Warming, rich, and caramel-sweet. Never served raw.
Octopus (Tako)
タコ
Chewy and slightly sweet. Boiled to tenderness, sliced thin on the bias. Clean ocean flavor, low fat.
Scallop (Hotate)
ホタテ
Creamy, sweet, almost custardy. Served raw or lightly seared. Pairs beautifully with a citrus ponzu.
Sea Urchin (Uni)
ウニ
The gonads of sea urchin — custard-like, briny, intensely oceanic. Hokkaido uni is world-class. An acquired taste that becomes addictive.
Mackerel (Saba)
サバ
Bold, oily, slightly funky. Usually salt-cured then vinegar-marinated before serving. One of the most traditional Edo-style nigiris.
Squid (Ika)
イカ
Mild, faintly sweet, lightly chewy. Scored in a crosshatch before serving to improve texture. Pairs well with shiso leaf.
Fatty Tuna (Toro)
トロ
Fatty belly of bluefin tuna. Otoro (ultra-fatty) and chutoro (medium-fatty) dissolve on the palate. The most coveted nigiri.
Clam (Hamaguri)
ハマグリ
Sweet, briny, lightly cooked. Seasonal in spring. Delicate texture with subtle umami depth from the sea.

Maki & Rolls — Sushi wrapped in nori and rice

Maki (巻き寿司) uses a bamboo mat to roll sushi rice and fillings inside nori (seaweed). Uramaki reverses the order — rice outside, nori inside. Many modern rolls were invented in the USA.

California Roll
カリフォルニアロール
Crab (or surimi), avocado, cucumber. Uramaki style. Invented in Los Angeles in the 1970s to suit American palates with no raw fish.
Spicy Tuna Roll
スパイシーツナ
Minced tuna mixed with sriracha and mayo. Uramaki or hosomaki. American invention; fiery, creamy, intensely satisfying.
Dragon Roll
ドラゴンロール
Shrimp tempura inside, avocado layered on the outside to resemble dragon scales. Topped with eel sauce and sesame.
Rainbow Roll
レインボーロール
California roll base topped with alternating slices of tuna, salmon, yellowtail, avocado, and shrimp for a colorful presentation.
Spider Roll
スパイダーロール
Soft-shell crab tempura, cucumber, avocado, spicy mayo. Crab legs often left sticking out the ends, like spider legs.
Philadelphia Roll
フィラデルフィアロール
Salmon, cream cheese, and cucumber. Controversial among purists but enormously popular. Cream cheese adds a rich tang.
Caterpillar Roll
キャタピラーロール
Eel and cucumber inside, avocado sliced thin on the outside to create a green caterpillar appearance. Drizzled with eel sauce.
Temaki (Hand Roll)
手巻き寿司
Cone-shaped roll held in the hand. Nori stays crisp only minutes — eat immediately. A fun, casual sushi format.
Futomaki
太巻き
Thick roll with 5–7 fillings: egg, spinach, pickled gourd, fish cake, shiitake. Traditional Japanese, eaten at festivals.
Cucumber Roll (Kappamaki)
かっぱ巻き
Simple cucumber hosomaki. Refreshing palate cleanser between richer bites. Named after the kappa water sprite.
Volcano Roll
ボルケーノロール
Baked spicy seafood mixture piled high on a California roll, then broiled until golden. American-style, rich and indulgent.

Sashimi — Pure sliced fish, no rice

Sashimi (刺身) is thinly sliced raw fish or seafood served without rice. It demands absolute freshness — sashimi-grade fish is handled with extreme care from catch to knife. Served with soy sauce, wasabi, and shiso leaf garnish.

Bluefin Tuna (Maguro)
本マグロ
Three cuts: akami (lean, deep red), chutoro (medium fatty, marbled), otoro (ultra-fatty, belly). Each a different experience.
Salmon (Sake)
サーモン
Vivid orange, buttery texture. Thick slices show off the fat marbling. Farm-raised Atlantic salmon dominates the market.
Yellowtail (Hamachi)
ハマチ
Silky white flesh with pink-ivory hue. Sliced thick. One of the most refined sashimi options available.
Halibut (Hirame)
ヒラメ
Delicate white fish with very mild flavor. The engawa (fin muscle) is a delicacy — chewy with concentrated fat.
Sea Bass (Suzuki)
スズキ
Clean, light, slightly sweet. Popular in summer. Beautiful white flesh with glistening silver skin.
Octopus (Tako)
タコ
Always cooked — octopus is boiled until tender then sliced. Mild flavor, distinctive chewy texture.
Squid (Ika)
イカ
Translucent white, soft yet firm. Cut thin or in strips. Often scored for texture. Sweet and clean flavor.
Horse Mackerel (Aji)
アジ
Small, flavourful, slightly oily. Bright flavor with a pleasant bite. Often served with grated ginger and scallion.

Fish Guide — Seasonality and sourcing

The best sushi chefs follow the fish calendar. Seasonality dictates fat content, flavor intensity, and price. Tsukiji and Toyosu markets in Tokyo set the global standard.

Bluefin Tuna
本マグロ / Honmaguro
Peak season: winter (Dec–Feb). Fattiest when cold. Three quality tiers: domestic Japanese, Atlantic, Pacific. Can cost $40+ per piece.
Salmon
サーモン / Sake
Year-round (farmed). Wild king salmon peaks June–August. Norwegian farm-raised is the global sushi standard for consistency.
Yellowtail
ハマチ / Hamachi
Best in winter when fat content rises. Young yellowtail is hamachi; adult is buri. Buri in winter is especially prized.
Mackerel
サバ / Saba
Peak autumn–winter. Always cured in salt and vinegar before use — never served truly raw due to parasites and rapid spoilage.
Flounder
ヒラメ / Hirame
Best in winter. Lean, white-fleshed flatfish. The thin fin muscle (engawa) is considered a delicacy by sushi connoisseurs.
Uni (Sea Urchin)
ウニ
Peak late spring to summer. Hokkaido Murasaki and Bafun uni are the top grades. Short shelf life — freshness is everything.
Bonito
カツオ / Katsuo
Two seasons: spring (hatsu-gatsuo) and fall (modori-gatsuo). Fall fish are fattier. Seared on the outside (tataki style) is classic.
Snapper
タイ / Tai
Red sea bream — the ceremonial fish of Japan, served at celebrations. Delicate, sweet, white flesh. Best spring–summer.
Sweet Shrimp
甘エビ / Amaebi
Raw spot prawns with intensely sweet, almost candy-like flavor. The heads are often deep-fried and served on the side.
Eel (Unagi)
うなぎ
Freshwater eel, always grilled with tare sauce. Never raw — contains ichthyotoxin. Peak summer eating per Japanese tradition (Doyo no Ushi).

Sushi Rice & Technique

The rice (shari or sumeshi) is the soul of sushi. A chef's rice is their signature. Japanese short-grain rice is seasoned with rice vinegar, sugar, and salt immediately after cooking while still hot, then fanned to a body-temperature finish.

Classic Sushi Rice Formula (per 3 cups dry rice)

  • 3 cups Japanese short-grain rice (Koshihikari)
  • 3 cups + 2 tbsp water (slightly less than normal)
  • 6 tbsp rice vinegar (unseasoned)
  • 3 tbsp white sugar
  • 2 tsp fine sea salt
  • Combine vinegar, sugar, salt; heat until dissolved, cool
  • Fold into hot rice using a wooden paddle (shamoji)
  • Fan while folding — never stir or mash the grains
  • Target temperature: 36–37°C (body temp) when shaping

Hand Technique — Nigiri

  • Wet hands with tezu (water + vinegar mixture)
  • Pick up ~20g of rice — roughly a golf ball
  • Gently shape into an oval with three quick motions
  • Apply a tiny smear of wasabi on the fish (optional)
  • Drape fish over rice, press once with index finger
  • Rotate 180°, press again with two fingers
  • The rice should feel light and barely hold together
  • A nigiri eaten 5 minutes old is already compromised

Rice Selection & Washing

  • Koshihikari variety is the gold standard
  • Wash rice 5–7 times until water runs nearly clear
  • Soak 30 minutes before cooking
  • Cook with kombu (kelp) in the water for extra umami
  • Rest 10 minutes covered after cooking before seasoning
  • Season in a wide, flat wooden tub (hangiri) if possible
  • Never refrigerate sushi rice — it hardens the starch

Wasabi — Real vs Imitation

  • Real wasabi: Wasabia japonica, grown in cold mountain streams
  • Grated on sharkskin (oroshi) just before serving — loses potency in 15 min
  • Over 95% of restaurants outside Japan use imitation
  • Imitation: horseradish + mustard + food coloring
  • Real wasabi is floral, complex, slightly sweet — not just hot
  • Never mix wasabi into soy sauce (dilutes both)

Sushi Etiquette — Rules of the counter

Traditional omakase dining at a high-end sushi counter has its own code. While casual conveyor-belt restaurants relax most rules, knowing proper etiquette deepens your appreciation of the craft.

01
Hands or chopsticks — both are correctNigiri was traditionally eaten by hand at standing sushi bars. Both methods are acceptable, though chopsticks are expected at formal kaiseki-style restaurants. At the counter, hands show you appreciate the texture.
02
Dip fish-side down, not rice-sideTurn the nigiri upside-down and briefly dip just the fish in soy sauce. Submerging the rice absorbs too much sodium, disintegrates the piece, and insults the chef's seasoning of the rice.
03
Eat nigiri in one biteNigiri is sized to be consumed whole. Biting it in half and setting it down disrupts the balance of fish, rice, and wasabi. If a piece is large, ask the chef to make it smaller.
04
Ginger is a palate cleanser, not a toppingPickled ginger (gari) is meant to be eaten between different types of fish to reset your taste buds. Placing it on top of sushi like a topping mixes flavors the chef has carefully balanced.
05
Never mix wasabi into the soy sauce dishThe chef has already placed the correct amount of wasabi on each piece. Mixing more into soy creates a diluted, imbalanced paste. Use fresh wasabi sparingly on the fish if you want more heat.
06
Eat nigiri immediately when servedThe chef times each piece for peak flavor and texture. Letting nigiri sit causes the rice to cool unevenly, the nori to lose crispness, and the fish to dull. Eat it within 30 seconds of being placed.
07
Trust the omakase orderOmakase (お任せ, "I leave it to you") means the chef controls the pace and order. Moving out of sequence by requesting specific pieces disrupts the chef's rhythm and narrative. Save requests for the end.
08
Don't apply perfume or cologneSushi is extremely aromatic. Strong fragrances interfere with your own tasting experience and disturb other diners. Many high-end sushi bars post this request explicitly.
09
Green tea is free; sake should complementHot green tea (agari) is traditionally served at the end of a sushi meal, not during. Sake enhances sushi — choose junmai or junmai ginjo; heavily sweet sakes compete with delicate fish.
10
Speak softly and engage the chef respectfullyThe sushi bar is a stage. The itamae (chef) is performing. Asking questions about the fish shows respect and often leads to extra pieces or stories about the day's catch. Treat it as a dialogue.
