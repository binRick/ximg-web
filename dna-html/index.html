Base Pairing Rules

Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) via 2 hydrogen bonds. Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) via 3 hydrogen bonds — making G-C pairs stronger.

Structure Facts

The DNA double helix is right-handed, with a full turn every 10 base pairs (~3.4 nm). Each nucleotide consists of a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.

DNA Replication

Semi-conservative replication: each daughter cell gets one original strand and one new strand.

Step 1 / 6
Initiation — Origin of Replication

Replication begins at specific sequences called origins of replication (ori). In bacteria there is 1 ori; humans have thousands. Initiator proteins bind and recruit helicase.

Transcription — DNA to mRNA

RNA polymerase reads the template strand (3'→5') and synthesizes mRNA (5'→3').

Initiation
Stage: Initiation

RNA polymerase binds to the promoter sequence (TATA box) upstream of the gene. The transcription bubble opens ~12-17 base pairs around the active site.

RNA vs DNA

In mRNA, Uracil (U) replaces Thymine (T). RNA is single-stranded. RNA polymerase does not need a primer unlike DNA polymerase.

Three Stages

Initiation: RNAP binds promoter.
Elongation: RNAP moves 3'→5' along template, builds mRNA 5'→3'.
Termination: RNAP reaches terminator sequence, mRNA released.

Translation — mRNA to Protein

The ribosome reads mRNA codons (3 nucleotides each) and assembles amino acids into a polypeptide.

Ribosome assembles at Start Codon (AUG)

The small (40S) ribosomal subunit scans the mRNA for the start codon AUG. The large (60S) subunit joins, forming the complete 80S ribosome. tRNA carrying Met enters the P site.
