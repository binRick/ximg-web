Farmers Market

Eat with the seasons — peak produce by season, farmers market tips, and how to store what you buy

Spring — March through May

Asparagus
Peak: April–May
Look for tight, firm tips. Snaps cleanly when fresh. Thin spears are tender; fat spears are meatier. Store upright in water like flowers.
Peas — English, Snap, Snow
Peak: April–June
English peas: shell them fresh. Snap peas: eat pod and all. They deteriorate quickly — buy day-of and eat same day for best sweetness.
Ramps / Wild Leeks
Peak: March–April
Intensely garlicky wild onion. Both leaf and bulb are edible. Foraged — only available briefly. Pickle the bulbs, sauté the leaves.
Morel Mushrooms
Peak: April–May
Honeycombed caps, rich earthy flavor. Must be cooked — never eat raw. Dry sauté first, then add butter. Worth the premium.
Strawberries
Peak: May–June
Farm strawberries are smaller and far more flavorful than supermarket. Pick fragrant, deeply red fruit — perfume is the best indicator.
Artichokes
Peak: March–May
California produces nearly all US artichokes. Heavy for size, tightly packed leaves. Squeak when squeezed = fresh. Baby artichokes are fully edible.
Fava Beans
Peak: April–June
Double-podded — remove from shell, blanch, then peel the inner skin. Labor intensive but uniquely buttery. Classic Italian spring ingredient.
Spring Radishes
Peak: March–May
French Breakfast, Easter Egg varieties. Crisp, peppery. Eat with good butter and salt. Greens are edible — slightly peppery.
Watercress & Pea Shoots
Peak: March–May
Delicate greens. Watercress is peppery; pea shoots taste like fresh peas. Best raw or barely wilted. Highly perishable.

Summer — June through August

Tomatoes
Peak: July–September
Heirloom varieties: Brandywine, Cherokee Purple, Green Zebra. Smell the stem end — strong tomato fragrance = ripe. Never refrigerate.
Corn
Peak: July–August
Sugars convert to starch quickly after harvest. Buy same day, cook same day. Peel back husk to check plump, firm kernels.
Zucchini & Summer Squash
Peak: June–August
Pick small (6–8 inches) for better flavor and fewer seeds. Farmers markets often have unusual varieties — pattypan, eight-ball, costata romanesco.
Peppers
Peak: July–September
Bell, shishito, padron, Hungarian wax, Jimmy Nardello (sweet frying pepper). Color indicates ripeness — red is fully ripe, sweetest.
Eggplant
Peak: July–September
Should feel heavy, skin taut and glossy. Globe, Japanese, Italian, Chinese varieties. Japanese and Chinese have fewer seeds, less bitterness.
Peaches & Nectarines
Peak: July–August
Freestone vs clingstone. Smell near the stem — fragrant means ripe. Slight give when pressed at the shoulders. Refrigerate only when fully ripe.
Blueberries
Peak: July–August
Should have a bloom (silvery coating) — sign of freshness. Highbush vs lowbush (wild — more intense flavor). Refrigerate immediately.
Watermelon
Peak: July–August
Look for a creamy yellow field spot (where it sat on ground). Sounds hollow when tapped. Heavier = juicier. Ask farmers when it was picked.
Basil
Peak: June–September
Don't refrigerate — turns black. Store in a glass of water at room temp. Genovese, Thai, lemon, purple — all peak in summer.

Fall — September through November

Apples
Peak: September–November
Farm varieties far exceed supermarket: Honeycrisp, Cox's Orange Pippin, Ashmead's Kernel, Calville Blanc. Sample before buying. Store in crisper drawer.
Winter Squash
Peak: September–November
Butternut, delicata, acorn, kabocha, Hubbard, spaghetti. Hard rind = ripe. Kabocha has superior flavor — dense, dry, chestnut-like.
Pears
Peak: September–October
Bosc, Bartlett, Comice, Seckel. Ripen at room temp; refrigerate once ripe. Check near the stem for ripeness — shouldn't be hard.
Brussels Sprouts
Peak: October–December
Buy on the stalk when possible — stay fresh longer. Frost-sweetened sprouts from late fall are notably better than summer sprouts.
Persimmons
Peak: October–November
Two types: Fuyu (tomato-shaped, eat firm) and Hachiya (acorn-shaped, must be fully soft or astringent). Hachiyas make excellent baked goods.
Sweet Potatoes
Peak: October–December
Cure after harvest improves sweetness. Garnet, Jewel, Japanese white-fleshed varieties. Don't refrigerate — cold damages them.
Pomegranate
Peak: October–January
Heavy for size = juicy. Blush color is fine; look for weight. Score and pry open in a bowl of water — arils sink, pith floats.
Beets
Peak: September–November
Red, golden, Chioggia (candy-striped). Greens are edible — like chard. Roast with skin on, slip off when cool. Golden beets won't stain.

Winter — December through February

Blood Oranges
Peak: January–March
Moro, Tarocco, Sanguinello varieties. Anthocyanins develop from cold nights — more color = colder growing conditions. Raspberry-berry notes.
Grapefruit
Peak: December–April
Ruby Red, Oro Blanco, Star Ruby. Heavy for size = juicy. Florida vs Texas vs California have different flavor profiles. Pairs well with salt and honey.
Meyer Lemons
Peak: December–March
Lemon × mandarin cross. Sweeter, less acidic, floral. Thin skin. Use zest and juice interchangeably with regular lemon but expect milder acidity.
Kale & Lacinato Kale
Peak: November–March
Frost-sweetened. Lacinato (dinosaur/cavolo nero) has more tender leaves. Massage raw kale with oil to soften. Pairs with lemon and garlic.
Celeriac / Celery Root
Peak: November–March
Knobby root. Nutty, celery-like flavor. Remoulade (shredded raw with mustard mayo) is the classic French preparation. Roasts beautifully.
Sunchoke / Jerusalem Artichoke
Peak: November–February
Nutty, earthy, slightly sweet. Unrelated to artichokes. Inulin content can cause digestive discomfort ("fartichoke"). Roast or make into soup.
Fennel
Peak: October–March
Anise-licorice flavor that mellows when cooked. Fronds are a garnish. Shave raw into salads or roast/braise until caramelized and tender.
Chestnuts
Peak: October–December
Score the flat side before roasting (prevents explosion). Fresh chestnuts store in the fridge. Starchy and sweet — roast, braise, or make chestnut soup.

Year-Round Staples

Carrots
Available year-round but sweetest in fall after frost. Heirloom varieties (Chantenay, Danvers, Cosmic Purple) far exceed standard orange. Keep tops separate — they draw moisture.
Onions & Alliums
Yellow, red, white, shallots, cipollini. Store in a cool, dry, dark place with good airflow. Keep away from potatoes — they exchange gases that hasten spoilage.
Garlic
Fresh garlic at markets is often softneck (mild, stores long) or hardneck (stronger flavor, shorter shelf life). Spring "green garlic" is a special treat.
Potatoes
Fingerlings, purple Peruvian, Yukon Gold, All Blue. Store in a dark, cool (not cold) place. Avoid refrigerating — converts starch to sugar, changes frying quality.
Eggs
Pastured farm eggs have bright orange yolks from foraging. Freshness test: fresh eggs sink in water, older ones float. Farm eggs don't need refrigeration if unwashed.
Mushrooms (Cultivated)
Shiitake, oyster, maitake, lion's mane available year-round from growers. Keep in paper bag in fridge (not plastic). Don't wash — wipe clean or dry brush.
Hardy Herbs
Rosemary, thyme, sage, oregano available year-round. Wrap in damp paper towel, store in fridge. Or dry them — these herbs dry beautifully and intensify in flavor.

Farmers Market Tips

Arrive Early for Best Selection

The first hour has the best variety and the freshest product. Popular items like heirloom tomatoes, fresh eggs, and specialty mushrooms sell out fast.

Arrive Late for Deals

In the last 30–60 minutes, vendors often discount what they don't want to pack back. Good for bulk buying for preserving, fermenting, or cooking large batches.

Bring Cash (and Small Bills)

Many small farms don't take credit cards. $1, $5, and $10 bills make transactions faster and vendors appreciate the change.

Ask About Farming Practices

Not every farm can afford organic certification but many grow without pesticides. Ask: "Do you spray?" or "Are you using any pesticides?" Farmers love talking about their methods.

CSA (Community Supported Agriculture)

Buy a share of a farm's harvest upfront. You get a weekly box of seasonal produce throughout the season. Lower cost than market prices. Ask farmers if they offer one.

Build Relationships

Regulars get benefits — priority on special items, farm tour invitations, "seconds" (cosmetically imperfect but perfectly good produce at discount). Show up weekly.

Understanding Labels

"Certified Organic" means USDA verified. "Grown without pesticides" is self-reported. "All natural" means nothing legally. "Regenerative" is an emerging certification focused on soil health.

Buy Imperfect Produce

Ugly tomatoes, misshapen carrots, and forked parsnips taste identical to perfect ones. Often discounted and they reduce farm waste. Perfect for sauces, soups, and ferments.
