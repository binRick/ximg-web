American Firearms

Gun types that define modern shooting sports and self-defense, plus the ten most popular firearms owned in the United States.

This guide is for law-abiding gun owners who want to be genuinely prepared — not just armed. Owning a firearm for home defense is a serious responsibility. The difference between a prepared defender and an unprepared one is training, planning, and mindset — not the gun itself. Read this before you need it.
🔫
Section 01
Choosing the Right Home Defense Firearm

The best home defense gun is the one you shoot accurately under stress. That said, the three most proven platforms each have real advantages.

A 12-gauge pump shotgun (Remington 870, Mossberg 500) is the most devastating close-range option. A load of 00 buckshot puts nine .33-caliber projectiles on target simultaneously. Penetration through interior walls is a concern, but at home-defense ranges its stopping power is unmatched. The racking sound alone has ended many situations without a shot fired.

A semi-automatic pistol (Glock 19, SIG P320, S&W M&P) offers maneuverability in tight hallways, the ability to operate one-handed while holding a phone or securing a child, and 15+ rounds on tap. Most people who train regularly find a pistol the most practical option because it is the gun they carry daily — the same manual of arms, the same trigger, under stress.

A semi-automatic rifle or pistol-caliber carbine (AR-15, Ruger PC Carbine) delivers superior accuracy, minimal recoil, and — with the right ammunition — less wall penetration than you might expect. An AR-15 loaded with 55-grain fragmenting ammo actually penetrates fewer walls than 9mm or buckshot in many tests.

Bottom Line
A pistol or shotgun is the practical choice for most homeowners. Whichever you choose, train with it until the manual of arms is completely automatic. In a 2 a.m. adrenaline surge you will not rise to the occasion — you will default to your level of training.

Section 02
The Right Ammunition for Home Defense

Never use full metal jacket (FMJ / ball) ammunition for home defense. FMJ is designed for range training — it passes straight through soft tissue without expanding, carrying lethal energy into walls, neighboring rooms, and neighbors' homes. Use quality hollow-point ammunition designed specifically for defensive use.

Hollow-point bullets expand on impact, transferring their energy into the target rather than passing through it. This increases stopping effectiveness and reduces over-penetration risk. Every major law enforcement agency in the United States issues hollow-point ammunition for exactly this reason.

9mm — Best Loads
Federal HST 124gr +P, Speer Gold Dot 124gr, Hornady Critical Duty 135gr. Any of these meet or exceed FBI penetration standards (12–18 inches in ballistic gel).
.45 ACP — Best Loads
Federal HST 230gr, Speer Gold Dot 230gr, Hornady Critical Defense 185gr. The .45's wide diameter delivers massive expansion — choose standard pressure for reliable cycling.
12 Gauge — Best Loads
Federal FliteControl 00 Buckshot is the gold standard — its wad keeps pellets tighter at indoor distances. Avoid birdshot for defense; it lacks adequate penetration depth.
.380 ACP — Best Loads
Hornady Critical Defense 90gr FTX, Federal HST 99gr Micro. The .380's limited power makes quality hollow-points even more critical — don't compromise on ammo in this caliber.
AR-15 / 5.56 — Best Loads
Federal 55gr Varmint (fragmenting) or Hornady TAP 75gr for reduced wall penetration. Avoid M193 and M855 military ball — they overpenetrate significantly in home environments.
Test Your Ammo
Run at least 50–100 rounds of your defensive load through your firearm before trusting it. Confirm your gun cycles it 100% reliably — some guns have feeding preferences with certain hollow-points.
Critical Rule
Inspect your defensive ammo every 6–12 months. Rounds repeatedly chambered during dry-fire practice can suffer bullet setback, raising pressure to dangerous levels. Rotate your carry ammo regularly and never re-chamber a round more than a handful of times.

Section 03
Storage — Accessible to You, Inaccessible to Everyone Else

This is the central tension of home defense: a gun locked away is useless in an emergency, but an unsecured gun is a liability — especially in homes with children, visitors, or theft risk. The solution is a purpose-built rapid-access pistol safe.

Modern quick-access safes open in 1–2 seconds via biometric fingerprint, RFID wristband, or a 4-button push-code. They mount under a nightstand, inside a drawer, or to a bedframe with steel cable. Practice opening yours in the dark — the middle of the night is not the time to discover the battery is dead or your fingerprint won't register with shaking hands.

🛏️
Bedside quick-access safe: Mount a biometric or push-button safe to the side or underside of your nightstand. Keep it at arm's reach while lying down. Practice the motion until it is automatic. This is where your primary defensive firearm lives.
🔋
Battery maintenance: Most quick-access safes run on AA batteries. Set a reminder every 6 months to replace them — don't wait for a low-battery warning. Keep a spare set in the nightstand drawer.
👶
Children in the home: If children are present, the quick-access safe is non-negotiable. Children are curious and will find any unsecured firearm. The safe keeps the gun out of reach while remaining accessible to you in seconds.
🏠
Secondary locations: Consider a second quick-access safe in your home office or living area if your bedroom is far from where you spend most of your day. Intruders can arrive at any hour — not just at night.
🔐
Long gun storage: A shotgun or rifle kept for home defense should be stored in a full-size locked safe when not actively staged. Trigger locks are a last resort — they add seconds you may not have and can be fumbled under stress.
Staged vs. Stored
"Staged" means loaded, chamber-ready, secured in a quick-access safe. "Stored" means in a full safe, possibly unloaded. Your home defense gun should be staged, not stored. An unloaded gun in an emergency is a throwing weapon.

Section 04
Training — The Only Thing That Matters Under Stress

Most gun owners shoot their defensive firearm once when they buy it and never again. This is the single most dangerous mistake in home defense preparation. Under extreme stress, fine motor skills deteriorate sharply. You will grip harder than you expect, forget safeties, and may miss malfunctions entirely. Training builds gross motor patterns deep enough to survive an adrenaline dump at 3 a.m.

📅
Shoot regularly: Minimum once a month with your defensive firearm. Focus on fundamentals — grip, sight alignment, trigger press. 50 deliberate rounds beats 200 casual ones every time.
🌑
Low-light training: Home invasions happen at night. Practice accessing your safe and drawing in a dark room. A weapon-mounted light (WML) — SureFire or Streamlight — illuminates and identifies a threat without occupying your support hand.
📐
Take a formal course: A one-day defensive pistol or shotgun course from a qualified instructor (NRA, USCCA, or Gunsite certified) will teach you more in 8 hours than a year of solo range trips — drawing, clearing malfunctions, moving while shooting, and decision-making under stress.
🏠
Dry-fire at home: With an unloaded, verified-clear firearm, practice your presentation from the safe, trigger press, and reloads. 10 minutes of dry-fire three times a week builds muscle memory that live fire cements. Always remove all ammunition from the room first.
🧠
Practice your plan, not just your shooting: Walk your home. Identify your safe room. Know where family members sleep. Practice the path in the dark. Tactics and positioning matter more than marksmanship at 10 feet.
🔄
Malfunction drills: Know the immediate action drill for your firearm cold — Tap (magazine), Rack (slide), Assess. A malfunction in the dark under stress is survivable only if clearing it is automatic.

Section 05
The Home Defense Plan — Know It Before You Need It

A firearm without a plan is improvisation under the worst possible circumstances. Write a home defense plan. Discuss it with every adult in your household. Practice it. The plan answers the questions your brain will struggle to answer at 3 a.m. with your heart rate at 180 bpm.

📍
Designate a safe room: The master bedroom is the natural choice — it's where you sleep, your gun is staged, and you have a door to barricade. Tell every family member: if you hear something, get to the safe room. This consolidates your family in a known location and eliminates the nightmare of shooting a family member in a dark hallway.
📞
Call 911 first: If you have any time before a threat reaches you, call 911 immediately. Give your address, report the intruder, and stay on the line. This is both the legally correct action and the tactically smart one — you want backup, and you want the dispatcher to hear what unfolds.
🛡️
Defend in place — don't go hunting: Leaving your safe room to search the house surrenders every tactical advantage. Hold your position, secure your family, call 911, and let the threat come to you. The intruder who opens your bedroom door to find a prepared defender is in the worst possible position.
💡
Control the light: From your safe room, turn on every light you can reach. Illuminate hallways. Use your weapon light. Darkness favors the intruder who has been moving through your home — you want to see clearly before making any decision.
🗣️
Challenge verbally: From behind your locked door: "I'm armed and I've called the police — leave now!" This warns a legitimate occupant, may cause a burglar to flee (most want property, not confrontation), and demonstrates on the 911 recording that you attempted de-escalation before using force.
Legal Reality
Know your state's laws on use of deadly force before you need them. Castle Doctrine and Stand Your Ground laws vary significantly by state. In some states you have a broad right to defend your home; in others, a duty-to-retreat may apply. Ignorance of the law is not a defense — consult your state statutes or take a USCCA legal self-defense course.

Section 06
You Hear Something at 3 A.M. — Step by Step

This is the scenario you have been preparing for. Your first reaction matters. Most bumps in the night are not home invasions — but your response to both situations needs to be identical until you know which you are dealing with.

1️⃣
Wake up fully before you act. Stay still for 5–10 seconds. Listen. Orient yourself. Adrenaline fires before your brain is fully engaged — let your mind catch up before your body moves.
2️⃣
Access your firearm. Open your quick-access safe. Take your firearm with finger off the trigger. You are not yet in a confirmed threat situation — keep the gun indexed, pointed in a safe direction.
3️⃣
Alert your household and call 911. Wake your partner quietly — one person covers the door while the other calls 911. If you live alone, call 911 yourself. Give your address and stay on the line. It costs nothing to call and cancel; it costs everything to need help and not have called.
4️⃣
Consolidate your family in the safe room. If it is safe to retrieve children from nearby rooms, do so. If not, have them lock their doors and communicate by phone. Get everyone behind a locked, barricadable door.
5️⃣
Cover the door from an angle. Position yourself to the side of the door — not directly in front of it. A door is not cover. An angle gives you a view of the entry while keeping you out of the fatal funnel. Turn on your weapon light. Verbal challenge ready.
6️⃣
Identify before you fire. You must positively identify your target as a threat before pulling the trigger. You cannot shoot at a sound, a shape, or a shadow — legally or morally. Your weapon light exists for this moment. A family member, guest, or sleepwalking child may be on the other side of that door.
7️⃣
After the threat resolves — stay on the line and show empty hands. Whether you fired or the intruder fled, remain on with 911 until police arrive. When officers enter, set the firearm down and immediately show empty hands. State clearly: "I am the homeowner." The responding officer does not know who is who — make it obvious.
The Single Most Common Fatal Mistake
Going to investigate the noise is how most defensive situations become tragedies. Most intruders flee the moment they realize the home is occupied. By leaving your safe room to search, you surrender your defensive position, encounter the intruder on their terms in the dark, and must make a lethal-force decision in a fraction of a second. Hold your ground. Let the threat come to you or leave.

Section 07
Mindset — The Weapon Behind the Weapon

Every serious instructor will tell you the same thing: the firearm is the last resort, not the first response. The layers of home defense — good locks, exterior lighting, a security system, a dog, situational awareness, and a practiced plan — are what prevent the situation from ever reaching the point where a gun is needed.

Carrying the decision to use lethal force is a psychological weight that must be settled before the emergency, not during it. Decide now, calmly, with knowledge of your state's law: under what circumstances will you fire? A clear answer made in advance means your judgment is not impaired in the moment by hesitation or panic. Hesitation kills defenders. So does shooting too quickly.

The goal of home defense is not to shoot an intruder. The goal is for you and your family to survive unharmed. If the intruder flees without a shot fired, that is the best possible outcome. A firearm you never had to use is a complete success.

850
Gunpowder Discovered — China
Tang Dynasty alchemists searching for immortality accidentally discover gunpowder — a mixture of charcoal, sulfur, and potassium nitrate (saltpeter). Early texts warn that the mixture can ignite violently, singeing hands and burning houses.
919
Fire Arrows in Battle
Chinese forces use gunpowder-propelled fire arrows (fei huo qiang) in warfare, marking the first military application of the technology. The psychological effect on cavalry and infantry is devastating.
1000s
The Fire Lance — First Proto-Gun
Chinese soldiers mount bamboo or metal tubes packed with gunpowder and shrapnel to spear shafts. When lit, the fire lance shoots flame and debris at close range — the direct ancestor of the handheld firearm.
1326
First European Cannon on Record
The city of Florence records a cannon and iron cannonballs in its arsenal — the earliest documented firearm in Europe. Gunpowder technology traveled the Silk Road from China through the Islamic world into Europe within two centuries of its discovery.
1400s
Matchlock — First Reliable Ignition
European gunsmiths develop the matchlock mechanism: a slow-burning match is clamped into a lever that swings it into a pan of priming powder when the trigger is pulled. For the first time, soldiers can aim and fire without a free hand to hold a lit coal.
1509
Wheel Lock — Birth of the Pistol
The wheel lock generates sparks by spinning a serrated wheel against iron pyrite, eliminating the need for a burning match. Cavalry can now carry and fire loaded pistols on horseback, fundamentally changing mounted warfare.
1610
Flintlock — 200 Years of Dominance
The flintlock mechanism uses a spring-loaded flint to strike steel and ignite priming powder. Cheaper, more reliable, and faster to load than the wheel lock, it dominates military and civilian firearms for over two centuries — through the American Revolution and Napoleonic Wars.
1836
Colt Patents the Revolver
Samuel Colt patents the first successful percussion revolver — a rotating cylinder holding multiple rounds that indexes automatically with each cock of the hammer. One man can now fire six shots without reloading. The Colt Paterson revolver changes personal combat forever.
1847
Percussion Cap Replaces Flintlock
The percussion cap — a small copper cup containing mercury fulminate — is struck by the hammer to ignite the main charge. Far more reliable in rain and wind than flintlock, it transitions military rifles worldwide and directly enables the self-contained metallic cartridge.
1860
Henry Rifle — First Lever-Action Repeater
Benjamin Tyler Henry patents the Henry repeating rifle, holding 16 rounds of .44 rimfire in a tubular magazine. Confederate soldiers call it "that damned Yankee rifle that you could load on Sunday and shoot all week." It foreshadows the end of single-shot military rifles.
1873
Winchester '73 & Colt SAA — The Old West
Winchester introduces the Model 1873 ("The Gun That Won the West") and Colt debuts the Single Action Army revolver the same year — both chambered in .44-40, so frontiersmen need only one type of ammunition. Together they define the iconography of the American frontier.
1884
Maxim Gun — First Automatic Machine Gun
Hiram Maxim demonstrates the first fully automatic machine gun, using the recoil of each fired round to reload the next. A single Maxim gun can fire 600 rounds per minute. Colonial armies deploy it to devastating effect, fundamentally altering the nature of warfare.
1911
Colt M1911 — John Browning's Masterpiece
John Moses Browning's .45 ACP semi-automatic pistol is adopted by the U.S. military following grueling tests. It serves American forces through two World Wars, Korea, and Vietnam — 74 continuous years of service. Its short-recoil tilting-barrel design remains the template for most modern pistols.
1936
M1 Garand — Semi-Auto Enters Service
The M1 Garand becomes the first semi-automatic rifle adopted as the standard infantry weapon of any military. Gen. George Patton calls it "the greatest battle implement ever devised." Every American GI in WWII carries one; German and Japanese soldiers remain largely bolt-action.
1947
AK-47 Designed
Mikhail Kalashnikov designs the Avtomat Kalashnikova, a gas-operated assault rifle chambered in 7.62×39mm. Its legendary reliability in mud, sand, and cold — and its simplicity of operation — make it the most produced firearm in history, with an estimated 100 million made.
1963
M16 / AR-15 Platform Adopted
Eugene Stoner's ArmaLite AR-15 design is adopted by the U.S. military as the M16, chambered in 5.56×45mm. Its lightweight aluminum and polymer construction and straight-line stock reduce felt recoil. The semi-automatic civilian AR-15 becomes the best-selling rifle platform in U.S. history.
1982
Glock 17 — The Polymer Revolution
Gaston Glock wins an Austrian military contract with a radical pistol: a polymer frame, striker-fired action, and no manual safety. Lighter, cheaper, and more corrosion-resistant than steel-framed competitors, the Glock design is adopted by law enforcement agencies worldwide and reshapes the handgun market.
2008
DC v. Heller — Supreme Court Affirms Individual Right
The U.S. Supreme Court rules 5–4 in District of Columbia v. Heller that the Second Amendment protects an individual's right to keep and bear arms, independent of militia service. The decision strikes down D.C.'s handgun ban and becomes the foundation of modern Second Amendment jurisprudence.
2022
Bruen Decision Expands Carry Rights
New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen establishes that firearms regulations must be consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. The ruling strikes down New York's "proper cause" requirement for concealed carry permits, extending Heller's reach.

Section 01
Port Arthur, 1996 — The Massacre That Changed Everything

On April 28, 1996, a gunman killed 35 people and wounded 23 others at the Port Arthur historic site in Tasmania, Australia. It was the deadliest peacetime mass shooting in Australian history — and within 12 days, the Australian government used it to dismantle civilian gun ownership on a scale never before attempted in a Western democracy.

Prime Minister John Howard — in office for just six weeks — moved with extraordinary speed. Before the victims were buried, before any formal inquiry, the National Firearms Agreement (NFA) was announced. Howard wore a bulletproof vest to his own press conference, because he expected angry Australians to protest the confiscation of their legally owned property. He was right to expect it. He did it anyway.

The lesson for Americans is not what happened at Port Arthur. Tragedies happen everywhere. The lesson is how fast a government can move to exploit one — and what happens when citizens have no constitutional protection for their rights.

📜
Section 02
The National Firearms Agreement — What Was Taken

The NFA, adopted unanimously by all Australian states, did the following:

🚫
Banned outright: All semi-automatic rifles. All semi-automatic shotguns. All pump-action shotguns with a magazine capacity above 5 rounds. These were not restricted — they were prohibited. Owning one became a criminal offense.
💸
Mandatory buyback: The government compulsorily purchased — at below-market valuations set by the government itself — every newly prohibited firearm. Gun owners had no choice. Hand it in, or go to prison. Approximately 650,000 to 700,000 firearms were confiscated. The buyback cost approximately AUD $500 million, funded by a special Medicare levy — meaning Australian taxpayers paid to have their own rights stripped away.
📋
National registry: All remaining firearms registered. Uniform licensing. Storage requirements so onerous that practical home defense became nearly impossible. "Genuine reason" requirements that excluded self-defense as a valid justification for owning a firearm in most states.

Let that sink in: self-defense was explicitly removed as a lawful reason to own a firearm. The Australian government decided its citizens had no right to protect themselves with a gun. Period.

🏚️
Section 03
What Australians Lost — A Culture Erased

Australia had a genuine gun culture. Firearms were woven into rural life — station owners, farmers, drovers, hunters. The Australians who bore the heaviest burden of the buyback were not suburban conservatives making a political point. They were rural workers for whom a rifle in the truck was as ordinary as a shovel.

🌾
Rural devastation: Farmers who used semi-automatic rifles for pest control — feral pigs, rabbits, foxes — lost their most effective tools. The bolt-action permitted as a substitute is slower and less suitable for controlling feral animal populations at scale. Australian agriculture still suffers the consequences.
🎯
Sport shooting destroyed: Practical shooting sports — IPSC, service rifle competition, 3-gun — were gutted. Disciplines that had thriving Australian communities were simply eliminated when the equipment required was banned. Decades of competitive shooting tradition, gone in twelve days of legislative panic.
👨‍👩‍👧
A right treated as a privilege: Gun ownership in Australia was never a right. It was always a privilege granted at government discretion. The Port Arthur massacre proved what happens to privileges: they can be revoked by the same government that granted them, whenever political conditions favor it. The confiscation was legal because Australians had no constitutional protection. There was nothing to stop it.
📊
Section 04
Did It Work? — The Statistics Debate

Gun control advocates point to Australia as proof that strict gun laws save lives. The reality is more complicated — and the honest data tells a different story than the talking points.

📉
The claim: Mass shootings stopped. In the 18 years before Port Arthur, Australia suffered 13 mass shootings. In the 22 years after, none meeting the same definition (5+ victims). Advocates say this is proof the ban worked.
🔍
The counter-evidence: Australia's homicide rate was already declining before 1996, following a downward trend common across all Western nations. Studies published in peer-reviewed journals — including by economists at the University of Melbourne — found the buyback had no statistically significant effect on firearm homicide rates beyond the existing trend. Australia's firearm suicide rate also declined — but the overall suicide rate did not, consistent with the well-documented phenomenon of suicide method substitution.
⚖️
What the buyback definitively did: It confiscated 650,000 firearms from law-abiding citizens. It did not disarm criminals, who by definition do not comply with gun laws. The illicit firearms market in Australia — fed by smuggling and theft — has grown steadily since 1996. Australian police seized record numbers of illegal firearms throughout the 2010s. The guns the government took were the guns of people who would never have committed crimes with them.

The great irony: the one thing the buyback certainly accomplished was making Australia's law-abiding gun owners defenseless. Whether it prevented a single murder by a criminal is, at best, statistically uncertain.

🛡️
Section 05
Why This Must Never Happen in America

Every time there is a tragedy involving a firearm in the United States, the same voices point to Australia. "Look what they did. Why can't we?" Here is why:

📜
The Second Amendment is not a privilege — it is a right. Australia had no constitutional protection for gun ownership. American citizens do. The Second Amendment was not written to protect duck hunting. It was written by men who had just fought a war against a government that tried to disarm them — specifically to preserve the people's ultimate check on tyranny. The Founders understood what Australians learned in 1996: a government that can take your guns will.
🔢
The scale makes confiscation impossible — and the attempt would be catastrophic. There are an estimated 400+ million firearms in private American hands. Australia confiscated 650,000 and called it a success. America would need to confiscate 400 million. The logistical impossibility is the point: the American people, over generations, made themselves ungovernable on this question. That was deliberate. That was wise.
🏘️
Self-defense is a natural right. The Australian government removed self-defense as a valid justification for gun ownership. American law — affirmed by the Supreme Court in Heller (2008) — explicitly protects the right to keep a handgun in the home for self-defense. Rural Americans, like rural Australians, may be 30+ minutes from the nearest police response. That farmer's rifle is not a policy choice. It is a lifeline.
🎢
The slippery slope is real — because we have seen it. "Nobody wants to take your guns" is the most demonstrably false statement in American politics. Australia took the guns. The United Kingdom took the guns. Canada is taking the guns. Every step toward restriction is framed as the last step needed. It never is. Once a right is surrendered, it does not return. There is no Australian politician campaigning to give back the 650,000 confiscated firearms. There never will be.
⚡
Governments move fast when they have the power to act. John Howard passed the NFA in 12 days. Twelve days. The guns were gone before most Australians could organize meaningful opposition. The American system of separated powers, federalism, and constitutional protection exists precisely to prevent this kind of executive speed. The Second Amendment is not just about guns — it is about the pace at which liberty can be destroyed.
🇺🇸
Section 06
What Americans Must Do to Protect Their Rights

The price of liberty is eternal vigilance. The Australian experience is not ancient history — it happened in 1996, within living memory. Here is how American gun owners protect what generations of citizens have preserved:

🗳️
Vote — in every election, at every level. Federal judges who interpret the Second Amendment serve for life. State legislatures pass the gun laws that actually affect daily life. Local prosecutors decide whether to enforce constitutional carry or harass lawful gun owners. Every race matters. Sitting out an election because the candidate is imperfect is how you lose rights.
📢
Support Second Amendment organizations. The NRA, Gun Owners of America, the Second Amendment Foundation, and state-level groups fund the litigation, lobbying, and education that keep gun rights alive. The gun control lobby is well-funded and never sleeps. The defense of the Second Amendment requires organized, sustained effort — not just individual gun ownership.
📍
Support constitutional carry. Thirty states now have permitless carry — the law as written, without bureaucratic permission structures imposed on top of a constitutional right. Each state that adopts constitutional carry makes the map of freedom larger and makes the argument for further restrictions harder. Push your state if it has not yet acted.
🚨
Oppose red flag laws without due process. "Red flag" or Extreme Risk Protection Orders allow firearms to be confiscated from individuals before any crime is committed, based on a complaint, without the owner being present at the hearing. Due process — the right to face your accuser and present a defense before property is seized — is a foundational American right. These laws invert it. They are the mechanism by which mass confiscation begins one person at a time.
📚
Know your history — and teach it. Most Americans who say "look at Australia" cannot tell you how many guns were confiscated, what self-defense law looks like there now, or what the actual homicide statistics show. Knowledge is armor. Learn the facts. Share them. The Second Amendment is defended at every dinner table and every conversation, not just in courts and legislatures.
⚖️
The final word: The Founders gave Americans the Second Amendment knowing that future generations would face exactly this pressure. They wrote it not to protect muskets but to protect the principle — that the right of the people to keep and bear arms shall not be infringed. Not restricted. Not licensed. Not registered. Not confiscated. Not infringed. Australia showed the world what happens when citizens lack that protection. America must never find out.
