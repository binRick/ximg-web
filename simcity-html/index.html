The SimCity Games
The Maxis Era — 1989–2003
🏙️
1989
SimCity
1989 Will Wright Maxis / Broderbund

The game that invented the genre. Will Wright's original SimCity gave players an empty plot of land and a set of municipal tools — zone residential, commercial, and industrial areas; lay roads and rail; build power plants; manage budgets. There were no win conditions, no score to beat, no enemies. Just a city to grow or neglect.

It sold modestly at first — publishers thought a game with no way to win was unsellable. Broderbund eventually took it on, and it became a phenomenon. The idea that a simulation itself could be the entertainment was completely new.

  • Introduced zoning mechanics: R/C/I, the foundation of every city sim since
  • Disasters: tornadoes, earthquakes, Godzilla-style monsters, nuclear meltdowns
  • Dr. Wright — your in-game advisor — named after the creator
  • Bundled with early Macs, introducing millions to the concept
🏗️
1993
SimCity 2000
1993 Will Wright Maxis

The jump that defined the series. SimCity 2000 added an isometric perspective, underground water and subway systems, elevation and terraforming, a proper budget window with bond financing, and an encyclopedia of in-game information. It was richer, deeper, and more beautiful than anything before it.

For many players, SimCity 2000 remains the definitive version of the game — the perfect balance of approachability and depth, before the series began adding complexity for its own sake.

  • Isometric view — the visual identity of the series for a decade
  • Underground infrastructure: water pipes, subway tunnels
  • Arcologies: end-game megastructures housing thousands
  • SCURK (SC Urban Renewal Kit) — tile editor for custom buildings
  • Power grid modeling: individual buildings could lose power
🌆
1999
SimCity 3000
1999 Will Wright Maxis / EA

The first SimCity published by Electronic Arts after their acquisition of Maxis. SimCity 3000 refined the 2000 formula with larger cities, a more detailed advisor system, improved terrain, and neighbor deals — trade agreements with adjacent cities for water, power, and garbage disposal. The animations and soundtrack were a step forward.

It was a refinement rather than a reinvention, and players appreciated that. The Unlimited edition added biome-based tilesets and additional buildings, keeping the game fresh years after release.

  • Neighbor deals: buy and sell resources to adjacent cities
  • Waste management as a separate system
  • Three architectural eras: classic, modern, futuristic
  • Petitions: citizens lobby for specific changes
🌃
2003
SimCity 4
2003 Ocean Quigley Maxis / EA Community Peak

The most sophisticated SimCity ever made — and one of the greatest city builders in the history of the genre. SimCity 4 introduced full 3D, regional play across multiple interconnected cities, a day/night cycle, detailed commute simulation, and a modding system that spawned one of gaming's most dedicated communities.

The Rush Hour expansion added street-level traffic views, U-Drive-It vehicle control, and highway interchanges. The modding community's Network Addon Mod (NAM) is still actively updated today — adding transit types, interchanges, and mechanics that surpass the original game.

  • Regional mode: build multiple linked cities that share commuters and resources
  • Full traffic simulation — individual Sims commute along real paths
  • Day/night cycle with distinct lighting
  • The Network Addon Mod (NAM) — 20 years later, still the gold standard city-sim mod
  • Terraforming and god mode for sculpting landscapes
The EA Divergence — 2007–2013
🏘️
2007
SimCity Societies
2007 Tilted Mill / EA Not a Maxis Title

The controversial departure. SimCity Societies was developed not by Maxis but by Tilted Mill Entertainment, and it showed. The traditional zoning system was replaced with a "social energy" mechanic — cities accumulated creativity, productivity, prosperity, or other traits based on buildings placed. The simulation depth of previous games was almost entirely absent.

It was not well received. Fans rejected it as a SimCity game in name only. The series' reputation took a hit, and Maxis was noticeably absent from the credits. Societies stands as a cautionary tale about what happens when a franchise is handed to a team that doesn't understand its core appeal.

  • Social energy replaced zoning — a fundamentally different design philosophy
  • Six city archetypes: Authoritarian, Creative, Spiritual, Prosperous, etc.
  • No traffic simulation, no transit networks, no commute modeling
  • Considered non-canon by most of the community
🌉
2013
SimCity (2013)
2013 Ocean Quigley Maxis / EA Always-Online DRM

The reboot that ended an era. The 2013 SimCity was visually stunning, built on the GlassBox simulation engine which modeled individual Sims moving through cities in real time. The multi-city region system was innovative and the art direction was excellent. But two decisions poisoned it entirely.

First: always-online DRM that required a server connection to play single-player. At launch, EA's servers collapsed under the load, making the game unplayable for days. The debacle became one of gaming's most notorious launches. Second: city sizes were a fraction of SimCity 4's — a decision later revealed to be driven by multiplayer design, not technical necessity, after modders unlocked larger maps within weeks.

  • GlassBox engine: individual agent-based simulation of every Sim
  • Specializations: gambling, electronics, culture, drilling, mining
  • Multiplayer region play across connected cities
  • Launch server failure — unplayable for days, refunds issued
  • City sizes artificially constrained — the community never forgave it
Mobile & Legacy
📱
2014
SimCity BuildIt
2014 EA Mobile — iOS / Android

EA's mobile interpretation of SimCity, free-to-play with optional purchases. BuildIt strips the series to its most accessible form: place zones, collect resources from buildings, fulfill citizen requests. The core loop is more casual management than urban simulation — production chains, trade, and expanding districts in a compact format.

It has been consistently profitable for EA and continues to receive updates, making it the most actively maintained SimCity product. For many players' first introduction to city building on mobile, it succeeds on its own terms even if it shares little with its PC predecessors in depth or philosophy.

  • Free-to-play with cosmetic and speed-up purchases
  • Production chains: factories supply goods for commercial zones
  • Contest of Mayors: competitive events against other players
  • Seasonal themes and limited-time content
Will Wright — The Visionary

The Man Who Invented a Genre

William Ralph Wright · Born January 20, 1960 · Atlanta, Georgia

Will Wright is the most original game designer in the history of the medium. In a field crowded with war games, platformers, and shooters, Wright spent his career asking a different question: what if the experience itself — the act of observing a complex system respond to your inputs — was enough? What if a game didn't need a win condition, an enemy to defeat, or a story to tell?

The answer was SimCity, The Sims, and Spore — three of the most influential games ever made, each one redefining what games could be. Wright didn't iterate on existing genres. He invented new ones from scratch, repeatedly, across three decades.

Origins: The Crash That Became SimCity

1984 — Raid on Bungeling Bay

Wright's first commercial game was Raid on Bungeling Bay (1984) — a helicopter combat game for the Commodore 64. It was competent. Unremarkable. But while building it, Wright noticed something: creating the maps and towns was more engaging than playing the game itself. The editor was the fun part.

He kept the editor. Stripped out the helicopters. Added zoning rules, traffic simulation, and a city budget. What remained had no enemies, no win state, and no way to lose in any conventional sense. Publishers told him no one would buy a game you couldn't win. He pitched it for years before Broderbund finally took a chance. SimCity shipped in 1989 and changed everything.

The Key Insight

Wright understood something that the games industry had missed: the human brain finds complex systems intrinsically rewarding to observe and manipulate. The satisfaction of watching a city grow in response to your decisions is its own form of play — not competition, not narrative, but the pleasure of understanding a system well enough to make it thrive. He called it a "software toy" rather than a game, and the distinction mattered enormously.

The Sims: Turning Inward

2000 — The Best-Selling PC Game of All Time

After SimCity's success, Wright spent the 1990s thinking about a different kind of simulation. Cities were one level of human organization — what about the level below? What about the simulation of individual domestic life?

The Sims launched in 2000 and became the best-selling PC game series of all time. It applied Wright's same philosophy — observable complex system, no win condition, emergent behavior from simple rules — but turned the lens inward, onto the household rather than the city. The result resonated with audiences who had never played a "game" before: people who wanted a dollhouse with physics, a drama generator, a life simulator.

Demographics Shattered

The Sims was the first major game franchise where women constituted the majority of the audience. Wright had designed not a game but a creative sandbox that appealed to human curiosity regardless of gender or gaming background. The industry noticed. The lesson — that simulation and creative tools could reach audiences that combat and competition never would — permanently altered game development strategy at every major studio.

Spore: The Most Ambitious Toy Ever Made

2008 — Procedural Evolution Across Five Stages

Wright's final major project at Maxis was Spore — an attempt to compress the entire arc of biological and civilizational evolution into a single game. Players guided a species from single-celled organism through creature, tribe, civilization, and space-faring stages. Each stage was a different genre entirely: action, strategy, city-building, RTS, space exploration.

The ambition was breathtaking. The procedural creature animation engine — which could take any arbitrary shape and make it walk, swim, and fly believably — remains a technical achievement that has never been fully replicated. The promise of a procedurally generated galaxy populated by creatures made by millions of players worldwide anticipated what games would spend the next decade reaching for.

The Gap Between Vision and Product

Spore was critically praised but commercially divisive. Its deep simulation systems — years in development — were simplified late in production to broaden the audience. Players who had followed its development for years felt the final product fell short of its potential. It is a game that is simultaneously a masterwork of creative vision and a demonstration of how hard it is to execute Wright's ideas at scale without compromising them.

Philosophy: Toys, Not Games

The Design Language of Will Wright

Wright has spoken extensively about the distinction between "games" — which have explicit goals, win/lose states, and competition — and "toys," which exist simply to be played with. SimCity, The Sims, and Spore are all toys in this sense. The player sets their own objectives. Failure is optional. The system rewards engagement, not performance.

This philosophy runs against the grain of most game design, which is built around challenge, mastery, and feedback loops reinforcing progress toward a defined objective. Wright argued that the absence of a win condition is itself a design choice — one that frees the player to find their own meaning in the system.

Mental Models

Wright's other central design principle is the mental model: a game should teach the player to build an accurate model of its simulation in their head. The more accurate the model, the more effective the player — and the more satisfying the experience. A city that responds predictably to your decisions is rewarding precisely because you can understand why. This is why SimCity's disasters, which violated the model, were so memorable: they were the moment the system surprised you.

Legacy

Wright left EA in 2009 to found Syntertainment, focused on the intersection of games and science education. He has spoken at TED, consulted for NASA on space exploration engagement, and lectured at universities worldwide. He is one of only a handful of game designers whose name alone is used to market a product — because his name means something specific: a game that will make you think differently about systems, simulation, and what play actually is.

The SimCity Influence

How One Game Reshaped an Industry

SimCity's direct descendants include every city builder, colony sim, and management game made in the thirty-five years since its release. Tropico, Cities: Skylines, Anno, Frostpunk, Dwarf Fortress, Banished, Foundation, Workers & Resources — all of them trace their lineage directly to Wright's 1989 original. The idea that a game can be built around managing a complex system — without a villain, without a level to complete — is his contribution to the medium.

Indirectly, his influence reaches further. The emergent narrative of The Sims — the stories players told about their Sims without any authorial intent on Wright's part — anticipated everything from open-world sandbox games to the player-generated content economy of the 2010s and 2020s. He built systems that told stories he never wrote.

The Award Record

Wright has received the Lifetime Achievement Award from BAFTA, the Game Developers Choice Award for Lifetime Achievement, induction into the Academy of Interactive Arts & Sciences Hall of Fame, and numerous other recognitions. More meaningfully, virtually every designer working in simulation, management, or sandbox games today cites him as a foundational influence. He didn't just make great games. He invented a way of making games that others have spent decades learning to replicate.

Cities: Skylines — The Heir

The Game SimCity Should Have Been

Colossal Order · Paradox Interactive · Released March 10, 2015

When SimCity 2013 launched in ruins — plagued by mandatory online connectivity, collapsed servers, and city sizes a fraction of SimCity 4's — the city-building genre had no home. Maxis had squandered its goodwill. EA had no credible path to redemption. Into that vacuum, a 14-person Finnish studio named Colossal Order shipped Cities: Skylines for $30, and the genre found its new standard-bearer overnight.

Within a week, Skylines had sold a million copies. Within a month, it had surpassed SimCity 2013's total lifetime sales. The community that had waited years for a worthy successor to SimCity 4 had found it. Cities: Skylines didn't just fill the void left by SimCity — it made SimCity feel like a historical footnote.

What Skylines Got Right

The Lessons of SimCity's Failure, Applied

Colossal Order studied SimCity 4's community exhaustively before building Skylines. They knew what the hardcore audience wanted: large maps, real traffic simulation, mod support, and no online requirement. They delivered all four on launch day.

Scale

Where SimCity 2013 offered city tiles roughly 2km × 2km, Skylines launched with maps up to 36km². A single fully unlocked map in Skylines is larger than all of SimCity 2013's regions combined. The scale difference wasn't incremental — it was a statement about design philosophy.

Traffic

Skylines models individual vehicles traveling on real paths to real destinations. The traffic simulation is sophisticated enough that real urban planners have used the game to prototype intersection designs and study traffic flow. Road hierarchy, roundabouts, grade separation — Skylines rewards genuine urban planning knowledge in a way no SimCity ever did.

Modding

Steam Workshop integration at launch meant the modding community could build on Skylines immediately. Within months, mods had added features that surpassed the base game. The modding ecosystem became the game's greatest feature — a multiplier on Colossal Order's own work that no single studio could replicate alone.

The Expansion Record

Ten Years of DLC — Building the Complete City Sim

Cities: Skylines has received more than twenty expansions since 2015, each adding a major new system to the game. After Dark added a day/night cycle and tourism-specific zones. Snowfall introduced seasonal weather and heating infrastructure. Mass Transit overhauled public transport with ferries, blimps, cable cars, and monorails. Industries added detailed production chains from raw resources to consumer goods. Campus, Green Cities, Parklife, and Natural Disasters each introduced complete new subsystems.

The cumulative result — base game plus expansions — is the most detailed city simulation ever shipped. No single system is modeled at the depth of a dedicated simulator, but the breadth and integration of systems produces emergent complexity that rivals anything in the genre's history.

Cities: Skylines II

The sequel launched in October 2023 to a mixed reception — performance issues and missing features at launch disappointed a community with high expectations. The development team has been transparent about the challenges and committed to a long roadmap of fixes and additions. The situation echoes, ironically, SimCity 2013's troubled launch — a reminder that even the successor to the genre is not immune to the pressures that felled the original.

The Spiritual Inheritance

What Skylines Owes to Wright's Vision

Colossal Order has always been explicit about their debt to SimCity. The core design language — zone, build infrastructure, watch the city develop, react to problems — is pure Will Wright. The satisfaction of Skylines is the satisfaction Wright identified in 1989: the pleasure of understanding a complex system well enough to make it thrive.

Where Skylines diverges is in depth and fidelity. Wright's games were toys — accessible, forgiving, focused on the pleasure of the system rather than mastery of it. Skylines trends harder toward simulation, rewarding genuine urban planning knowledge and penalizing poor road design in ways that Wright deliberately avoided. Both approaches are valid. They serve different players.

The Succession Question

Whether Cities: Skylines is SimCity's "successor" depends on what you think SimCity was. If SimCity was a simulation of urban systems, Skylines is the successor — it simulates more, better, at greater scale. If SimCity was Will Wright's software toy philosophy applied to cities — a creative sandbox with no wrong answers — then Skylines is something adjacent but distinct: it has opinions about good urban design, and it enforces them through its traffic simulation.

The truth is probably that Skylines is the closest thing to a new SimCity that the market has produced, and that it carries the torch with far more care and competence than EA ever showed after Wright left. If Wright's original dream was a city simulation that respected the player's intelligence and gave them real tools, Cities: Skylines is the most complete realization of that dream yet built.

SimCity — A History
1984
Wright Begins "Micropolis"origin
Will Wright starts building a map editor for his Commodore 64 game Raid on Bungeling Bay and realizes the city-building tool is more fun than the game. He begins developing a standalone city simulator, originally called Micropolis.
1987
Maxis Foundedcompany
Brøderbund and other major publishers pass on SimCity — too abstract, no way to win. Wright teams up with Jeff Braun and founds Maxis Software to self-publish. The scrappy two-person studio is born in Orinda, California.
1989
SimCity Releasedrelease
SimCity ships for the Macintosh and Commodore Amiga — the first commercial city-building simulation game. Critics love it; it wins multiple awards and sells 300,000 copies in its first year. A DOS version follows in 1990.
1991
SimCity for SNES — Bundled Worldwidemilestone
Nintendo bundles SimCity with the Super NES in Japan, then across Europe and the Americas. Tens of millions of players encounter their first city builder on a console. The SNES port by Nintendo adds a Dr. Wright advisor character and unique disasters including Bowser.
1993
SimCity 2000release
SimCity 2000 reimagines the series with a full isometric view, underground tunnels, highways, subways, and arcologies. The depth and visual leap are enormous — SC2000 becomes one of the best-selling PC games of its era and defines the "god game" genre for a generation.
1993
Maxis IPOcompany
Maxis goes public on the NASDAQ, riding the success of SimCity 2000. The IPO raises $40 million and the company expands to over 200 employees. Wright is now a celebrity game designer — a rare thing in an era before gaming personalities.
1999
SimCity 3000release
SimCity 3000 adds waste management, neighboring cities to trade with, larger maps, and a lush new art style. Critics praise it as the most fully realized city sim yet — though some note the four-year wait between SC2000 and SC3000 tested fan patience.
2000
EA Acquires Maxiscompany
Electronic Arts buys Maxis for $125 million in stock. Wright stays on as a creative lead. EA pushes the studio hard on The Sims, which Wright had been quietly developing — it ships the same year and becomes the best-selling PC game franchise of all time.
2003
SimCity 4release
SimCity 4 introduces Regions — a grid of interconnected city tiles simulated in real time. Citizens (MySimCity) commute between tiles. The Rush Hour expansion adds bridges, highways, and a street-level camera. Still widely played today via the NAM modding community.
2008
Will Wright Departs EAcompany
After shipping Spore — a massively ambitious evolution game that under-delivered on its promises — Wright leaves EA and Maxis to found Stupid Fun Club, a think tank for experimental entertainment. The soul of SimCity walks out the door with him.
2013
SimCity (2013) — Launch Disasterdisaster
EA and Maxis reboot SimCity with a modern engine, beautiful graphics, and a GlassBox simulation — then require always-online DRM. The launch is catastrophic: servers collapse under demand for days. EA offers free games as apology. The tiny 2 km² city size and online requirement draw widespread fury. Reviewers pull scores.
2014
Offline Mode Patch & Maxis Emeryville Closedclosure
EA quietly ships an offline mode for SimCity 2013 — proving the always-online requirement had always been a DRM decision, not a technical one. Months later, EA shuts down Maxis Emeryville entirely. 175 staff are laid off. The studio that created SimCity ceases to exist.
2015
Cities: Skylines — The Torch Passesmilestone
Colossal Order's Cities: Skylines launches on Steam for $30. Built by a team of 12 people, it launches with bigger maps, real traffic simulation, and full mod support. It sells 500,000 copies in its first week — the city-building crown passes to a new pretender.
2024
SimCity BuildIt Still Active; Legacy Endureslegacy
EA's mobile spinoff SimCity BuildIt remains active with tens of millions of installs. SimCity 4 still receives community patches and mods via the Network Addon Mod project — 20 years on. Micropolis, Wright's original source code, is open-sourced under the GPL.
