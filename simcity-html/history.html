GAMES WILL WRIGHT CITIES: SKYLINES HISTORY
SimCity — A History
1984
Wright Begins "Micropolis"origin
Will Wright starts building a map editor for his Commodore 64 game Raid on Bungeling Bay and realizes the city-building tool is more fun than the game. He begins developing a standalone city simulator, originally called Micropolis.
1987
Maxis Foundedcompany
Brøderbund and other major publishers pass on SimCity — too abstract, no way to win. Wright teams up with Jeff Braun and founds Maxis Software to self-publish. The scrappy two-person studio is born in Orinda, California.
1989
SimCity Releasedrelease
SimCity ships for the Macintosh and Commodore Amiga — the first commercial city-building simulation game. Critics love it; it wins multiple awards and sells 300,000 copies in its first year. A DOS version follows in 1990.
1991
SimCity for SNES — Bundled Worldwidemilestone
Nintendo bundles SimCity with the Super NES in Japan, then across Europe and the Americas. Tens of millions of players encounter their first city builder on a console. The SNES port by Nintendo adds a Dr. Wright advisor character and unique disasters including Bowser.
1993
SimCity 2000release
SimCity 2000 reimagines the series with a full isometric view, underground tunnels, highways, subways, and arcologies. The depth and visual leap are enormous — SC2000 becomes one of the best-selling PC games of its era and defines the "god game" genre for a generation.
1993
Maxis IPOcompany
Maxis goes public on the NASDAQ, riding the success of SimCity 2000. The IPO raises $40 million and the company expands to over 200 employees. Wright is now a celebrity game designer — a rare thing in an era before gaming personalities.
1999
SimCity 3000release
SimCity 3000 adds waste management, neighboring cities to trade with, larger maps, and a lush new art style. Critics praise it as the most fully realized city sim yet — though some note the four-year wait between SC2000 and SC3000 tested fan patience.
2000
EA Acquires Maxiscompany
Electronic Arts buys Maxis for $125 million in stock. Wright stays on as a creative lead. EA pushes the studio hard on The Sims, which Wright had been quietly developing — it ships the same year and becomes the best-selling PC game franchise of all time.
2003
SimCity 4release
SimCity 4 introduces Regions — a grid of interconnected city tiles simulated in real time. Citizens (MySimCity) commute between tiles. The Rush Hour expansion adds bridges, highways, and a street-level camera. Still widely played today via the NAM modding community.
2008
Will Wright Departs EAcompany
After shipping Spore — a massively ambitious evolution game that under-delivered on its promises — Wright leaves EA and Maxis to found Stupid Fun Club, a think tank for experimental entertainment. The soul of SimCity walks out the door with him.
2013
SimCity (2013) — Launch Disasterdisaster
EA and Maxis reboot SimCity with a modern engine, beautiful graphics, and a GlassBox simulation — then require always-online DRM. The launch is catastrophic: servers collapse under demand for days. EA offers free games as apology. The tiny 2 km² city size and online requirement draw widespread fury. Reviewers pull scores.
2014
Offline Mode Patch & Maxis Emeryville Closedclosure
EA quietly ships an offline mode for SimCity 2013 — proving the always-online requirement had always been a DRM decision, not a technical one. Months later, EA shuts down Maxis Emeryville entirely. 175 staff are laid off. The studio that created SimCity ceases to exist.
2015
Cities: Skylines — The Torch Passesmilestone
Colossal Order's Cities: Skylines launches on Steam for $30. Built by a team of 12 people, it launches with bigger maps, real traffic simulation, and full mod support. It sells 500,000 copies in its first week — the city-building crown passes to a new pretender.
2024
SimCity BuildIt Still Active; Legacy Endureslegacy
EA's mobile spinoff SimCity BuildIt remains active with tens of millions of installs. SimCity 4 still receives community patches and mods via the Network Addon Mod project — 20 years on. Micropolis, Wright's original source code, is open-sourced under the GPL.
