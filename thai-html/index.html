Thai Food

The five regions of Thailand — aromatic, spicy, and impossibly balanced

Regional Thai Cuisine — Four distinct cooking traditions

Thailand's geography divides it into four culinary regions, each shaped by climate, neighboring countries, local ingredients, and cultural heritage. Bangkok's Central cuisine has become internationally dominant, but it represents only one of four very different traditions.

Central Region (Bangkok)

Refined · Coconut-Rich · Royal Tradition

Birthplace of royal Thai cuisine — elaborate presentations, balanced sweet-sour-salty flavors, heavy use of coconut milk. Central cuisine is the "default" Thai food known internationally. Pad Thai, green curry, tom kha gai, and massaman curry all originate here or were standardized here.

  • Moderate heat — heat is balanced with sweetness
  • Coconut milk in most curries and soups
  • Jasmine rice as the standard accompaniment
  • Signature: pad thai, green curry, tom yum, khao man gai

Northern Thailand (Lanna)

Milder · Herb-Heavy · Myanmar & Laos Influence

Chiang Mai is the culinary capital. Northern cooking is milder, less sweet, with more use of dry spices and bitter herbs. Khao soi — coconut curry broth with egg noodles — is the region's crown jewel. Sticky rice replaces jasmine rice. Northern curries use no coconut milk (unlike the south).

  • Khao soi (coconut curry noodle soup)
  • Sai oua (northern Thai sausage with lemongrass and galangal)
  • Larb muang (minced meat salad with toasted rice powder)
  • Sticky rice (khao niao) as the staple carbohydrate

Northeastern Thailand (Isaan)

Bold · Fermented · Laos-Influenced

Isaan is Thailand's largest region and the source of some of its most distinctive flavors. Fermented fish (pla ra), intense funk, sticky rice, and extreme heat define the tradition. Som tum (green papaya salad) originated in Isaan. The food is rustic, honest, and does not compromise on flavor.

  • Som tum (green papaya salad with pla ra and dried shrimp)
  • Larb (minced meat salad with toasted rice, fish sauce, lime)
  • Gai yang (grilled chicken with sticky rice and jeow dipping sauce)
  • Tom saep (spicy and sour pork rib soup with galangal)

Southern Thailand

Very Spicy · Seafood-Heavy · Malaysian Influence

Southern Thai food is the most intense in the country — very spicy, pungent from shrimp paste and fermented fish, heavy on fresh turmeric, and deeply reliant on seafood. Malaysian and Indonesian influences bring longer spice lists. Khao yam (herb rice salad) is a uniquely southern breakfast.

  • Gaeng tai pla (intensely funky fermented fish kidney curry)
  • Khua kling (dry stir-fried minced meat with intense spice paste)
  • Satay with peanut sauce (Malaysian border influence)
  • Fresh turmeric in nearly every dish
