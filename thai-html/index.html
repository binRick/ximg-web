Thai Food

The five regions of Thailand — aromatic, spicy, and impossibly balanced

Regional Thai Cuisine — Four distinct cooking traditions

Thailand's geography divides it into four culinary regions, each shaped by climate, neighboring countries, local ingredients, and cultural heritage. Bangkok's Central cuisine has become internationally dominant, but it represents only one of four very different traditions.

Central Region (Bangkok)

Refined · Coconut-Rich · Royal Tradition

Birthplace of royal Thai cuisine — elaborate presentations, balanced sweet-sour-salty flavors, heavy use of coconut milk. Central cuisine is the "default" Thai food known internationally. Pad Thai, green curry, tom kha gai, and massaman curry all originate here or were standardized here.

  • Moderate heat — heat is balanced with sweetness
  • Coconut milk in most curries and soups
  • Jasmine rice as the standard accompaniment
  • Signature: pad thai, green curry, tom yum, khao man gai

Northern Thailand (Lanna)

Milder · Herb-Heavy · Myanmar & Laos Influence

Chiang Mai is the culinary capital. Northern cooking is milder, less sweet, with more use of dry spices and bitter herbs. Khao soi — coconut curry broth with egg noodles — is the region's crown jewel. Sticky rice replaces jasmine rice. Northern curries use no coconut milk (unlike the south).

  • Khao soi (coconut curry noodle soup)
  • Sai oua (northern Thai sausage with lemongrass and galangal)
  • Larb muang (minced meat salad with toasted rice powder)
  • Sticky rice (khao niao) as the staple carbohydrate

Northeastern Thailand (Isaan)

Bold · Fermented · Laos-Influenced

Isaan is Thailand's largest region and the source of some of its most distinctive flavors. Fermented fish (pla ra), intense funk, sticky rice, and extreme heat define the tradition. Som tum (green papaya salad) originated in Isaan. The food is rustic, honest, and does not compromise on flavor.

  • Som tum (green papaya salad with pla ra and dried shrimp)
  • Larb (minced meat salad with toasted rice, fish sauce, lime)
  • Gai yang (grilled chicken with sticky rice and jeow dipping sauce)
  • Tom saep (spicy and sour pork rib soup with galangal)

Southern Thailand

Very Spicy · Seafood-Heavy · Malaysian Influence

Southern Thai food is the most intense in the country — very spicy, pungent from shrimp paste and fermented fish, heavy on fresh turmeric, and deeply reliant on seafood. Malaysian and Indonesian influences bring longer spice lists. Khao yam (herb rice salad) is a uniquely southern breakfast.

  • Gaeng tai pla (intensely funky fermented fish kidney curry)
  • Khua kling (dry stir-fried minced meat with intense spice paste)
  • Satay with peanut sauce (Malaysian border influence)
  • Fresh turmeric in nearly every dish

Iconic Thai Dishes — The essential list

Pad Thai
ผัดไทย
Stir-Fried Noodles
Rice noodles stir-fried with egg, tofu or shrimp, bean sprouts, green onions, in a tamarind-fish sauce-palm sugar sauce. Finished with crushed peanuts and lime. Thailand's most famous export — created/promoted as a national dish in the 1940s.
Green Curry
แกงเขียวหวาน
Curry · Coconut
Green curry paste (fresh green chiles, lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, basil) in coconut milk with chicken, eggplant, and Thai basil. The color comes from fresh green chiles. Fragrant, spicy, and sweet — perhaps the most aromatic Thai curry.
Tom Yum
ต้มยำ
Hot & Sour Soup
Thailand's signature soup — lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaves, fish sauce, lime juice, chili in a clear or milky broth. Tom yum kung (shrimp) is the classic. Intensely aromatic, simultaneously sour, spicy, and salty.
Som Tum
ส้มตำ
Papaya Salad
Shredded green papaya pounded in a mortar with lime, fish sauce, palm sugar, chili, garlic, tomatoes, and dried shrimp. Isaan origin. The pounding releases the papaya's cells for better sauce absorption. Can contain fermented pla ra (funk level: extreme).
Massaman Curry
แกงมัสมั่น
Mild · Complex
Thailand's most complex curry — influenced by Persian and Malay traders. Dried spices (cardamom, cinnamon, star anise, nutmeg) in coconut milk with potatoes, peanuts, and beef. Mild heat, deeply aromatic. Named best dish in the world by CNN Travel in 2011.
Pad See Ew
ผัดซีอิ๊ว
Wide Noodles
Wide flat rice noodles (sen yai) stir-fried over very high heat with soy sauce, oyster sauce, egg, Chinese broccoli, and chicken or beef. Wok hei (breath of the wok) is essential — the charred smokiness of a ripping-hot wok.
Larb
ลาบ
Minced Meat Salad
Minced chicken, pork, beef, or duck dressed with fish sauce, lime, chili, and toasted rice powder (khao khua) — ground toasted rice that adds nutty flavor and texture. Served with raw herbs, cabbage, and sticky rice. Isaan origin, now national.
Khao Man Gai
ข้าวมันไก่
Poached Chicken Rice
Thai-style Hainanese chicken rice — poached chicken served on rice cooked in the chicken broth, with sliced cucumber, a clear soup, and a pungent ginger-garlic-soybean dipping sauce. The sauce is the differentiator. Comfort food for millions of Thais daily.
Mango Sticky Rice
ข้าวเหนียวมะม่วง
Dessert
Glutinous sticky rice steamed and sweetened with coconut milk and sugar, served with ripe Thai mango and a drizzle of salted coconut cream. Seasonal (April–June when mangoes peak). The best version uses Nam Dok Mai mango.
Tom Kha Gai
ต้มข่าไก่
Coconut Galangal Soup
Chicken soup in coconut milk with galangal (kha), lemongrass, kaffir lime leaves, mushrooms, and fish sauce. Milder than tom yum but deeply aromatic. The galangal is the defining flavor — earthier, pinier, less sharp than ginger.

Thai Curries — Paste, coconut, and complexity

Thai curry paste is made fresh by pounding aromatics in a mortar — not from powder. The color of the paste indicates the chili used, which determines the heat level and flavor profile. Each paste has a different combination of aromatics rooted in centuries of tradition.

Green Curry (Gaeng Khiao Wan)

Paste: fresh green bird's eye chiles, lemongrass, galangal, shallots, garlic, kaffir lime zest, coriander root. In coconut milk with Thai eggplant, bamboo shoots, and Thai basil. The hottest of the three major curries — fresh chiles deliver sharper heat than dried.

Red Curry (Gaeng Phet)

Paste: dried red chiles (less sharp, more fruity than green), lemongrass, galangal, shallots, garlic, shrimp paste. Versatile — pairs with chicken, beef, duck, pork, or tofu. Red curry duck with lychee is a beloved restaurant presentation. Richer, more complex than green.

Yellow Curry (Gaeng Garee)

Influenced by Indian cuisine — turmeric, yellow curry powder, dried red chiles, lemongrass, and coconut milk. Mildest Thai curry. Often with potatoes and onion alongside chicken. Sweeter, earthier, more accessible to those new to Thai food.

Massaman Curry

Muslim-influenced curry paste: dried red chiles, roasted whole spices (cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, star anise, cumin, nutmeg, mace). Coconut milk, palm sugar, tamarind, potatoes, peanuts. The deepest, most complex Thai curry — mellow heat with extraordinary aromatic depth.

Panang Curry

A drier, richer red curry variant. Ground peanuts in the paste (differentiator). Less coconut milk than standard red curry — thicker, more concentrated. Finished with kaffir lime leaves sliced thin on top. Beef panang is the classic; the sauce coats the meat densely.

Khao Soi

Northern Thailand's signature — a red/yellow curry paste–based coconut broth with egg noodles, served with both soft braised noodles in the soup AND crispy fried noodles on top. Two textures, one bowl. Chicken or beef. Topped with shallots, pickled mustard greens, and chili oil.

Thai Noodle Dishes

Thailand's noodle culture was significantly influenced by Chinese immigrants from the 19th century onward. The noodle types — rice, glass, egg — and the cooking methods (stir-fried, braised in broth, cold dressed) span the full range of Thai cuisine's Chinese and indigenous influences.

Pad Thai
ผัดไทย
Rice Noodles · Stir-Fried
Thin rice noodles (sen lek) stir-fried with tamarind paste, fish sauce, palm sugar, egg, bean sprouts, and your choice of protein. Finished at the table with lime, peanuts, chili flakes, and fish sauce — adjust to taste. Deceptively complex when made with fresh tamarind.
Pad See Ew
ผัดซีอิ๊ว
Wide Rice Noodles
Flat wide rice noodles (sen yai) in dark soy and oyster sauce, with egg and Chinese broccoli (gai lan). Must be cooked in a screaming-hot wok for proper char. Slightly sweet-savory, smoky. One of the most satisfying stir-fried noodle dishes in Thai cuisine.
Pad Woon Sen
ผัดวุ้นเส้น
Glass Noodles
Cellophane (mung bean starch) noodles stir-fried with egg, vegetables, oyster sauce, and soy sauce. The glass noodles absorb the sauce completely, becoming slippery and intensely flavored. Often made with minced pork, crab, or shrimp.
Khao Soi
ข้าวซอย
Egg Noodles · Curry Broth
Northern specialty: egg noodles in a coconut-curry broth. Two textures — soft braised noodles below and crispy fried noodles on top. The contrast of soft/crispy with the rich broth is the genius of the dish. Unavoidable in Chiang Mai.
Boat Noodles
ก๋วยเตี๋ยวเรือ
Dark Broth · Small Bowls
Small bowls of thin noodles in an intensely dark, spiced broth made with pork or beef blood, five-spice, cinnamon, and star anise. Originally sold from boats in Bangkok's canals. The broth is deeply savory and slightly sweet — ordered by the bowlful, stacked up.
Rad Na
ราดหน้า
Wide Noodles · Gravy
Wide rice noodles topped with a thick, gelatinous oyster sauce-based gravy with Chinese broccoli and pork or seafood. The thick sauce coats the noodles completely. Different from pad see ew — rad na is braised, not stir-fried at high heat.

Herbs, Aromatics & Seasonings

Thai cuisine achieves its characteristic fragrance through a palette of fresh aromatics that are rarely cooked down — they're added at the last moment or bruised raw. The balance of these aromatics is what distinguishes Thai food from all other Southeast Asian cuisines.

Thai Basil (Horapa)

โหระพา

Sweet, anise-like, with a slight licorice note. Completely different from Italian basil. Added at the last moment to stir-fries and curries. Wilts instantly in heat — don't overcook. Used in pad kra pao (holy basil stir-fry) with holy basil's spicier cousin.

Holy Basil (Kra Pao)

กะเพรา

Peppery, clove-like, slightly medicinal. The basil of pad kra pao — the dish is named after it. More intense than Thai basil; can handle higher heat. Hard to find fresh outside Thailand — Thai basil is often substituted internationally.

Kaffir Lime Leaves

ใบมะกรูด

Distinctive double-lobed leaves with an intensely citrusy, floral aroma. Used whole in soups (tom yum, tom kha), sliced thin as garnish. The zest of the kaffir lime is used in curry pastes. Irreplaceable — nothing else provides this specific fragrance.

Lemongrass (Takrai)

ตะไคร้

Fibrous stalks with a sharp lemony-floral scent. Bruised and added to soups, or finely minced into curry pastes. The lower white portion is eaten; the green upper stalks are discarded after infusing. Essential in tom yum and all major Thai curry pastes.

Galangal (Kha)

ข่า

Looks like ginger but tastes completely different — piney, citrusy, slightly medicinal. Sliced and simmered in tom kha gai. Cannot be substituted with ginger — the flavor profiles are entirely distinct. The Thai name for galangal (kha) gave "tom kha" its name.

Thai Chili (Prik Kee Noo)

พริกขี้หนู

"Mouse dropping chili" — tiny bird's eye chiles with ferocious heat. Used fresh in salads, stir-fries, and curry pastes. Fermented in fish sauce as prik nam pla — the essential Thai table condiment. Don't underestimate their size-to-heat ratio.

Fish Sauce (Nam Pla)

น้ำปลา

Fermented fish and salt — the primary salt seasoning of Thai cuisine. Adds umami depth and salinity. The Thai equivalent of soy sauce but fundamentally different in profile — more complex, oceanic. Quality varies enormously; premium fish sauce is aged 12–24 months.

Oyster Sauce

ซอสหอยนางรม

Chinese-derived condiment common in Thai stir-fries. Thick, sweet-savory, viscous. Used in pad see ew, morning glory stir-fry, and Chinese-Thai fusion dishes. Provides body and glaze to stir-fries without the sharpness of fish sauce.

Tamarind (Makham)

มะขาม

The souring agent of central Thai cuisine — thick, fruity, complex sourness with a slight sweetness. Used in pad thai sauce, massaman curry, tamarind-based soups. Sold as a paste; must be dissolved in warm water and strained. More rounded than lime juice.

Coconut Milk

กะทิ

The fat-and-liquid base of central and southern Thai curries. The cream (top layer of unshaken coconut milk) is used to fry curry paste first — a critical technique. Fresh coconut milk is incomparably richer than canned. The fat level determines the curry's richness.

Shrimp Paste (Kapi)

กะปิ

Fermented ground shrimp, salted and dried — intensely pungent, salty, umami-rich. Used in small amounts in curry pastes and sauces. The backbone of many Thai and Southeast Asian flavor bases. Roasted before use in some applications to develop sweeter, nuttier notes.

Palm Sugar (Nam Tan Peep)

น้ำตาลปีป

Less refined than white sugar — mild, slightly caramel, with a hint of coconut. Used to balance the heat of chiles and saltiness of fish sauce. The four-flavor balance of Thai cooking (spicy, sour, salty, sweet) requires a sugar that integrates rather than overwhelms.

Street Food — Thailand's open kitchen

Thai street food culture is unmatched in Asia — arguably the world. Bangkok's street stalls and night markets operate as a decentralized restaurant ecosystem: specialists who do one thing perfectly for decades. Michelin has awarded Bib Gourmands and even stars to Bangkok street stalls.

Moo Ping
หมูปิ้ง
Pork Skewers
Grilled pork skewers marinated in coconut milk, fish sauce, palm sugar, garlic, and coriander root. The coconut milk caramelizes on the grill for a slightly charred, sweet surface. Sold from carts at dawn — the ultimate Thai breakfast with sticky rice.
Pad Kra Pao
ผัดกะเพรา
Holy Basil Stir-Fry
Minced pork or chicken stir-fried at maximum heat with oyster sauce, fish sauce, Thai chili, garlic, and holy basil. Topped with a crispy fried egg (kai dao). Thailand's most-ordered dish. Every Thai person has eaten this hundreds of times.
Roti
โรตี
Thai-Muslim Flatbread
Flaky, crispy-edged flatbread pan-fried in clarified butter on a flat griddle. Eaten savory with massaman curry, or sweet with banana and condensed milk. Southern Thailand's street snack — a South Asian influence through Muslim trading routes.
Satay (Sateh)
สะเต๊ะ
Grilled Skewers
Skewered and grilled meat (chicken or pork) marinated in turmeric and lemongrass. Served with peanut dipping sauce and ajad (pickled cucumber-chili relish). Malaysian-Thai border tradition. The peanut sauce is made fresh, not from a jar.
Khao Niao Mamuang
ข้าวเหนียวมะม่วง
Mango Sticky Rice
Seasoned glutinous rice with ripe mango and salted coconut cream. Street cart specialty at its peak in April–June. The salted coconut cream drizzle is critical — without it, the dish is too sweet. A perfect dessert.
Gai Tod (Fried Chicken)
ไก่ทอด
Crispy Fried Chicken
Thai fried chicken marinated in lemongrass, garlic, coriander root, and fish sauce. Fried until shatteringly crispy. Served with sticky rice and a sweet chili-garlic dipping sauce. Incomparably better than Western fried chicken due to the aromatic marinade.
Kanom Krok
ขนมครก
Coconut Rice Pancakes
Small round coconut milk and rice flour pancakes cooked in a special dimpled cast iron pan. Crispy exterior, soft and custardy interior. Sweet and slightly salty version sold simultaneously. A purely Thai street snack with no international equivalent.
Tod Mun Pla
ทอดมันปลา
Fish Cakes
Ground fish mixed with red curry paste, kaffir lime leaves, and green beans, formed into flat patties and deep-fried until crispy. Served with sweet chili sauce and crushed peanuts. One of the most distinctive Thai appetizers — the curry paste in the fish cake is the revelation.
Hoi Tod
หอยทอด
Oyster Omelette
Fresh oysters mixed with starch batter and egg, fried on a screaming-hot griddle until crispy-edged and slightly gooey in the center. Topped with bean sprouts and served with sriracha. A Bangkok street specialty that requires the right amount of heat and a confident flip.
Khao Tom
ข้าวต้ม
Rice Congee
Thai-style rice porridge with ginger, garlic, and your choice of minced pork, fish, or shrimp. A late-night comfort food eaten after midnight at Bangkok street stalls. Garnished with fried garlic, fresh ginger threads, and white pepper.
