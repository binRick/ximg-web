Herbs, Aromatics & Seasonings

Thai cuisine achieves its characteristic fragrance through a palette of fresh aromatics that are rarely cooked down — they're added at the last moment or bruised raw. The balance of these aromatics is what distinguishes Thai food from all other Southeast Asian cuisines.

Thai Basil (Horapa)

โหระพา

Sweet, anise-like, with a slight licorice note. Completely different from Italian basil. Added at the last moment to stir-fries and curries. Wilts instantly in heat — don't overcook. Used in pad kra pao (holy basil stir-fry) with holy basil's spicier cousin.

Holy Basil (Kra Pao)

กะเพรา

Peppery, clove-like, slightly medicinal. The basil of pad kra pao — the dish is named after it. More intense than Thai basil; can handle higher heat. Hard to find fresh outside Thailand — Thai basil is often substituted internationally.

Kaffir Lime Leaves

ใบมะกรูด

Distinctive double-lobed leaves with an intensely citrusy, floral aroma. Used whole in soups (tom yum, tom kha), sliced thin as garnish. The zest of the kaffir lime is used in curry pastes. Irreplaceable — nothing else provides this specific fragrance.

Lemongrass (Takrai)

ตะไคร้

Fibrous stalks with a sharp lemony-floral scent. Bruised and added to soups, or finely minced into curry pastes. The lower white portion is eaten; the green upper stalks are discarded after infusing. Essential in tom yum and all major Thai curry pastes.

Galangal (Kha)

ข่า

Looks like ginger but tastes completely different — piney, citrusy, slightly medicinal. Sliced and simmered in tom kha gai. Cannot be substituted with ginger — the flavor profiles are entirely distinct. The Thai name for galangal (kha) gave "tom kha" its name.

Thai Chili (Prik Kee Noo)

พริกขี้หนู

"Mouse dropping chili" — tiny bird's eye chiles with ferocious heat. Used fresh in salads, stir-fries, and curry pastes. Fermented in fish sauce as prik nam pla — the essential Thai table condiment. Don't underestimate their size-to-heat ratio.

Fish Sauce (Nam Pla)

น้ำปลา

Fermented fish and salt — the primary salt seasoning of Thai cuisine. Adds umami depth and salinity. The Thai equivalent of soy sauce but fundamentally different in profile — more complex, oceanic. Quality varies enormously; premium fish sauce is aged 12–24 months.

Oyster Sauce

ซอสหอยนางรม

Chinese-derived condiment common in Thai stir-fries. Thick, sweet-savory, viscous. Used in pad see ew, morning glory stir-fry, and Chinese-Thai fusion dishes. Provides body and glaze to stir-fries without the sharpness of fish sauce.

Tamarind (Makham)

มะขาม

The souring agent of central Thai cuisine — thick, fruity, complex sourness with a slight sweetness. Used in pad thai sauce, massaman curry, tamarind-based soups. Sold as a paste; must be dissolved in warm water and strained. More rounded than lime juice.

Coconut Milk

กะทิ

The fat-and-liquid base of central and southern Thai curries. The cream (top layer of unshaken coconut milk) is used to fry curry paste first — a critical technique. Fresh coconut milk is incomparably richer than canned. The fat level determines the curry's richness.

Shrimp Paste (Kapi)

กะปิ

Fermented ground shrimp, salted and dried — intensely pungent, salty, umami-rich. Used in small amounts in curry pastes and sauces. The backbone of many Thai and Southeast Asian flavor bases. Roasted before use in some applications to develop sweeter, nuttier notes.

Palm Sugar (Nam Tan Peep)

น้ำตาลปีป

Less refined than white sugar — mild, slightly caramel, with a hint of coconut. Used to balance the heat of chiles and saltiness of fish sauce. The four-flavor balance of Thai cooking (spicy, sour, salty, sweet) requires a sugar that integrates rather than overwhelms.
