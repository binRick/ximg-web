From vine to glass — grapes, regions, terroir, and the art of pairing

Red Wines

Cabernet Sauvignon

The king of red wine. Full-bodied, high tannins, dark fruit (blackcurrant, blackberry, plum) with cedar, tobacco, and bell pepper notes. High structure means it ages beautifully — great Bordeaux peaks at 15–30 years. California Cabs are riper and more fruit-forward. Requires decanting when young.

Merlot

Softer, plummier sibling of Cabernet. Round tannins, medium body, red fruit (plum, cherry, raspberry) with hints of chocolate, bay leaf, and mocha. Pomerol (Château Pétrus) and Saint-Émilion produce world-class Merlot-dominant wines. Early-drinking but also ages well. Misrepresented by "Sideways" — rehabilitated.

Pinot Noir

The heartbreak grape — notoriously difficult to grow, devastatingly beautiful when right. Light body, low tannins, high acidity. Red fruit (cherry, raspberry, strawberry) with earthy, mushroom, and forest floor notes in Burgundy. Oregon Willamette Valley produces elegant, earthy styles. California wines are riper and more fruit-driven. Burgundy: the world standard.

Syrah / Shiraz

Northern Rhône (Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie): elegant, Northern cool-climate expression — dark fruit, pepper, olive, smoked meat. Australian Shiraz (Barossa): bigger, riper, jammy — dark plum, chocolate, eucalyptus. Both are the same grape with vastly different expressions. High tannins. Pepper from rotundone — the same molecule as black pepper. Pairs with lamb and game.

Malbec

Originally from Cahors (SW France) where it's called Côt. Found its spiritual home in Mendoza, Argentina where altitude (3,000–5,000 ft) and intense sunshine create deeply colored, velvety wines. Dark fruit (blueberry, blackberry, plum), violet aromas, cocoa, and tobacco. Soft tannins. Excellent value. Argentina's flagship grape and wine export.

Tempranillo

Spain's great red grape. The backbone of Rioja and Ribera del Duero. Cherry, leather, tobacco, and vanilla (from oak aging). Traditional Rioja aging categories: Crianza (1yr oak + 1yr bottle), Reserva (1yr oak + 2yr bottle), Gran Reserva (2yr oak + 3yr bottle). The long oak aging creates complex oxidative flavors. Also used in Toro and Penedès.

Sangiovese

The great Italian grape. High acidity, firm tannins, bright red cherry, tomato leaf, and earthy notes. The soul of Chianti Classico, Brunello di Montalcino (most age-worthy Italian red), and Morellino. Brunello di Montalcino (100% Sangiovese Grosso "Brunello") requires 5 years aging minimum. Chianti: fresh and food-friendly. Sangiovese's high acidity cuts through rich pasta and pizza.

Nebbiolo (Barolo & Barbaresco)

"The wine of kings, the king of wines." Barolo and Barbaresco from Piedmont, Italy. Nebbiolo has the tannins of Cabernet, the acidity of Sangiovese, and an unmistakable aroma of roses, tar, and dried cherries. Young Barolo is nearly unapproachable — needs 10+ years. Grand cru Barolo from single vineyards (Cannubi, Brunate, Serralunga) commands $100–$400+. Barbaresco is more approachable young.

White Wines

Chardonnay

The world's most planted white grape — a blank canvas that expresses terroir and winemaker technique. Unoaked (Chablis, Mâcon): crisp, mineral, green apple, citrus. Oaked (Meursault, California): butter, vanilla, hazelnut, tropical fruit. Malolactic fermentation converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid — creating the creamy, buttery texture associated with California Chardonnay.

Oaked vs unoakedChablis = mineralMLF = buttery

Sauvignon Blanc

Crisp, high-acid, aromatic. Loire Valley (Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé): flinty, herbaceous, gooseberry, citrus. New Zealand Marlborough: intensely aromatic, passionfruit, fresh-cut grass, grapefruit. Sémillon blended in Bordeaux Blanc. Pyrazines (bell pepper aroma) are the defining aromatic compound. Fermented cold in stainless steel to preserve aromatics. Drink young.

High acidMarlborough = tropicalSancerre = flinty

Riesling

One of the world's greatest white grapes — dramatically underappreciated. Can range from bone-dry (Alsace, Mosel Kabinett) to intensely sweet (Trockenbeerenauslese, Eiswein). High acidity provides structure and aging potential — great Rieslings age 20–30+ years. Floral (jasmine, white flowers), citrus, peach, petrol note (TDN compound) in aged wines. Germany's Mosel produces ethereal, low-alcohol examples.

Dry to sweetHigh acidityAges 20–30yr

Pinot Grigio / Gris

Italian Pinot Grigio: light, crisp, neutral — lemon, almond, subtle pear. Designed for easy drinking, not complexity. Often produced in massive volumes with mediocre quality. Alsatian Pinot Gris (same grape): rich, full-bodied, off-dry, honeyed peach and spice — completely different. Oregon Pinot Gris: between the two styles. The grape's skin has gray-pink color — some winemakers make orange wine from it.

Italian = lightAlsatian = richSame grape

Gewürztraminer

One of the most perfumed grapes in existence — litchi, rose, ginger, Turkish delight, jasmine, and orange blossom. Low acidity. Naturally high alcohol. Thick-skinned grapes produce a slightly pink-tinged wine. Alsace is the spiritual home. Also excellent in South Tyrol (Italy), Germany, and New Zealand. Best paired with spicy Asian food — the sweetness and aromatics complement chili heat beautifully.

Highly aromaticLitchi + roseAlsace

Albariño

Spain's great white grape, grown in Galicia's Rías Baixas region — influenced by Atlantic Ocean. High acid, low alcohol, intense peach, apricot, citrus, and saline minerality. The proximity to the sea gives a distinctive briny quality. Portugal's version is called Alvarinho (Vinho Verde region). Exceptional with seafood — particularly shellfish, white fish, and octopus. The wine of Galicia's seafood culture.

Saline mineralRías BaixasSeafood pairing

Grüner Veltliner

Austria's signature white grape. Dry, high acid, medium body with white pepper (rotundone), lime, green herbs, and pea shoot notes. The pepper spice is the defining characteristic — distinct from any other white wine. Ranges from light and simple (Steinfeder) to rich and age-worthy (Smaragd, 13%+ ABV). Excellent with asparagus and artichoke — notoriously difficult pairings for other wines.

White pepperAustriaAsparagus pairing

Viognier

Aromatic, full-bodied, low acid, typically dry white with peach, apricot, honeysuckle, and orange blossom aromas. Northern Rhône appellation Condrieu is its spiritual home. Nearly extinct in the 1960s (only 8ha planted), now cultivated globally. Often co-fermented with Syrah in Côte-Rôtie — the small addition of Viognier stabilizes color and adds aromatic complexity to the red wine. Rich texture from high glycerol.

Peach + honeysuckleCondrieuLow acid

Wine Regions

Bordeaux, France

Left Bank (Médoc): Cabernet Sauvignon-dominant blends — Pauillac (Mouton Rothschild, Latour, Lafite), Saint-Julien, Margaux. Structured, tannic, age 15–50 years. Right Bank (Pomerol, Saint-Émilion): Merlot-dominant — rounder, more approachable young. Entre-Deux-Mers and Pessac-Léognan for dry whites. The 1855 Classification (Premier to Cinquième Cru) ranks the Left Bank's best châteaux.

Cab Sauv / MerlotLeft / Right Bank1855 Classification

Burgundy (Bourgogne), France

The most terroir-focused wine region on earth. Pinot Noir (red) and Chardonnay (white) only. Hierarchical classification from regional to village to premier cru to grand cru. Grand Crus (33 in Côte d'Or): the most expensive wines in the world — DRC Romanée-Conti $10,000+/bottle. Chablis: Chardonnay of exceptional mineral purity. Côte de Nuits: reds. Côte de Beaune: whites and reds.

Pinot Noir + ChardonnayGrand CruTerroir hierarchy

Champagne, France

The only wine legally allowed to be called Champagne. Traditional method: second fermentation in the bottle creates bubbles. Chardonnay (Blanc de Blancs), Pinot Noir, Pinot Meunier. Non-vintage (NV): blended across years for house style consistency. Vintage: exceptional years only. Prestige cuvées: Dom Pérignon, Krug Grande Cuvée, Cristal. Dosage (sugar addition after disgorging) determines: Brut Nature → Extra Brut → Brut → Sec → Demi-Sec.

Traditional methodNV vs VintageDosage levels

Rhône Valley, France

Northern Rhône: Syrah reds (Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie, Crozes-Hermitage) and Viognier whites (Condrieu). Single-grape, cool-climate elegance. Southern Rhône: Châteauneuf-du-Pape (up to 18 permitted varieties, mostly Grenache-dominated blends), Gigondas, Vacqueyras. GSM blends (Grenache-Syrah-Mourvèdre) are the Southern Rhône template. Côtes du Rhône: excellent value entry point.

Northern = SyrahSouthern = GSMChâteauneuf-du-Pape

Tuscany, Italy

Home of Sangiovese. Chianti Classico DOCG: central Tuscany, food-friendly. Brunello di Montalcino DOCG: most age-worthy Italian red, 100% Sangiovese Grosso, 5 year minimum aging. Bolgheri and the "Super Tuscans" (Sassicaia, Ornellaia, Tignanello): Cabernet and Merlot blends that existed outside the DOC system, earning IGT status. Vernaccia di San Gimignano: Tuscany's notable white.

SangioveseBrunelloSuper Tuscans

Napa Valley, California

America's most prestigious wine region. 16 sub-appellations including Oakville, Rutherford, and Stags Leap. Famous for structured, age-worthy Cabernet Sauvignon and rich Chardonnay. Judgment of Paris (1976): blind tasting where California wines beat French in both categories — shocked the wine world. Cult Cabernets (Screaming Eagle, Harlan, Opus One) fetch $500–$3,000+ per bottle. Dry Creek and Alexander Valley in Sonoma offer excellent alternatives.

Cabernet SauvignonJudgment of ParisCult Cabs

Mendoza, Argentina

South America's most important wine region. High-altitude vineyards (2,500–5,000 ft above sea level) in the foothills of the Andes. Intense UV radiation and extreme diurnal temperature variation (hot days/cold nights) create concentrated, aromatic grapes with good natural acidity. Malbec dominates — deep, velvety, accessible. Luján de Cuyo and the Uco Valley are the premium sub-zones. World-class Malbec under $20 remains available.

MalbecHigh altitudeAndes

Marlborough, New Zealand

Put New Zealand on the wine map with intensely aromatic Sauvignon Blanc. Cool maritime climate, long growing season, high UV. Tropical (passionfruit, mango) and herbaceous (fresh-cut grass, capsicum) notes in a single wine. Cloudly Bay pioneered the style in the 1980s. Also excellent for cool-climate Pinot Noir and Pinot Gris. Central Otago (further south): produces New Zealand's most serious Pinot Noir in extreme continental climate.

Sauvignon BlancTropical + herbalCloudy Bay

Grape Varieties

Grenache / Garnacha

World's most widely planted red grape. High alcohol, low tannin, medium acid. Red fruit (strawberry, raspberry, dried cherry), white pepper, herbs. The foundation of Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Priorat (Spain), and Sardinian Cannonau. Old-vine Grenache from Priorat or Gigondas rivals Burgundy in complexity. Blends beautifully in the GSM template.

High alcoholLow tanninPriorat / CdP

Barbera

Piedmont's everyday red — Barbera d'Asti and Barbera d'Alba. Very high acidity (distinctive), low tannins, deep color, dark fruit. Traditionally early-drinking and rustic; modern producers use oak aging for added complexity. Never commands the price of Barolo but delivers genuine pleasure. The Piemontese's daily table wine. Pairs with everything from pizza to braised meats.

Very high acidityLow tanninsPiedmont daily wine

Chenin Blanc

The Loire Valley's great white grape. Extraordinary range: Vouvray (dry to sweet), Savennières (bone-dry, mineral masterpiece), Quarts de Chaume (sweet, noble rot). High acidity and waxy texture are the signatures. South Africa's "Steen" is Chenin Blanc — produces some of the world's best value whites. Ages magnificently — 30+ year Vouvray can be transcendent.

High acidityLoire / South AfricaDry to sweet

Mourvèdre / Monastrell

Dark, tannic, savory variety. Bandol (Provence) is its finest expression — aged in oak, black fruit, olive, game, smoked meat, iron. Needs warmth to ripen fully. Third component in GSM blends, adding structure and spice. Spain calls it Monastrell — concentrated, rustic wines from Jumilla and Yecla. Unusual iron and blood-like mineral note is unmistakable.

Dark fruitSavory / gameyBandol

Muscat

Ancient grape family — arguably the oldest cultivated grape. The most "grapey" of all varieties. Moscato d'Asti (Italy): light, sweet, low-alcohol, effervescent. Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise: fortified sweet wine, lush and floral. Alsatian Muscat: dry, aromatic. Rutherglen Muscat (Australia): intensely sweet, fortified — raisined, toffee, orange blossom — the world's finest sticky.

Grapey + floralMoscato d'AstiDiverse styles

Vermentino

Mediterranean white grape thriving in Sardinia, Corsica, and Liguria. Medium body, refreshing acidity, almond bitterness on the finish, citrus, white peach, and saline mineral notes. The saline quality reflects its coastal origins. Vermentino di Gallura DOCG is the top appellation. Excellent everyday wine for seafood and Mediterranean food. Related to Rolle (Provence) and Favorita (Piedmont).

Saline mineralSardinia / CorsicaSeafood

Food & Wine Pairing

Beef & Lamb

Beef: Cabernet Sauvignon (tannins bind to protein, softening the tannins while the fat softens the wine). Argentinian Malbec with a steak is classic. Syrah with braised beef short ribs. Lamb: classic Bordeaux pairing (Médoc), Côte du Rhône Syrah, or Rioja Reserva. The "what grows together, goes together" principle holds: Bordeaux châteaux neighbor lamb farms.

Beef = CabernetLamb = BordeauxFat softens tannins

Fish & Seafood

White wine's traditional domain. Rich fish (salmon, tuna, swordfish): oaked Chardonnay or light Pinot Noir. Delicate white fish: unoaked Chablis or Sauvignon Blanc. Oysters: Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne), Chablis Premier Cru. Shellfish: Albariño with grilled prawns or clams. Sushi: dry Riesling or Champagne Blanc de Blancs. The old "white with fish" rule has many exceptions — acid and structure are what matter.

Rich fish = ChardonnayOysters = ChablisSushi = Champagne

Pasta & Italian Food

Sangiovese with any tomato-based pasta — the high acidity mirrors and cuts through tomato acidity. Carbonara (rich, creamy): Soave or Gavi for balance. Truffle pasta: mature Barolo or white Burgundy. Pesto: Vermentino or Ligurian whites. The principle: Italian food with Italian wine almost never fails. Regional pairings developed over centuries of co-evolution.

Tomato = SangioveseCreamy = SoaveItalian with Italian

Cheese Pairings

Champagne with aged Parmesan or salty sheep cheese: effervescence cuts fat. Cabernet Sauvignon with aged Cheddar: tannins and protein interact perfectly. Sancerre with Chèvre: Sauvignon Blanc + goat cheese is the Loire's classic. Sauternes with Roquefort: the ultimate sweet-salty contrast. Port with Stilton: legendary. Avoid: tannic reds with fresh soft cheeses (tannins turn metallic).

Sancerre + ChèvreSauternes + RoquefortPort + Stilton

Spicy Food

High-alcohol and tannic wines amplify heat — avoid with very spicy food. Sweet, low-alcohol wines are the best partners: off-dry Riesling, Gewürztraminer, demi-sec Vouvray. The residual sugar and fruitiness cool the heat. Rosé (especially Provence) works with moderately spicy dishes. Sparkling wine refreshes the palate. Thai, Indian, and Sichuan cuisine: Riesling Spätlese is a revelation.

Off-dry RieslingGewürztraminerLow alcohol

Dessert Wines

Rule: wine must be sweeter than the dessert. Sauternes (Château d'Yquem) with crème brûlée, foie gras, or fruit tarts. Moscato d'Asti with fresh berries or panna cotta. Vintage Port with chocolate or walnuts. Ice wine (Eiswein) with blue cheese. Pedro Ximénez sherry poured over vanilla ice cream is a 60-second dessert. Tawny Port with pecan pie. German TBA or Beerenauslese for the ultimate luxury pairing.

Sweeter than dessertSauternes = YquemPX over ice cream

Service & Storage

Serving Temperatures

White wine served too cold loses aroma; red wine served at "room temperature" (70°F+) is too warm — it tastes flat and alcoholic. Full-bodied reds (Barolo, Cabernet): 62–65°F. Medium reds (Pinot Noir, Chianti): 60–63°F. Light reds (Beaujolais): 55–58°F. Full-bodied whites (oaked Chardonnay): 55–60°F. Light whites and rosé: 48–52°F. Sparkling and dessert wine: 43–47°F. 20 minutes in the fridge cools a red to serving temp.

Reds 60–65°FWhites 48–55°FSparkling 43–47°F

Glassware

Glass shape directs wine to specific parts of the tongue and concentrates aromas. Bordeaux glass: tall, large bowl — aerates tannic reds. Burgundy glass: wider, rounder bowl — captures delicate Pinot aromas. White wine glass: narrower bowl preserves cold temperature and floral aromas. Champagne flute: vertical column preserves bubbles and shows mousse. Riedel and Zalto are benchmark manufacturers. Machine-made glasses are adequate; hand-blown Zalto for special occasions.

Bordeaux = large bowlFlute = bubblesRiedel / Zalto

Decanting

Young tannic reds (Barolo, young Bordeaux): decant 1–4 hours to allow oxygen to open tightly wound tannins and release complex aromas. Also removes sediment in older wines. Pour slowly against a light source — stop pouring when sediment reaches the neck. Old wines (20+ years): decant briefly (30 min max) — they're fragile and can fade quickly after opening. No need to decant light reds, whites, or sparkling wine.

Young reds 1–4hrOld wines 30minRemove sediment

Storage Conditions

Ideal storage: 55°F (13°C), 60–80% humidity, darkness, no vibration, bottles horizontal (keeps cork moist). Temperature fluctuation is more damaging than a slightly elevated constant temperature. Light (especially UV) degrades wine through photo-oxidation — why wine is in tinted bottles. Vibration disturbs sediment and disrupts aging. A wine fridge at 55°F handles all of these requirements.

55°F idealHorizontal storageNo UV light

Opening Aged Wines

Old wines (15+ years) often have fragile corks. Use a two-prong "Ah-So" opener that grips the cork from both sides rather than piercing it. A corkscrew can crumble a 30-year-old cork. Have a funnel and cheesecloth ready to filter any cork fragments. Open carefully, minimize tilting. Let the wine breathe in the glass. Taste within an hour — old wines can fade or "close down" after opening.

Ah-So openerCheesecloth filterDrink within 1hr

Vintages

Climate in the growing season determines vintage quality — heat and sunshine ripen grapes; rain during harvest dilutes and causes rot. Great recent vintages: Bordeaux 2016, 2015, 2010, 2009. Burgundy 2019, 2015, 2012. Barolo 2016, 2013, 2010. Napa Cabernet: generally excellent in warm California. For everyday wine, vintage matters less than for age-worthy bottles. Robert Parker and Wine Spectator publish vintage charts.

Climate-dependentBordeaux 2016Burgundy 2019
