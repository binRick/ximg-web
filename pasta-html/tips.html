Cooking Tips — The technique behind great pasta

  • Al dente is not a preference — it's a technique. "Al dente" means "to the tooth" — the pasta should have a slight resistance when bitten, with no raw-flour center. It finishes cooking in the sauce (mantecatura). Overcooked pasta cannot be fixed.
  • Salt pasta water like the sea. The only chance to season the pasta itself is the cooking water. Use 10g of salt per liter of water (roughly 1 tablespoon per quart). It should taste seasoned, not faintly salty. Unsalted pasta water produces flat-tasting pasta regardless of the sauce.
  • Reserve pasta water before draining. One to two cups of the starchy cooking water is liquid gold — the starch emulsifies fat-based sauces, loosens thick sauces, and binds everything together. Always collect it before draining. Always.
  • Never rinse pasta after draining. Rinsing removes the surface starch that helps sauce adhere to the pasta. Cold rinsing also drops the temperature, causing the sauce to break. Drain; do not rinse; immediately add to the sauce.
  • Finish pasta in the sauce, not in the strainer. Add the slightly underdone pasta directly to the sauce in the pan. Add pasta water. Toss over high heat for 60–90 seconds. The pasta absorbs the sauce; the sauce coats the pasta. This step — mantecatura — is the difference between restaurant and home quality.
  • Match pasta thickness to sauce weight. Delicate sauces (olive oil, clams, fresh herbs) demand thin pasta. Hearty ragù demands thick pasta. A thin pasta overwhelmed by chunky bolognese is not an aesthetic preference issue — it's structural failure.
  • Fresh pasta is not always superior to dry pasta. For carbonara, cacio e pepe, and spaghetti dishes: dry pasta (bronze-die extruded, slow-dried) is correct. Fresh egg pasta is correct for tagliatelle bolognese, stuffed pastas, and Emilia-Romagna dishes. Substituting one for the other changes the dish's character.
  • Parmigiano-Reggiano and Pecorino Romano are not interchangeable. Parmesan is milder and nuttier. Pecorino is saltier, sharper, and more pungent. Roman classics (carbonara, cacio e pepe, amatriciana) call for Pecorino. Northern Italian dishes call for Parmigiano. Many chefs blend both for balance.
