Pasta

Italian pasta — shapes, sauces, and the rules that matter

Long Pasta — Strand shapes

Long pasta works best with smooth, coating sauces that cling to the strands. Chunky sauces fall off — reserve those for shapes with ridges and hollows. The thickness of the strand should match the weight of the sauce.

Spaghetti
Thin round strands
The most iconic pasta in the world. Medium-thin, round cross-section. Pairs beautifully with carbonara, cacio e pepe, aglio e olio, and simple marinara. The universal long pasta baseline.
Linguine
Flat, narrow ribbons
Slightly flattened spaghetti — creates more surface area. Traditional partner for vongole (clams). The extra surface helps coat the pasta more evenly with oil-based sauces.
Fettuccine
Flat, wide ribbons
6–7mm wide flat ribbon. Roman classic — the proper home of Alfredo (though Alfredo in Rome uses only butter and Parmigiano, no cream). Pairs with robust cream or meat ragù.
Tagliatelle
Bologna's wide ribbon
6–8mm wide, egg-enriched. The official pasta of Bologna — as decreed by the Italian Academy of Cuisine in 1972. The only correct pasta for bolognese ragù. Not spaghetti. Never.
Pappardelle
Very wide Tuscan ribbon
Wide flat ribbons 2–3cm. Tuscan in origin. Needs a hearty, robust sauce — wild boar ragù (cinghiale), slow-braised lamb, or rich mushroom. The sauce should be as bold as the pasta.
Bucatini
Hollow spaghetti
Thick spaghetti with a hole through the center. Sauce enters the tube AND coats the outside — double-flavor delivery. The traditional pasta for all'amatriciana. Satisfying, chewy, unusual.
Spaghettini
Extra thin spaghetti
Thinner than spaghetti, cooks quickly. Best with very light sauces — fresh tomato, clam juice, lemon-herb. Too delicate for chunky meat sauces; they overwhelm the strand.
Vermicelli
Very thin strands
Even thinner than spaghettini. Matches delicate seafood or light tomato broths. Common in southern Italian seafood dishes. Quick cook time — careful with your timer.

Short Pasta — Shapes designed to trap sauce

Short pasta shapes are engineered to hold chunky, hearty sauces in tubes, ridges, and cavities. The geometry is not decorative — each shape was designed to maximize the ratio of sauce to pasta in every bite.

Penne
Tubes, cut at angle
The hollow tube traps sauce inside while ridged exterior (rigate) holds sauce outside. All'arrabbiata or penne alla vodka are its classic partners. Penne rigate always preferred over penne lisce (smooth).
Rigatoni
Large ridged tubes
Larger than penne, cut straight. Deep ridges and the wide tube make it the best shape for absorbing chunky meat sauces. Rigatoni alla pajata (Roman veal intestine ragù) is its highest calling.
Fusilli
Corkscrew spirals
The spiral traps thick, chunky sauces beautifully — pesto clings to every groove, ragù wedges into every coil. Works warm or cold (pasta salad). Most versatile short pasta shape.
Farfalle
Bow ties / butterflies
Festive, visually charming. The pinched center stays slightly underdone while edges cook fully — two textures in one piece. Best with light cream sauces or pea-prosciutto combinations.
Orecchiette
Little ears (Puglia)
Thumb-pressed concave discs from Puglia. The cup shape holds chunky ingredients — the classic preparation is orecchiette with broccoli rabe and sausage. Chewy, hearty, made by hand traditionally.
Cavatappi
Double elbow spirals
Hollow, ridged spiral tube. Exceptional at holding thick cheese sauces inside and outside simultaneously. The best shape for mac and cheese according to many pasta enthusiasts.
Conchiglie
Shells
Shell-shaped pasta in various sizes (conchigliette to large conchiglioni). The shell holds chunky ingredients inside like a cup. Large versions can be stuffed and baked. Ricotta-stuffed conchiglioni is a southern Italian classic.
Paccheri
Very large smooth tubes
Enormous open tubes from Campania. Each pacchero is nearly a single bite. Best with luxurious seafood — fresh tuna, octopus, lobster — where the tube can be stuffed with a single piece.
Ziti
Straight long tubes
Long smooth tubes, often broken before cooking. The classic base for baked ziti — layered with ricotta, mozzarella, and tomato sauce. Common in Neapolitan-American cooking.

Stuffed & Filled Pasta

Stuffed pasta requires fresh egg dough thin enough to reveal the filling through the translucent skin. Each region of Italy claims a different filled pasta as its own — a matter of fierce local pride.

Ravioli
Square-cut pillows
The most universal stuffed pasta. Square or rectangular pockets of egg pasta filled with ricotta, spinach, meat, or various combinations. Served with butter-sage, cream, or light tomato. Endless regional variations.
Tortellini
Ring-shaped (Bologna/Modena)
Small rings of pasta filled with a mix of pork, prosciutto, mortadella, Parmigiano-Reggiano, and nutmeg. The filling recipe was registered with the Bologna Chamber of Commerce in 1974. Served in broth (in brodo) is the traditional and correct way.
Tortelloni
Large tortellini
Larger version of tortellini, typically filled with ricotta and spinach (or butternut squash). Less intricate than tortellini; more filling per bite. Served with butter and sage or walnut sauce.
Gnocchi
Potato dumplings
Not technically pasta — potato-based dumplings that cook quickly and float when done. Incredibly light when done well, dense and leaden when wrong. Thursday is gnocchi day in Rome (Gnocchi di giovedì).
Cannelloni
Rolled tubes, baked
Large fresh pasta sheets rolled around filling (usually ricotta-spinach or meat ragù), placed in baking dish, covered in béchamel and tomato sauce, baked. A labor-intensive Sunday dish.
Agnolotti
Piedmontese pillows
Piedmont's stuffed pasta — small square pillows filled with braised meat and vegetables. The filling is made from the leftover braised meats of the previous night's dinner. Served in the braising juices or with butter and fresh truffles.

Classic Sauces — The canon

Italian pasta sauce culture has an oral tradition of rules as strict as any legal code. Many of these rules exist for flavor reasons that become obvious once you understand them — and others exist purely because Italians are Italian.

Carbonara

Rome · Lazio

Guanciale (cured pork cheek), Pecorino Romano, eggs, and black pepper. The fat from the guanciale, emulsified with egg and pasta water, creates the "sauce." The heat of the pasta cooks the egg just enough — never scrambled. Absolutely zero cream. This rule is enforced with regional passion.

The rule: No cream, no onion, no garlic, no peas. Guanciale only — not pancetta, not bacon.

All'Amatriciana

Amatrice / Rome · Lazio

Guanciale, San Marzano tomatoes, Pecorino Romano, white wine, and red chili flakes. From the mountain town of Amatrice (devastated by earthquake in 2016). The sauce is registered with an EU geographical indication. Served on bucatini or rigatoni.

The rule: Guanciale (not pancetta), bucatini or rigatoni, no onion in the traditional version.

Cacio e Pepe

Rome · Lazio

Three ingredients: Pecorino Romano, black pepper, pasta. The art is in the technique — finely grated cheese must be emulsified with starchy pasta water and pepper-infused water to create a creamy coating sauce with no clumping. One of the most technically difficult simple dishes in cooking.

The rule: Pecorino Romano only (not Parmesan). Pasta water is the only liquid. No butter, no cream, no oil.

Bolognese (Ragù alla Bolognese)

Bologna · Emilia-Romagna

Ground beef (and sometimes pork), soffritto (onion, carrot, celery), white wine, whole milk, and a small amount of tomato. Cooked low for 3–4 hours minimum. The milk tenderizes the meat and rounds the acidity. Officially registered with the Bologna Chamber of Commerce. Served with tagliatelle.

The rule: Tagliatelle, not spaghetti. Milk in the sauce. Minimal tomato — this is a meat sauce, not a tomato sauce.

Pesto alla Genovese

Genoa · Liguria

Basil, Parmigiano-Reggiano, Pecorino Sardo, pine nuts, garlic, extra virgin olive oil. Made in a marble mortar with a wooden pestle (the name comes from pestare, to pound). Never blended — the heat of a blade bruises the basil and turns it bitter. Served at room temperature on trofie or trenette with green beans and potatoes.

The rule: Small-leaf Genoese basil (DOP). Never cook the pesto — toss cold with hot pasta. Add a spoonful of pasta water to loosen.

Arrabbiata

Rome · Lazio

San Marzano tomatoes, garlic, and generous red chili flakes (peperoncino). The name means "angry" — referring to the spice level. One of the simplest pasta sauces; its excellence depends entirely on the quality of tomatoes and olive oil. Serves with penne rigate.

The rule: No onion. No meat. No cream. Penne only. The heat level should justify the name.

Vongole (alle Vongole)

Naples · Campania

Fresh clams, garlic, white wine, parsley, chili, olive oil. Two versions: in bianco (white, no tomato — the classic) and in rosso (with cherry tomatoes). The clam juice released during cooking becomes the sauce. Served on linguine or spaghetti. Clams must be fresh — briny and alive.

The rule: Never use canned clams. Live clams only. In bianco is the original and preferred version in Naples.

Sauce & Shape Pairings

The matching of pasta shape to sauce is not arbitrary — it follows culinary logic: light sauces on delicate pastas, heavy sauces on hearty shapes, chunky sauces on shapes with hollows and ridges to trap them. Italian nonnas have enforced these rules for centuries.

Cooking Tips — The technique behind great pasta

  • Al dente is not a preference — it's a technique. "Al dente" means "to the tooth" — the pasta should have a slight resistance when bitten, with no raw-flour center. It finishes cooking in the sauce (mantecatura). Overcooked pasta cannot be fixed.
  • Salt pasta water like the sea. The only chance to season the pasta itself is the cooking water. Use 10g of salt per liter of water (roughly 1 tablespoon per quart). It should taste seasoned, not faintly salty. Unsalted pasta water produces flat-tasting pasta regardless of the sauce.
  • Reserve pasta water before draining. One to two cups of the starchy cooking water is liquid gold — the starch emulsifies fat-based sauces, loosens thick sauces, and binds everything together. Always collect it before draining. Always.
  • Never rinse pasta after draining. Rinsing removes the surface starch that helps sauce adhere to the pasta. Cold rinsing also drops the temperature, causing the sauce to break. Drain; do not rinse; immediately add to the sauce.
  • Finish pasta in the sauce, not in the strainer. Add the slightly underdone pasta directly to the sauce in the pan. Add pasta water. Toss over high heat for 60–90 seconds. The pasta absorbs the sauce; the sauce coats the pasta. This step — mantecatura — is the difference between restaurant and home quality.
  • Match pasta thickness to sauce weight. Delicate sauces (olive oil, clams, fresh herbs) demand thin pasta. Hearty ragù demands thick pasta. A thin pasta overwhelmed by chunky bolognese is not an aesthetic preference issue — it's structural failure.
  • Fresh pasta is not always superior to dry pasta. For carbonara, cacio e pepe, and spaghetti dishes: dry pasta (bronze-die extruded, slow-dried) is correct. Fresh egg pasta is correct for tagliatelle bolognese, stuffed pastas, and Emilia-Romagna dishes. Substituting one for the other changes the dish's character.
  • Parmigiano-Reggiano and Pecorino Romano are not interchangeable. Parmesan is milder and nuttier. Pecorino is saltier, sharper, and more pungent. Roman classics (carbonara, cacio e pepe, amatriciana) call for Pecorino. Northern Italian dishes call for Parmigiano. Many chefs blend both for balance.
