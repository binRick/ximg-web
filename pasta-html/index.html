Pasta

Italian pasta — shapes, sauces, and the rules that matter

Long Pasta — Strand shapes

Long pasta works best with smooth, coating sauces that cling to the strands. Chunky sauces fall off — reserve those for shapes with ridges and hollows. The thickness of the strand should match the weight of the sauce.

Spaghetti
Thin round strands
The most iconic pasta in the world. Medium-thin, round cross-section. Pairs beautifully with carbonara, cacio e pepe, aglio e olio, and simple marinara. The universal long pasta baseline.
Linguine
Flat, narrow ribbons
Slightly flattened spaghetti — creates more surface area. Traditional partner for vongole (clams). The extra surface helps coat the pasta more evenly with oil-based sauces.
Fettuccine
Flat, wide ribbons
6–7mm wide flat ribbon. Roman classic — the proper home of Alfredo (though Alfredo in Rome uses only butter and Parmigiano, no cream). Pairs with robust cream or meat ragù.
Tagliatelle
Bologna's wide ribbon
6–8mm wide, egg-enriched. The official pasta of Bologna — as decreed by the Italian Academy of Cuisine in 1972. The only correct pasta for bolognese ragù. Not spaghetti. Never.
Pappardelle
Very wide Tuscan ribbon
Wide flat ribbons 2–3cm. Tuscan in origin. Needs a hearty, robust sauce — wild boar ragù (cinghiale), slow-braised lamb, or rich mushroom. The sauce should be as bold as the pasta.
Bucatini
Hollow spaghetti
Thick spaghetti with a hole through the center. Sauce enters the tube AND coats the outside — double-flavor delivery. The traditional pasta for all'amatriciana. Satisfying, chewy, unusual.
Spaghettini
Extra thin spaghetti
Thinner than spaghetti, cooks quickly. Best with very light sauces — fresh tomato, clam juice, lemon-herb. Too delicate for chunky meat sauces; they overwhelm the strand.
Vermicelli
Very thin strands
Even thinner than spaghettini. Matches delicate seafood or light tomato broths. Common in southern Italian seafood dishes. Quick cook time — careful with your timer.
