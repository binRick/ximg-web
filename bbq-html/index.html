American BBQ

Low and slow — the four great American BBQ regions and the art of smoke

Texas BBQ — Beef is king

Texas BBQ is about beef — specifically brisket — cooked over post oak wood with nothing but salt and pepper. It's the purist tradition: no sauce, no shortcuts, just time, smoke, and heat. Central Texas defined modern American BBQ culture.

Central Texas

Salt · Pepper · Post Oak · No Sauce

The Lockhart school — Kreuz Market, Smitty's, Black's BBQ — uses the simplest technique: coarse salt and cracked black pepper rub, post oak fire, offset smoker at 250°F for 12–18 hours. The brisket bark (crust) is nearly black, the interior pink with a smoke ring, the fat fully rendered to silk. Sauce is an insult.

  • Brisket (full packer, 14–18 lbs) is the centerpiece
  • Beef ribs — dinosaur-sized short ribs smoked 8+ hours
  • Sausage links (all-beef, German immigrant influence)
  • Served on butcher paper, sides: pickles, white bread, raw onion

East Texas

Slow-Cooked · Tomato Sauce · Tender

East Texas style cooks lower and longer — meat almost falls off the bone. Tomato-based BBQ sauce (sweeter, thinner) is served alongside or mopped on during cooking. African American pit master traditions are central to this style. Pork is more common than in Central Texas.

  • Pulled pork shoulders cooked until completely tender
  • Chopped beef sandwiches with sweet tomato sauce
  • Links with more pork content than Central Texas sausages
  • Sides: potato salad, coleslaw, pinto beans

South Texas / Barbacoa

Mexican Heritage · Beef Head · Mesquite

South Texas BBQ draws on Mexican and Tejano traditions. Barbacoa (beef cheeks, tongue, and head meat) cooked wrapped in maguey or banana leaves underground. Mesquite wood gives an intensely smoky, slightly bitter flavor. Weekend-only at most spots — cooked overnight.

  • Beef cheek (cachete) — meltingly tender after 12 hours
  • Lengua (tongue) — clean flavor, silky texture
  • Served with fresh corn tortillas and salsa roja
  • Cabrito (young goat) roasted whole over mesquite

Carolina BBQ — Whole Hog Tradition

The Carolinas claim the oldest continuous BBQ tradition in America — rooted in Native American and West African cooking techniques that predate the colonies. The debate between Eastern and Western Carolina is passionate and ongoing.

Eastern North Carolina

Whole Hog · Vinegar-Pepper · No Tomato

The purest form: entire hog cooked over hardwood coals for 8–12 hours, pulled, chopped, and dressed with nothing but apple cider vinegar and red pepper flakes. No tomato — ever. The Skylight Inn in Ayden has been doing this since 1947. The sauce IS the essence: acidic, spicy, cuts through the rich pork fat perfectly.

  • Whole hog (200–250 lbs) split and cooked skin-up
  • 100% wood coals — no gas, no electric, no shortcuts
  • Vinegar sauce: cider vinegar, crushed red pepper, salt
  • Coleslaw with vinegar dressing served on the sandwich

Western NC / Lexington Style

Pork Shoulder · Tomato-Vinegar · Dip

Lexington, NC uses pork shoulder (not whole hog) and introduces a small amount of ketchup to the vinegar sauce — creating the "Lexington Dip." More than 100 BBQ joints in a town of 20,000. The tomato content is minimal but sufficient to start wars with Eastern purists.

  • Boston butt (pork shoulder) cooked over hickory coals
  • Lexington Dip: cider vinegar + ketchup + sugar + pepper
  • Red slaw (coleslaw dressed with the Lexington Dip)
  • Bark and outside brown mixed into the pulled pork

South Carolina — Mustard Belt

Mustard Sauce · German Influence · Whole Hog

South Carolina has four distinct sauce regions in one state, but the golden mustard sauce (yellow mustard, vinegar, sugar, pepper) is uniquely South Carolinian — a legacy of German immigrant settlers in the Midlands. Maurice's BBQ is the most famous purveyor. Whole hog cooking remains the tradition.

  • Golden mustard sauce — tangy, bright, unlike anything in other states
  • Whole hog pits, often with direct wood heat
  • Hash and rice — a side dish specific to SC (pork organ stew served over rice)
  • Coast areas also feature vinegar and light tomato sauces

Kansas City BBQ — Ribs, Burnt Ends & Thick Sauce

Kansas City sits at the center of the American BBQ universe — it draws from every surrounding tradition while creating its own. The thick, sweet, tomato-molasses sauce that most Americans picture when they think "BBQ sauce" is a Kansas City invention.

Kansas City Style

Hickory Smoke · Sweet Sauce · Burnt Ends

KC BBQ is democratic — it does everything. Ribs (beef and pork), brisket, pulled pork, chicken, turkey, sausage. The unifying element is hickory smoke and the thick, sweet, tomato-based sauce applied at the end of cooking. The city has over 100 BBQ restaurants — more per capita than anywhere in America.

Burnt Ends
Kansas City Invention
The point end of the brisket, twice-smoked and cut into cubes. Caramelized exterior, rendered fat interior, sauced and returned to the smoker. KC's most famous contribution to BBQ. Originally thrown away as scrap — now the most coveted bite.
Pork Spare Ribs
Hickory · Sauced
Full spare ribs smoked over hickory for 5–6 hours, sauced in the final 30 minutes. The KC style: fall-off-the-bone tender (controversial in competition BBQ), lacquered with sweet sauce.
Arthur Bryant's
Est. 1930
The original Kansas City BBQ institution. Harry Truman ate here. Calvin Trillin called it "the single best restaurant in the world." The sauce is vinegary, peppery, and completely unlike modern sweet KC sauces.
Gates BBQ
Est. 1946
Six locations in Kansas City. Famous for the aggressive greeting "Hi, may I help you?" the moment you walk in. Rich, thick, spiced sauce. A KC institution across generations.

Kansas City Sauce Profile

Sweet · Thick · Tomato-Molasses

Base of tomato (ketchup or tomato sauce), molasses or brown sugar, apple cider vinegar, Worcestershire, onion powder, garlic, paprika, black pepper. Thick enough to coat a spoon. Applied in layers during the last 30–60 minutes of smoking to caramelize. This is the sauce that shaped American BBQ culture globally.

Memphis BBQ — Pork Ribs & Dry Rub Mastery

Memphis is pork country, and ribs are the religion. The pivotal Memphis question is dry vs. wet — and both traditions are fiercely defended. Memphis-style has influenced Nashville, St. Louis, and most of the mid-South BBQ scene.

Dry Ribs

Rub Only · No Sauce · The Pure Form

A complex dry rub is applied generously to pork ribs — paprika, garlic powder, onion powder, celery salt, black pepper, cayenne, brown sugar — then the ribs are smoked over hickory or charcoal for 4–5 hours with no mopping, no basting, no sauce. The surface becomes a thick, crackling, spiced crust. Charlie Vergos' Rendezvous made this style famous.

  • Charlie Vergos' Rendezvous — the temple of dry ribs since 1948
  • Ribs finished directly over charcoal (not offset) — distinctive char
  • Dry rub with heavy paprika, garlic, and celery salt
  • Sauce served on the side — never applied

Wet Ribs

Mopped · Sauced · Caramelized

The same ribs, but mopped with a thin, tangy, vinegar-based sauce during smoking, then given a final glaze of thicker sauce at the end. Results in caramelized, lacquered ribs that glisten. The debate between dry and wet is the Memphis BBQ defining argument.

Pulled Pork Sandwiches

Chopped · Piled High · Coleslaw On Top

Memphis-style pulled pork is cooked until the shoulder completely surrenders. Chopped (not pulled) into a mix of meat and bark. Piled high on a bun with tangy coleslaw directly on top of the pork — the slaw's acid and crunch cut the richness perfectly. A thin, tangy sauce is drizzled over.

Woods & Smoke — The hidden ingredient

The wood defines the smoke, and the smoke defines the meat. Different woods impart different flavors, burn at different temperatures, and suit different proteins. Wood selection is one of the most consequential choices a pit master makes.

Post Oak
Texas BBQ Wood
Medium smoke, slightly sweet, clean finish. Burns long and hot at a consistent temperature. The definitive wood of Central Texas BBQ — neutral enough to let beef shine.
Hickory
Strong · Bacon-Like
The most used BBQ wood in America. Strong, bacon-like smoke flavor. Can overpower delicate meats. Perfect for pork ribs and pork shoulder. Kansas City and Memphis staple.
Mesquite
Bold · Intense · Texas
Burns hot and fast with a very strong, bold smoke. Used extensively in South and West Texas. Can turn bitter with long cooks — best for high-heat grilling over short periods rather than long smokes.
Applewood
Mild · Fruity · Sweet
Light, mildly sweet smoke with a subtle fruity note. One of the most versatile BBQ woods. Excellent with pork, poultry, and fish. Creates a beautiful mahogany color on chicken skin.
Cherrywood
Sweet · Deep Red Color
Sweet, mild smoke with a slight tartness. Produces a deep, mahogany-red bark color. Often blended with oak or hickory to add sweetness without overpowering. Great on pork and poultry.
Pecan
Medium · Nutty · Rich
Rich, slightly sweet, nutty smoke. More subtle than hickory. Native to the American South — a natural pairing with Southern BBQ traditions. Excellent on brisket and pork ribs.
Alder
Light · Pacific Northwest
Very mild, delicate smoke — the classic wood for Pacific salmon smoking. Gentle enough for fish and chicken. Won't overpower light proteins. Common in Pacific Northwest smoking traditions.
Maple
Mild · Sweet · Smoke Ring
Sweet, light smoke with a subtle maple syrup note. Creates a beautiful smoke ring. Works beautifully with ham, bacon, and poultry. Often blended with stronger woods for balance.
White Oak
Classic All-Rounder
Medium smoke, earthy, clean. Burns very consistently and long — a pit master's reliable choice. Pairs with any protein. The backbone of many regional BBQ blends.
Grapevine
Fruity · Wine Country
Intense fruity smoke with a wine-grape aroma. Burns quickly. Best as a small addition to other woods for complexity. Pairs particularly well with lamb and duck.

Rubs & Sauces

Texas Brisket Rub
Salt + Pepper Only
50/50 coarse kosher salt and 16-mesh black pepper. Nothing else. Applied generously 1 hour before smoking. The simplicity is intentional — let the beef and smoke do the work.
Memphis Dry Rub
Complex Spice Blend
Paprika (40%), brown sugar, garlic powder, onion powder, celery salt, black pepper, cayenne, cumin, dried thyme. Rubbed in hard and rested overnight. Creates an exceptional crust on pork.
KC All-Purpose Rub
Sweet + Savory
Brown sugar, paprika, black pepper, salt, chili powder, garlic powder, onion powder, cayenne. The sweet base caramelizes beautifully with KC's sticky tomato-molasses finishing sauce.
Carolina Vinegar Sauce
Eastern NC Style
Apple cider vinegar, red pepper flakes, salt, black pepper. Thin, sharp, acidic. Applied to pulled pork after cooking. Its acid cuts the fat and provides contrast — deceptively simple.
Kansas City Sauce
Sweet Tomato-Molasses
Ketchup, molasses, brown sugar, cider vinegar, Worcestershire, paprika, garlic, onion. Thick, sticky, sweet. Applied in the last 30 min of cooking, then again when serving.
South Carolina Mustard
Golden Yellow
Yellow mustard, apple cider vinegar, brown sugar, hot sauce, garlic, black pepper, butter. Tangy, bright, completely unique. Applied to whole hog pork — cuts richness perfectly.

Cuts — Anatomy of BBQ

Brisket — Flat
Lean · Sliceable
The thin, lean section of the brisket. Slices beautifully. Less marbling than the point. Easier to cook evenly. Target 195–205°F internal for slice; must rest 1–2 hours wrapped.
Brisket — Point
Fatty · Burnt Ends
The thick, heavily marbled "deckle" end. Cooks slower than the flat. After separating, can be cubed and returned to the smoker to make burnt ends — the most prized BBQ product.
Beef Short Ribs
Dinosaur Ribs
Chuck or plate short ribs — 3–4 bone slabs weighing 2–3 lbs each. Enormous, rich, intensely beefy. Smoked 8–10 hours until probe-tender at 200–205°F. The showpiece of Texas BBQ.
Pork Spare Ribs
Full Rack
Full rack from the belly side — more meat and fat than baby backs. Requires trimming (St. Louis cut removes the sternum and cartilage). 5–6 hours at 225–250°F. The classic BBQ rib.
St. Louis Cut Ribs
Spare Ribs Trimmed
Spare ribs with the sternum, cartilage, and rib tips removed. More uniform shape, cooks evenly. The standard for competition BBQ. More surface area for bark and rub.
Baby Back Ribs
Loin Ribs · Leaner
From the top of the rib cage near the loin. Leaner, shorter, more tender than spare ribs. Faster cook: 4–5 hours. Higher price point. Most widely sold in American restaurants.
Pork Shoulder (Boston Butt)
Pulled Pork
Upper portion of the pork shoulder (despite the name, no proximity to the rear). Rich in intramuscular fat and collagen. Cooked 8–12 hours at 225°F until 195–205°F internal. Falls apart into pulled pork.
Whole Hog
Carolina Tradition
200–250 lb hog cooked split over hardwood coals for 8–12 hours. Requires a dedicated pit. The bark from the skin, mixed into the pulled meat, is the most prized element. A community event as much as a cooking method.
