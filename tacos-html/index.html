Tacos

Mexico's greatest street food — tacos al pastor, birria, carnitas, and more

Regional Taco Styles — Mexico's diverse taco traditions

Mexico's taco culture is hyperlocal. Each region developed distinct flavors, proteins, and preparation methods shaped by indigenous ingredients, colonial history, and geography. No two regions make tacos the same way.

Mexico City

CDMX Style

Home of tacos al pastor (spit-roasted pork with pineapple, Lebanese-origin), tacos de canasta (basket tacos, steamed and eaten mid-morning), and suadero (brisket). The CDMX taqueria runs 24 hours. Small corn tortillas, abundance of fresh cilantro and onion.

Tijuana / Baja California

California Style

Birthplace of the fish taco — battered, fried white fish in a corn tortilla, topped with cabbage, crema, and salsa. Also famous for carne asada tacos with guacamole. California-Mexican fusion crossed the border and became its own genre.

Jalisco

Birria Country

Origin of birria: slow-braised goat or beef marinated in dried chiles, achiote, and spices. The modern birria taco is dipped in consommé (the cooking broth), pan-fried until crispy, and loaded with cheese — birria quesatacos took the internet by storm globally.

Yucatan Peninsula

Mayan Roots

Cochinita pibil dominates — pork rubbed with achiote paste and sour orange, wrapped in banana leaves, and slow-roasted in an underground pit (pib). Pink pickled red onions are the essential garnish. Flavor is earthy, tangy, and deeply aromatic.

Oaxaca

Land of Mole

Tlayudas rather than standard tacos — large, crispy tortillas topped with black beans, asiento (unrefined pork fat), Oaxacan string cheese, and grilled meats. Also chapulines (toasted grasshopper) tacos, a pre-Columbian tradition still eaten today.

Monterrey / Norteño

Northern Beef Culture

Northern Mexico is cattle country. Carne asada (grilled beef) rules here, often in flour tortillas rather than corn — the flour tortilla tradition is stronger in the north. Arrachera (skirt steak) and cabrito (young goat) are regional specialties.

Classic Tacos — The canonical lineup

Al Pastor
Pork · CDMX
Lebanese-inspired spit-roasted pork (trompo) marinated in dried chiles, achiote, and pineapple. Shaved directly onto a small corn tortilla. Topped with fresh pineapple, onion, and cilantro. Mexico City's signature.
Birria
Beef/Goat · Jalisco
Slow-braised meat in a rich consommé of dried guajillo, ancho, and morita chiles. Modern style: dipped in broth, griddled with Oaxacan cheese until crispy (quesabirria). Served with consommé for dipping.
Carnitas
Pork · Michoacán
Pork slow-braised in lard until fall-apart tender, then crisped. Made from every part: shoulder, belly, head, intestine. Michoacán style finishes with orange juice and milk for a complex caramelized crust.
Barbacoa
Beef/Lamb · Hidalgo
Traditionally beef cheek or whole lamb wrapped in maguey leaves and slow-cooked in an underground pit overnight. Weekend-only in most taquerias. Incredibly tender, smoky, with mineral depth.
Suadero
Beef · CDMX
Thinly cut beef from the area between skin and muscle (brisket/flank junction). Slow-poached in oil or lard until silky soft, then crisped on a comal. Mild flavor, succulent texture.
Lengua
Beef Tongue
Beef tongue braised for hours until tender, then sliced or chopped and griddled. One of the most tender taco fillings — no connective tissue, pure silky meat. Often underrated by newcomers.
Cabeza
Beef Head
Various cuts from the beef head: cachete (cheek), trompa (snout), sesos (brain). Steam-cooked low and slow. A nose-to-tail tradition that wastes nothing and rewards adventurous eaters.
Carne Asada
Grilled Beef · Norteño
Marinated skirt or flank steak grilled over charcoal, chopped or sliced. Lime, salt, and guacamole. Flour tortillas in the north. The definitive taco of Tijuana and Sonora.
Taco de Pescado
Fish · Baja California
Beer-battered or grilled white fish (cod, tilapia, mahi-mahi) with shredded cabbage, lime crema, and salsa. Born in Ensenada in the 1960s. The taco that conquered California.
Cochinita Pibil
Pork · Yucatan
Achiote-marinated pork slow-cooked in banana leaves. Vivid orange-red, earthy and slightly sour from sour orange juice. Mandatory garnish: habanero salsa and pickled red onions.
Chorizo
Pork Sausage · Nationwide
Fresh Mexican chorizo — red with chile and vinegar, crumbled and fried. Often mixed with potato (chorizo con papa). Completely different from Spanish chorizo — wet-cured vs fresh.

Proteins — Preparation methods

Pork (Cerdo)
Most Versatile
Al pastor (spit-roasted), carnitas (lard-braised), cochinita (pit-roasted), chorizo (fresh sausage), chicharrón (fried skin). No animal is used more completely in Mexican taco culture.
Beef (Res)
Carne Asada · Birria
Carne asada (grilled), birria (braised), barbacoa (pit-cooked), suadero (poached/crisped), lengua (braised tongue), cabeza (steamed head meats). Northern Mexico especially favors beef.
Chicken (Pollo)
Grilled · Shredded
Tinga de pollo — shredded chicken in smoky chipotle-tomato sauce. Grilled adobo chicken (pollo asado). Milder flavor that carries spicy salsas well. Popular at family taquerias.
Fish (Pescado)
Fried · Grilled
Beer-battered for Baja-style; grilled for lighter preparations. White fish like cod, tilapia, and mahi-mahi are standard. Topped with cabbage for crunch.
Shrimp (Camarón)
Coastal · Sautéed
Sautéed with garlic, butter, and chiles. Common along both Pacific and Gulf coasts. Often paired with avocado and mango-based salsas for a sweet-spicy contrast.
Vegetables (Verduras)
Mushroom · Nopal
Huitlacoche (corn fungus, a Mexican delicacy), rajas con queso (roasted poblano strips with cheese), sautéed mushrooms, nopal (cactus paddle) — earthy, rich vegetarian options with deep tradition.

Salsas — The soul of the taco

A taco without salsa is incomplete. Mexican salsa culture is staggering in variety — raw and cooked, smooth and chunky, mild and face-melting. Every taqueria has a house salsa that defines its identity.

Salsa Roja
Red · Cooked
Roasted tomatoes, serranos or jalapeños, garlic, and onion blended smooth. The baseline salsa of Mexico. Heat level varies widely by recipe and region.
Salsa Verde
Green · Tomatillo
Charred or boiled tomatillos with serrano chiles, cilantro, and garlic. Tangy and bright. Pairs especially well with pork tacos and carnitas.
Pico de Gallo
Fresh · Raw
Diced raw tomato, white onion, jalapeño, cilantro, lime juice, salt. No cooking. Maximum freshness — the antithesis of a smooth salsa. Best on fish and carne asada.
Salsa de Habanero
Orange · Nuclear
Roasted habanero with tomato and vinegar. Intensely fruity heat — habaneros are floral and citrusy before the searing pain arrives. Yucatan's essential salsa.
Salsa Negra
Black · Deep
Dried chile negro or mulato blended with garlic and lard. Dark, complex, slightly bitter, almost smoky-sweet. Goes especially well with mushroom or chicken tacos.
Salsa de Árbol
Red · Fiery
Chile de árbol (small, very hot) with toasted sesame seeds, tomato, and vinegar. Thin, sharp, intensely hot. The go-to high-heat salsa at most taquerias.
Guacamole
Avocado · Fresh
Mashed Hass avocado with lime, salt, cilantro, onion, serrano. Simple at its best — resist the urge to add extras. A whole food group in taco culture.
Crema & Lime Sauce
White · Creamy
Mexican crema (thinner than sour cream) with lime zest and sometimes chipotle puree. Drizzled on fish tacos for cooling contrast to the heat.
Mole Negro
Complex · Oaxacan
Over 20 ingredients: dried chiles, chocolate, plantain, spices, herbs. Black as night. A labor of love — some moles take 3 days to make. Poured over shredded turkey or chicken.
Salsa Taquera
Al Pastor Companion
Taquero-style red chile sauce specific to al pastor stands — slightly oily, thick, made from guajillo and morita chiles. Drizzled from a squeeze bottle by the taquero.

Tortillas — The vessel that defines the taco

The tortilla is not merely a wrapper — it contributes flavor, texture, and structural integrity. The choice of corn vs. flour, and the type of corn, completely changes the taco experience.

White Corn Tortilla
Most Common
Ground dried white corn nixtamalized with calcium hydroxide (cal). The standard throughout central and southern Mexico. Subtle flavor, slightly chewy. Must be kept warm — cool and dry, it cracks.
Yellow Corn Tortilla
Deeper Flavor
Yellow corn has more pronounced earthy, maize flavor than white. Common in the US and some regions of Mexico. Slightly sturdier than white corn varieties.
Blue Corn Tortilla
Antioxidant-Rich
Made from blue maize (morado), an indigenous variety. Slightly nuttier, denser flavor. Higher anthocyanin content. Visually striking — dark purple-blue color turns heads at any taco stand.
Flour Tortilla
Northern Mexico
Wheat flour, lard, water, and salt. Soft, pliable, slightly chewy. Dominates in Sonora, Chihuahua, Sinaloa. Also in Tex-Mex. Can hold much more filling than corn without breaking.
Handmade vs Machine
Texture Matters
Handmade tortillas (hechas a mano) pressed and cooked to order are incomparably better than mass-produced versions. Uneven thickness creates pockets of puffed dough and chewier edges.
Tlayuda
Oaxacan Large Tortilla
Large, partially dried corn tortilla 12–14 inches across. Not folded — spread flat with black beans, asiento, and toppings. Crispy at edges, flexible in center. Oaxaca's answer to pizza.

Build Your Own Taco

The anatomy of a perfect taco: a doubled corn tortilla, one protein, one salsa, fresh garnishes. More than three toppings and the taco loses focus. Great taco philosophy is about restraint.

Tortilla Base

Primary Protein
Al pastor · Carnitas · Carne asada · Birria · Barbacoa · Fish · Shrimp · Chicken tinga · Chorizo · Suadero · Lengua
Salsa (pick one)

Creamy Additions

Chili powder · Dried oregano · Sea salt flakes · Toasted sesame seeds · Chile flakes
Extras & Sides

The Golden Rules of Taco Assembly
  • ▸ Always double-stack corn tortillas — one tears, two hold
  • ▸ Protein goes in first, directly on the warm tortilla
  • ▸ Salsa goes on top of the protein, not the tortilla — it keeps the base intact
  • ▸ Cilantro and onion are non-negotiable for authentic street tacos
  • ▸ Lime squeezed immediately before eating, never before — it cooks the meat
  • ▸ Guacamole is a topping, not a dipping sauce
  • ▸ Eat over a plate tilted at 45° — the taco lean is real
  • ▸ Radishes are a palate cleanser between bites, not decoration
