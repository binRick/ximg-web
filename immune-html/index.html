The Immune System

Watch the body's defense system activate in real time — from first contact to antibody production.

T = 0 hours
Pathogen invasion detected

A pathogen (bacterium or virus) breaches the epithelial barrier and enters tissue. Damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) trigger the innate immune response.

Innate Response
0–96 hours — Non-specific, fast
Neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells
Adaptive Response
Day 4–14 — Specific, slower but stronger
T cells, B cells, antibodies, memory cells

Immune Cell Types

All immune cells derive from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow.

Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)

Y-shaped glycoproteins produced by plasma cells (differentiated B cells). Each antibody binds to a specific antigen via its variable regions.

Antibody Structure

Variable region (Fab): Antigen-binding site. Unique amino acid sequence per antibody. Two identical Fab arms per antibody.

Constant region (Fc): Determines antibody class (IgG, IgM, etc.). Binds Fc receptors on immune cells and complement proteins.

Heavy chains (2): ~55 kDa each. Determine antibody class.

Light chains (2): ~25 kDa each. Either kappa (κ) or lambda (λ).

Lock and key — antibody-antigen binding

The variable regions of the Fab arms fold into a precise 3D shape complementary to the antigen's epitope. Binding involves hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions, van der Waals forces, and hydrophobic interactions.

How Vaccines Work

Vaccines train the immune system to recognize a pathogen without causing disease.

Vaccine Administration

A vaccine introduces an antigen (or instructions to make one) into the body. The immune system responds as if to a real infection — producing antibodies and memory cells — without the risk of disease.
