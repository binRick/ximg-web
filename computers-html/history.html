🇺🇸  Made in America

The history of computing is American history

From wartime calculating machines to the AI revolution, the milestones that defined the computer age were conceived, funded, and built in the United States — in Pentagon labs, university basements, Silicon Valley garages, and suburban shopping malls.

1945
ENIAC — First General-Purpose Electronic Computermilestone
J. Presper Eckert and John Mauchly complete ENIAC at the University of Pennsylvania, funded by the U.S. Army. It weighs 30 tons, occupies 1,800 square feet, and uses 18,000 vacuum tubes. It calculates artillery firing tables 1,000× faster than a human. Every computer ever built descends from this machine.
1947
The Transistor Invented at Bell Labsmilestone
William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain invent the transistor at Bell Labs, Murray Hill, New Jersey. It replaces fragile, power-hungry vacuum tubes and makes miniaturization possible. The three share the 1956 Nobel Prize in Physics. Without the transistor, every device in this room is impossible.
1958
Integrated Circuit Inventedmilestone
Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments and Robert Noyce at Fairchild Semiconductor independently invent the integrated circuit — multiple transistors etched onto a single chip of silicon. Kilby wins the Nobel Prize in 2000. The IC is the foundation of every CPU, GPU, and memory chip ever made.
1964
IBM System/360 — Computer Architecture Bornproduct
IBM's System/360 is the first family of computers designed as a compatible, scalable range — from small business machines to massive scientific systems. Software runs across the entire lineup unmodified. IBM bets the company — $5B in development, more than the Manhattan Project. The concept of a "computer architecture" is born here.
1965
Moore's Lawmilestone
Intel co-founder Gordon Moore observes that the number of transistors on a chip doubles roughly every two years while cost stays flat. Moore's Law becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy driving 50 years of exponential progress — from the 2,300-transistor 4004 to NVIDIA's 80-billion-transistor H100.
1971
Intel 4004 — First Commercial Microprocessorproduct
Intel ships the 4004 — the world's first commercially available microprocessor. Designed by Federico Faggin, Ted Hoff, and Stanley Mazor, it packs 2,300 transistors onto a chip the size of a fingernail and runs at 740 kHz. Calculators, then computers, then the entire world follow.
1975
Altair 8800 & Microsoft Foundedproduct
MITS ships the Altair 8800 kit for $397. A 19-year-old Bill Gates and Paul Allen read about it in Popular Electronics and cold-call MITS claiming they have a BASIC interpreter. They write it in three weeks on a PDP-10. Microsoft is founded. The personal computer era begins in a New Mexico warehouse.
1977
Apple II — Personal Computing Goes Mainstreamproduct
Steve Wozniak designs the Apple II — a complete, ready-to-use computer in a beige plastic case with color graphics and a keyboard. VisiCalc (1979), the first spreadsheet, makes it indispensable for business. The Apple II sells 5 million units and turns two guys in a garage into a $1.79B IPO.
1981
IBM PC — The Standard Is Setproduct
IBM ships the PC 5150 with an open architecture and MS-DOS. Using off-the-shelf parts to ship in one year, IBM inadvertently creates a clone industry. Compaq reverse-engineers the BIOS legally in 1982. Within five years IBM-compatible PCs dominate entirely. The concept of a "PC" means IBM-compatible to this day.
1984
Apple Macintosh — The GUI Revolutionproduct
Apple introduces the Macintosh during Super Bowl XVIII with Ridley Scott's "1984" ad — the most famous commercial ever made. The Mac is the first mass-market computer with a graphical interface and mouse, inspired by Xerox PARC research. Windows follows in 1985. The command line begins its long retreat.
1991
Linux — Open Source Takes Rootmilestone
Finnish student Linus Torvalds posts: "I'm doing a (free) operating system." Linux eventually powers 96% of the world's top web servers, every Android phone, and every major cloud. The open-source model — strangers collaborating across the internet on critical infrastructure — becomes one of the defining ideas of the internet age.
1993
Mosaic Browser — The Web Becomes Visualmilestone
Marc Andreessen and Eric Bina at NCSA ship Mosaic — the first graphical web browser. It makes the Web (invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989) accessible to ordinary people. Andreessen founds Netscape. The browser wars erupt. The internet transforms from an academic curiosity into global civilization infrastructure.
2007
iPhone — The Pocket Computerproduct
Steve Jobs announces the iPhone: "An iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator." The multi-touch screen, mobile Safari, and App Store (2008) create a new computing paradigm. The smartphone becomes the most widely used computer in history — 6.8 billion in service today. More people access the internet via phone than PC.
2012
AlexNet & the Deep Learning Explosionmilestone
Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton train AlexNet on two NVIDIA GPUs and win the ImageNet competition by a staggering margin. Deep learning — neural networks trained on GPUs — becomes the dominant paradigm in AI. NVIDIA's stock begins a decade-long ascent. The GPU replaces the CPU as the most important chip in tech.
2020
Apple M1 — The ARM Revolutionproduct
Apple ships the M1 — the first ARM chip for Mac. Designed in-house, it delivers 2–3× the performance per watt of Intel's best. The x86 architecture's 40-year desktop dominance cracks. Qualcomm's Snapdragon X Elite and Microsoft Copilot+ PCs follow. The CPU architecture wars restart for the first time since the 1990s.
2022
ChatGPT & the Generative AI Eramilestone
OpenAI releases ChatGPT — 100 million users in two months, the fastest product adoption in history. The transformer architecture (Google, 2017), trained on NVIDIA H100 clusters at $1B+ compute budgets, powers a wave of AI that reshapes software, search, and knowledge work. The next computing era has arrived.
