The Copenhagen Interpretation

Before observation, a quantum particle exists as a wave of probability — the wavefunction ψ describes all possible positions simultaneously. The filled curve shows |ψ|², the probability density of finding the particle at each position. When you observe the particle, the wavefunction collapses to a definite location. Afterward, a new wave packet begins spreading again.

ψ(x,t) = A · e^(−(x−x₀)²/4σ²) · e^(i(k₀x − ωt))

Why does observation collapse the wave?

Observation requires interaction — even a single photon to "see" the particle carries enough energy to disturb it. This measurement interaction entangles the particle with the measuring apparatus, causing decoherence: the quantum superposition becomes a classical definite state. The act of measurement is physically unavoidable.

The Double Slit Experiment

Fire particles one at a time through two slits. With observation OFF, each particle passes through both slits simultaneously (as a wave) and interferes with itself — creating bright/dark fringes on the screen. With observation ON (detecting which slit), the particle must "choose" a slit, destroying the interference pattern. The act of knowing which path collapses the wave.

A qubit |ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + e^(iφ)sin(θ/2)|1⟩ lives on the Bloch sphere surface. The poles are |0⟩ (north) and |1⟩ (south). The equator is maximum superposition. Drag the sliders to explore all qubit states.

Entangled Qubits (right) — Bell State

Two entangled qubits in the Bell state |Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 are perfectly correlated. Measuring qubit A instantly determines qubit B — no matter the distance. This is not faster-than-light communication: you can't choose which outcome you get, only discover it. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance."

Copenhagen Interpretation

The cat exists in a superposition of alive and dead until observed. Opening the box constitutes a measurement. The wavefunction collapses to one definite state. Before measurement, neither state is more "real" than the other.

Many-Worlds Interpretation

The wavefunction never collapses. Instead, the universe splits: in one branch the cat is alive, in another it is dead. Both outcomes are equally real — the observer themselves enters superposition with the cat. There is no collapse, only branching.

Quantum Decoherence

A macroscopic cat interacts with ~10²³ air molecules per second. Each interaction entangles the cat with its environment, destroying quantum coherence almost instantly (~10⁻³⁰ s). Real superposition is impossible at cat scale — the thought experiment reveals the measurement problem.

The Measurement Problem

Schrödinger's equation is deterministic and reversible — it never shows collapse. Yet we always observe definite outcomes. Why does measurement yield a single result? This remains one of quantum mechanics' deepest unresolved questions, debated since 1926.
