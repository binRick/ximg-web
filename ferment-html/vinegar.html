Vinegar

Apple Cider Vinegar (Raw)

Made from fermented apple juice. Two stages: alcoholic fermentation (yeast converts apple sugars to hard cider), then acetic fermentation (Acetobacter converts alcohol to acetic acid). Raw, unfiltered ACV contains "the mother" — strands of beneficial bacteria. Pasteurized commercial ACV has no mother. Used in salad dressings, wellness tonics, marinades, and as a meat tenderizer.

Wine Vinegar

Red wine vinegar: bold, fruity, used in vinaigrettes and Mediterranean cooking. White wine vinegar: lighter, more delicate. Champagne vinegar: the mildest wine vinegar, excellent for delicate sauces. Quality matters enormously — cheap wine vinegar is harsh and one-dimensional. Aged wine vinegar (in wood barrels) develops complex, mellow flavor. Sherry vinegar (from Andalusia) is aged in a solera system and is worth seeking out.

Balsamic Vinegar

Traditional Balsamic (Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena): aged 12–25+ years in a series of progressively smaller barrels (solera). Made from Trebbiano grape must. Sweet, thick, complex — used sparingly as a condiment. Very expensive ($100–$300+ for 100ml). Commercial balsamic (what most people buy) is wine vinegar with caramel coloring and sweetener — entirely different product. The DOP label guarantees authenticity.

Rice Vinegar

Made from fermented rice wine (sake). The mildest, least acidic common vinegar. Essential in Japanese cuisine: seasoning sushi rice, making ponzu, pickling ginger. Chinese black vinegar (Chinkiang) is made from glutinous rice — dark, malty, complex. Korean rice vinegar is similar to Japanese but slightly less sweet. Seasoned rice vinegar has added sugar and salt — convenient for sushi rice but not a 1:1 substitute in other applications.

Making Your Own

Start with homemade or commercial wine/cider at 5–10% ABV. Pour into a wide-mouth jar with a cloth cover (needs oxygen). Add a vinegar mother if available; otherwise let wild Acetobacter colonize. In 4–8 weeks at 65–80°F, the alcohol converts to acetic acid. Keep away from heat and light. Taste-test: should be sharp with no alcohol taste. Filter through cheesecloth; age in a cool dark place.
