Kimchi

Baechu-Kimchi (Traditional)

The classic — napa cabbage kimchi. Cabbage quartered or halved and salted heavily for 1–2 hours (or overnight). Rinsed, dried. Paste: gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes), fish sauce or salted shrimp (jeot), garlic, ginger, green onion. Mixed thoroughly. Pack into jar, press down hard to eliminate air pockets. Room temp 1–2 days, then refrigerate. Peaks at 1–2 weeks; continues developing for months.

Kkakdugi (Radish Kimchi)

Cubed daikon radish kimchi — crunchier and quicker to ferment than baechu. Radish cut into 3/4" cubes, salted briefly, then tossed with the same paste. The radish's higher water content means it doesn't need as much brining time. Ready in 1–3 days. Spicier and more pungent than baechu. Often served alongside soup, especially seolleongtang (ox bone soup).

DaikonCrunchyReady in 1–3 days

Oi Sobagi (Cucumber Kimchi)

Stuffed cucumber kimchi — cut cucumbers are scored with a cross cut and stuffed with a spicy paste of green onion, garlic, ginger, gochugaru, and sometimes carrots or chives. Ferments very quickly (a few hours to overnight) due to cucumber's thin cell walls. Not a long-storage kimchi — best eaten within a week. Refreshing and crispy; perfect in summer.

Mak Kimchi (Quick/Easy)

The "rough" or "easy" kimchi — cabbage cut into bite-sized pieces rather than keeping the head intact. Quicker preparation: no need to quarter the cabbage and spread paste leaf-by-leaf. Same ingredients as baechu. Great for beginners and for making large batches for cooking (kimchi fried rice, kimchi jjigae). Less elegant presentation but identical flavor.

Mul Kimchi (Water Kimchi)

A mild, soup-like kimchi in a clear brine. No gochugaru — pale pink color from a small amount of beet or gentle red pepper. Made with napa cabbage, radish, green onion, garlic, and ginger in a lightly salted, slightly sweet water brine. Refreshing and mild enough for those who can't eat spicy food. Often served as a palate cleanser between spicy dishes.

Vegan Kimchi

Traditional kimchi uses fish sauce or salted shrimp for umami and fermentation support. Vegan substitutes: kelp powder + soy sauce, miso, or fermented soybean paste (doenjang). The umami is slightly different but still excellent. Some Korean families have always made vegan kimchi using only vegetables. The fermentation process is identical — salt + lactic acid bacteria do the work.

Fermentation Stages

Day 1–2 (fresh, geotjeori): bright, spicy, crunchy — eaten immediately. Day 3–7 (well-fermented,익은): tangy, complex, bubbling — the peak for most people. Day 2–8 weeks (aged, pungent): intensely sour, funky; excellent for kimchi jjigae and kimchi fried rice where the flavors meld with heat. Years-aged kimchi (mukeunji): prized delicacy — sour and complex beyond description.
