Fermentation Basics

Lacto-Fermentation

The most common home fermentation. Lactobacillus bacteria (naturally present on vegetables and in salt brines) convert sugars to lactic acid. No starter culture needed — salt selects for lactic acid bacteria by suppressing competing pathogens. Creates sauerkraut, kimchi, pickles, kvass. pH drops to 3–4, making the environment self-preserving.

Salt brineLactic acidSelf-preserving

Wild Yeast Fermentation

Saccharomyces and other wild yeasts naturally present on flour, fruit skins, and in the environment. Convert sugars to CO₂ and ethanol. The basis of sourdough bread, wine, beer, and kvass. Wild yeast communities are location-specific — San Francisco sourdough has different microorganism ratios than a Tokyo starter. Temperature and feeding ratio shape the yeast/bacteria balance.

Wild yeastCO₂ + ethanolLocation-specific

Acetic Acid Fermentation

A two-step process: first, yeast ferments sugar to ethanol; then Acetobacter bacteria oxidize ethanol to acetic acid (vinegar). Requires oxygen — unlike lacto-fermentation. The mother of vinegar is a cellulose mat of Acetobacter. Kombucha uses this process in its second fermentation phase. Apple cider vinegar, wine vinegar, and rice vinegar all use this pathway.

Role of Salt

Salt is the key variable in lacto-fermentation. 2–3% salt by weight (relative to vegetables) is the standard range. Too little: undesirable bacteria compete. Too much: fermentation slows or stalls. Salt draws water out of vegetables via osmosis, creating the brine. It selects for salt-tolerant Lactobacillus while suppressing mold and pathogens. Kosher salt or non-iodized sea salt — iodine inhibits fermentation.

2–3% saltNon-iodizedOsmosis

pH and Anaerobic Environment

Fermentation jars should be kept anaerobic (oxygen-free) to prevent mold and kahm yeast (harmless white surface film, but unpleasant). Weights keep vegetables submerged below the brine. Airlock lids allow CO₂ to escape while blocking oxygen. Starting pH is neutral (6–7); finished ferments reach pH 3.5–4. This acidity is what preserves the food for months.

Temperature Effects

Temperature is the primary lever for fermentation speed and flavor. 65–75°F: ideal lacto-fermentation range, active but controlled. Below 55°F: very slow (fridge), good for cold-proofing sourdough or slowing kimchi aging. Above 80°F: faster fermentation but more acetic acid — sharper, more tangy. In hot climates, ferments go sour quickly; move to a cool basement or use less salt.

Benefits

Fermented foods offer: probiotics (live cultures supporting gut microbiome), enhanced nutrient bioavailability (fermentation predigests phytates that block mineral absorption), preservation (lactic acid extends shelf life for months), reduced lactose in dairy ferments, and dramatically increased flavor complexity. B vitamins synthesized by bacteria. The microbial diversity in traditional ferments exceeds commercial probiotic supplements.
