Fermentation Basics

Lacto-Fermentation

The most common home fermentation. Lactobacillus bacteria (naturally present on vegetables and in salt brines) convert sugars to lactic acid. No starter culture needed — salt selects for lactic acid bacteria by suppressing competing pathogens. Creates sauerkraut, kimchi, pickles, kvass. pH drops to 3–4, making the environment self-preserving.

Salt brineLactic acidSelf-preserving

Wild Yeast Fermentation

Saccharomyces and other wild yeasts naturally present on flour, fruit skins, and in the environment. Convert sugars to CO₂ and ethanol. The basis of sourdough bread, wine, beer, and kvass. Wild yeast communities are location-specific — San Francisco sourdough has different microorganism ratios than a Tokyo starter. Temperature and feeding ratio shape the yeast/bacteria balance.

Wild yeastCO₂ + ethanolLocation-specific

Acetic Acid Fermentation

A two-step process: first, yeast ferments sugar to ethanol; then Acetobacter bacteria oxidize ethanol to acetic acid (vinegar). Requires oxygen — unlike lacto-fermentation. The mother of vinegar is a cellulose mat of Acetobacter. Kombucha uses this process in its second fermentation phase. Apple cider vinegar, wine vinegar, and rice vinegar all use this pathway.

Two-stepNeeds oxygenAcetic acid

Role of Salt

Salt is the key variable in lacto-fermentation. 2–3% salt by weight (relative to vegetables) is the standard range. Too little: undesirable bacteria compete. Too much: fermentation slows or stalls. Salt draws water out of vegetables via osmosis, creating the brine. It selects for salt-tolerant Lactobacillus while suppressing mold and pathogens. Kosher salt or non-iodized sea salt — iodine inhibits fermentation.

2–3% saltNon-iodizedOsmosis

pH and Anaerobic Environment

Fermentation jars should be kept anaerobic (oxygen-free) to prevent mold and kahm yeast (harmless white surface film, but unpleasant). Weights keep vegetables submerged below the brine. Airlock lids allow CO₂ to escape while blocking oxygen. Starting pH is neutral (6–7); finished ferments reach pH 3.5–4. This acidity is what preserves the food for months.

AnaerobicpH 3.5–4Keep submerged

Temperature Effects

Temperature is the primary lever for fermentation speed and flavor. 65–75°F: ideal lacto-fermentation range, active but controlled. Below 55°F: very slow (fridge), good for cold-proofing sourdough or slowing kimchi aging. Above 80°F: faster fermentation but more acetic acid — sharper, more tangy. In hot climates, ferments go sour quickly; move to a cool basement or use less salt.

65–75°F idealCold = slowHeat = more acetic

Benefits

Fermented foods offer: probiotics (live cultures supporting gut microbiome), enhanced nutrient bioavailability (fermentation predigests phytates that block mineral absorption), preservation (lactic acid extends shelf life for months), reduced lactose in dairy ferments, and dramatically increased flavor complexity. B vitamins synthesized by bacteria. The microbial diversity in traditional ferments exceeds commercial probiotic supplements.

ProbioticsNutrient bioavailabilityPreservation

Kimchi

Baechu-Kimchi (Traditional)

The classic — napa cabbage kimchi. Cabbage quartered or halved and salted heavily for 1–2 hours (or overnight). Rinsed, dried. Paste: gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes), fish sauce or salted shrimp (jeot), garlic, ginger, green onion. Mixed thoroughly. Pack into jar, press down hard to eliminate air pockets. Room temp 1–2 days, then refrigerate. Peaks at 1–2 weeks; continues developing for months.

Napa cabbageGochugaru1–2 days room temp

Kkakdugi (Radish Kimchi)

Cubed daikon radish kimchi — crunchier and quicker to ferment than baechu. Radish cut into 3/4" cubes, salted briefly, then tossed with the same paste. The radish's higher water content means it doesn't need as much brining time. Ready in 1–3 days. Spicier and more pungent than baechu. Often served alongside soup, especially seolleongtang (ox bone soup).

DaikonCrunchyReady in 1–3 days

Oi Sobagi (Cucumber Kimchi)

Stuffed cucumber kimchi — cut cucumbers are scored with a cross cut and stuffed with a spicy paste of green onion, garlic, ginger, gochugaru, and sometimes carrots or chives. Ferments very quickly (a few hours to overnight) due to cucumber's thin cell walls. Not a long-storage kimchi — best eaten within a week. Refreshing and crispy; perfect in summer.

CucumberStuffedEat within 1 week

Mak Kimchi (Quick/Easy)

The "rough" or "easy" kimchi — cabbage cut into bite-sized pieces rather than keeping the head intact. Quicker preparation: no need to quarter the cabbage and spread paste leaf-by-leaf. Same ingredients as baechu. Great for beginners and for making large batches for cooking (kimchi fried rice, kimchi jjigae). Less elegant presentation but identical flavor.

Bite-sized piecesBeginner-friendlyCooking kimchi

Mul Kimchi (Water Kimchi)

A mild, soup-like kimchi in a clear brine. No gochugaru — pale pink color from a small amount of beet or gentle red pepper. Made with napa cabbage, radish, green onion, garlic, and ginger in a lightly salted, slightly sweet water brine. Refreshing and mild enough for those who can't eat spicy food. Often served as a palate cleanser between spicy dishes.

No gochugaruMildLiquid brine

Vegan Kimchi

Traditional kimchi uses fish sauce or salted shrimp for umami and fermentation support. Vegan substitutes: kelp powder + soy sauce, miso, or fermented soybean paste (doenjang). The umami is slightly different but still excellent. Some Korean families have always made vegan kimchi using only vegetables. The fermentation process is identical — salt + lactic acid bacteria do the work.

Kelp powderMiso substituteSame fermentation

Fermentation Stages

Day 1–2 (fresh, geotjeori): bright, spicy, crunchy — eaten immediately. Day 3–7 (well-fermented,익은): tangy, complex, bubbling — the peak for most people. Day 2–8 weeks (aged, pungent): intensely sour, funky; excellent for kimchi jjigae and kimchi fried rice where the flavors meld with heat. Years-aged kimchi (mukeunji): prized delicacy — sour and complex beyond description.

Fresh = geotjeoriPeak at 1 weekAged for cooking

Sourdough

Building a Starter

Mix equal weights of flour and water (100% hydration). Leave at room temperature. Wild yeast and lactobacillus from flour and environment colonize the mixture. Feed daily by discarding half and adding fresh flour and water. Days 1–3: may smell unpleasant (enterobacteria colonizing). Days 4–7: starter becomes frothy and yeasty. Day 7–14: stable starter ready to bake with. Whole wheat or rye flour accelerates the process.

Flour + waterDaily feeding7–14 days

Feeding Schedule

Room-temperature starter: feed daily (1:1:1 ratio — 1 part starter : 1 part flour : 1 part water). Refrigerator storage: feed once a week; take out the night before baking to activate. The 1:2:2 or 1:5:5 ratio feeds less frequently and keeps the starter less acidic (milder flavor). High ratios (1:10:10) are used to reduce sourness and build mild, active starter for competitions or delicate bakes.

1:1:1 standardFridge = weeklyRatio controls sourness

Float Test & Peak Activity

Drop a small amount of starter into water — if it floats, it's active with CO₂ bubbles trapped throughout. Bake at "peak" — when the starter has doubled (or more) and is domed on top, before it collapses. Peak timing: 4–8 hours after feeding at 75°F. A starter that has peaked and collapsed is still usable but produces a denser, more sour loaf. The levain (pre-ferment built from starter) is preferred for baking.

Float testBake at peakLevain method

Hydration Levels

65% hydration: firm, easy to shape, tight crumb — good for sandwiches. 75%: balanced — open crumb, some shaping challenge. 80–85%: very open, irregular crumb — beautiful for artisan loaves but requires experience. 90%+: extremely slack dough — almost impossible to shape by hand. High hydration requires more stretch-and-fold sets and confidence. Whole wheat absorbs more water — add 5% hydration per 10% whole wheat substitution.

65% = firm80%+ = open crumbWW absorbs more

Bulk Fermentation

After mixing, the dough ferments at room temperature for 4–12 hours (depending on temperature and starter strength). During this time, perform stretch-and-fold sets every 30 minutes for the first 2–3 hours. The dough should increase 50–75% in volume and feel airy, with bubbles visible on the sides. Warmer temperatures speed bulk; cooler temps slow it. Over-fermented dough becomes slack and doesn't hold shape.

4–12 hours50–75% riseStretch and fold

Cold Proof (Retard)

After shaping, the loaf goes into a lined banneton or loaf pan, covered, into the fridge for 8–18 hours (or up to 36 hours). Cold slows fermentation and allows continued flavor development. The cold dough is also easier to score cleanly. Bake straight from the fridge — the thermal shock helps oven spring. Cold proofing is the most reliable way to develop complex flavor without over-fermenting.

8–18 hours coldEasier scoringMore flavor

Kombucha

SCOBY Explained

Symbiotic Culture Of Bacteria and Yeast. A rubbery, cellulose disc produced by Acetobacter bacteria as a protective mat. Not technically required (liquid culture works fine) but traditional. The SCOBY grows larger with each batch; remove the top layers periodically. Store extra SCOBYs in a "SCOBY hotel" — a jar with starter liquid and a loose lid. SCOBYs can be dried, composted, or eaten.

Symbiotic cultureCellulose matOptional but traditional

First Fermentation (F1)

Brew sweet tea: 1 gallon water, 1 cup sugar, 4–6 tea bags (black tea is traditional; green tea gives a milder flavor). Cool to room temperature. Add SCOBY and 1–2 cups starter liquid (previous batch or raw apple cider vinegar). Cover with breathable cloth (not airtight). Ferment 7–14 days at 68–78°F. Taste daily after day 7. Done when pleasantly tart. pH should reach 2.5–3.5.

7–14 dayspH 2.5–3.5Breathable cover

Second Fermentation (F2) — Carbonation

Bottle the F1 liquid in flip-top bottles (Grolsch-style). Add flavorings — ginger, fruit juice, berries, citrus. Seal tightly. Leave at room temperature 2–5 days. The residual yeast ferments residual sugar, producing CO₂ that dissolves into the liquid under pressure. "Burp" bottles daily to check pressure and prevent explosion. Move to fridge when fizzy enough. Cold stops fermentation.

2–5 daysBurp dailyFlip-top bottles

Flavoring Options

Ginger + lemon (classic): 1 tsp fresh ginger juice + squeeze of lemon per 16oz bottle. Mixed berry: 2 tbsp frozen berries per bottle. Elderflower: 2 tbsp elderflower cordial. Hibiscus: 1 tsp dried hibiscus flowers (beautiful magenta color). Mango + habanero: exotic heat. Lavender + blueberry. Passion fruit. The sugar in fruit juice drives F2 carbonation. Avoid: citrus pith (bitter), artificial sweeteners (yeast can't ferment them).

Ginger + lemonFruit juiceDried flowers

pH Monitoring

Test pH throughout fermentation with cheap pH strips or a digital meter. Starting pH (sweet tea): ~7. After F1: target 2.5–3.5. Below 2.5 = over-fermented, too vinegary. Above 3.5 = under-fermented, too sweet. pH strips accurate to ±0.5 — adequate for home brewing. Calibrated digital meters (±0.01) for those who want precision. A healthy, active F1 batch should reach target pH in 7–10 days at 74°F.

Target pH 2.5–3.5pH strips workDigital for precision

Continuous Brew Method

Use a large vessel (1–2.5 gallon) with a spigot at the bottom. Harvest kombucha from the spigot daily, replacing with fresh sweet tea. The large volume creates a stable pH environment, reducing risk of contamination. Flavor develops more complexity over time. Requires weekly cleaning every 2–3 months. Produces a milder, more consistent kombucha than batch brewing. Popular with daily drinkers.

Large vesselSpigot harvestMilder flavor

Vinegar

Apple Cider Vinegar (Raw)

Made from fermented apple juice. Two stages: alcoholic fermentation (yeast converts apple sugars to hard cider), then acetic fermentation (Acetobacter converts alcohol to acetic acid). Raw, unfiltered ACV contains "the mother" — strands of beneficial bacteria. Pasteurized commercial ACV has no mother. Used in salad dressings, wellness tonics, marinades, and as a meat tenderizer.

Two-stageThe motherUnfiltered = raw

Wine Vinegar

Red wine vinegar: bold, fruity, used in vinaigrettes and Mediterranean cooking. White wine vinegar: lighter, more delicate. Champagne vinegar: the mildest wine vinegar, excellent for delicate sauces. Quality matters enormously — cheap wine vinegar is harsh and one-dimensional. Aged wine vinegar (in wood barrels) develops complex, mellow flavor. Sherry vinegar (from Andalusia) is aged in a solera system and is worth seeking out.

Red/white/champagneBarrel agingSherry = premium

Balsamic Vinegar

Traditional Balsamic (Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena): aged 12–25+ years in a series of progressively smaller barrels (solera). Made from Trebbiano grape must. Sweet, thick, complex — used sparingly as a condiment. Very expensive ($100–$300+ for 100ml). Commercial balsamic (what most people buy) is wine vinegar with caramel coloring and sweetener — entirely different product. The DOP label guarantees authenticity.

12–25 year agingGrape mustDOP = authentic

Rice Vinegar

Made from fermented rice wine (sake). The mildest, least acidic common vinegar. Essential in Japanese cuisine: seasoning sushi rice, making ponzu, pickling ginger. Chinese black vinegar (Chinkiang) is made from glutinous rice — dark, malty, complex. Korean rice vinegar is similar to Japanese but slightly less sweet. Seasoned rice vinegar has added sugar and salt — convenient for sushi rice but not a 1:1 substitute in other applications.

Mildest aciditySushi riceChinkiang = black

Making Your Own

Start with homemade or commercial wine/cider at 5–10% ABV. Pour into a wide-mouth jar with a cloth cover (needs oxygen). Add a vinegar mother if available; otherwise let wild Acetobacter colonize. In 4–8 weeks at 65–80°F, the alcohol converts to acetic acid. Keep away from heat and light. Taste-test: should be sharp with no alcohol taste. Filter through cheesecloth; age in a cool dark place.

4–8 weeksNeeds oxygenWide-mouth jar

Dairy Ferments

Yogurt

Thermophilic (heat-loving) bacteria — Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus — ferment lactose to lactic acid. Heat milk to 180°F (denatures whey proteins for thicker texture), cool to 110°F, add 2 tbsp live-culture yogurt as starter, incubate at 110°F for 6–12 hours. Longer = tangier and thicker. Strain through cheesecloth for Greek yogurt. Mesophilic yogurt (viili, filmjölk) ferments at room temperature.

110°F incubation6–12 hoursStrain for Greek

Kefir

Milk kefir grains (polysaccharide-protein matrix colonized by bacteria and yeast) ferment milk at room temperature in 24–48 hours. More probiotic diversity than yogurt — 30–50 bacterial strains vs yogurt's 2–3. Slightly effervescent and thinner than yogurt. Grains grow with each batch — share the extras. Water kefir uses different grains to ferment sugar water or coconut water. Both are powerful probiotic sources.

Room temperature24–48 hours30+ bacterial strains

Crème Fraîche & Sour Cream

Crème fraîche: heavy cream (36%+ fat) fermented with mesophilic culture (buttermilk works as starter) at room temperature for 12–24 hours. Thicker and richer than sour cream; fat content makes it heat-stable (won't curdle in hot sauces). Sour cream: lower fat cream with the same process. Commercial crème fraîche uses specific mesophilic cultures. The high fat buffers the acidity for a mild, tangy result.

Heavy creamRoom temp 12–24 hrHeat-stable

Cultured Butter

Made by churning crème fraîche (fermented cream) instead of sweet cream. The fermentation adds lactic acid, creating a more complex, tangy flavor prized in European-style butter. Common in Denmark, France, and Germany. The same fat content as regular butter (~82%) but with the flavor complexity of the ferment. Exceptional on bread or finishing sauces. Worth making at home from your crème fraîche.

Churned crème fraîcheTangy complex flavorEuropean style

Labneh (Strained Yogurt Cheese)

Middle Eastern fresh cheese made by straining yogurt through cheesecloth for 12–24 hours. The result is thick, spreadable, and tangy — somewhere between cream cheese and goat cheese in texture. Season with olive oil, za'atar, and salt. Roll into balls and store in olive oil for months. The liquid whey drained off is nutritious — use in smoothies, bread, or soup stock. No rennet, no cooking needed.

Strained yogurt12–24 hoursSpreadable cheese

Other Ferments

Miso

Japanese fermented soybean paste made with koji (Aspergillus oryzae mold grown on rice, barley, or soybeans). Koji produces enzymes that break down protein and starch into amino acids and sugars — deep umami. White miso (shiro): short fermentation 1–3 months, mild and sweet. Red miso (aka): 1–3+ years, intense and salty. Mixed (awase): blend for balance. Always add to soup after turning off heat to preserve beneficial enzymes.

Koji enzymesMonths to yearsDeep umami

Tempeh

Indonesian fermented soybean cake. Rhizopus oligosporus mold (and related species) grow through cooked, dehulled soybeans in solid-state fermentation. The white mycelium binds beans into a firm, sliceable cake with a mushroom-like, nutty flavor. Ferments at 85–88°F for 24–48 hours. Higher protein digestibility than unfermented soybeans. Phytic acid largely eliminated. Slice and pan-fry for crispy edges or marinate and grill.

Rhizopus mold85–88°F24–48 hours

Sauerkraut

Finely shredded cabbage lacto-fermented with only salt (2% by weight). Massage the cabbage vigorously after salting to break cell walls and release liquid — this becomes the brine. Pack tightly into a jar, press below brine, and ferment 1–4 weeks at cool room temperature (65–75°F). German and Alsatian cuisine cornerstone. Aged sauerkraut (months in a crock) develops profound complexity. Rich in Vitamin C — historically prevented scurvy on ships.

2% salt1–4 weeksCabbage only

Fermented Hot Sauce

Blend chiles with 2–3% salt, ferment 5–14 days in an airlock jar. The lacto-fermentation adds complexity and fruity depth that fresh hot sauces lack — the acidity is rounder and more integrated. After fermentation, blend with vinegar (to taste), strain, bottle. Tabasco is fermented in white oak barrels for up to 3 years. Fermented habanero-mango sauce: intensely fruity with building heat. Keeps for months refrigerated.

2–3% saltAirlock5–14 days

Pickles (Lacto vs Vinegar)

True lacto-fermented pickles: cucumbers in salt brine (3–5%), fermented 3–7 days. Crunchy, complex, probiotic — the original "dill pickle." Requires a tannin source (grape leaf, oak leaf, or horseradish leaf) to keep pickles crisp. Vinegar pickles (quick pickles): cucumber submerged in hot vinegar brine — ready in 24 hours but contain no live cultures and have a simpler flavor. "Vlasic" style is vinegar; traditional Jewish deli pickles are lacto.

Lacto = probioticTannin for crunchVinegar = no cultures

Beer & Wine Basics

Beer: malted barley steeped in hot water (mash) to extract fermentable sugars, boiled with hops, cooled, pitched with brewer's yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae for ales, S. pastorianus for lagers), fermented 1–4 weeks. Wine: crushed grapes (natural yeast on skins or added wine yeast) ferment juice 1–4 weeks. Wild/natural wine uses only native yeasts. Both require clean sanitized equipment — the enemy is wild bacteria and mold, not the intended yeast.

Mash → boil → pitch1–4 weeksSanitation critical
