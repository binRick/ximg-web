Sourdough

Building a Starter

Mix equal weights of flour and water (100% hydration). Leave at room temperature. Wild yeast and lactobacillus from flour and environment colonize the mixture. Feed daily by discarding half and adding fresh flour and water. Days 1–3: may smell unpleasant (enterobacteria colonizing). Days 4–7: starter becomes frothy and yeasty. Day 7–14: stable starter ready to bake with. Whole wheat or rye flour accelerates the process.

Feeding Schedule

Room-temperature starter: feed daily (1:1:1 ratio — 1 part starter : 1 part flour : 1 part water). Refrigerator storage: feed once a week; take out the night before baking to activate. The 1:2:2 or 1:5:5 ratio feeds less frequently and keeps the starter less acidic (milder flavor). High ratios (1:10:10) are used to reduce sourness and build mild, active starter for competitions or delicate bakes.

Float Test & Peak Activity

Drop a small amount of starter into water — if it floats, it's active with CO₂ bubbles trapped throughout. Bake at "peak" — when the starter has doubled (or more) and is domed on top, before it collapses. Peak timing: 4–8 hours after feeding at 75°F. A starter that has peaked and collapsed is still usable but produces a denser, more sour loaf. The levain (pre-ferment built from starter) is preferred for baking.

Hydration Levels

65% hydration: firm, easy to shape, tight crumb — good for sandwiches. 75%: balanced — open crumb, some shaping challenge. 80–85%: very open, irregular crumb — beautiful for artisan loaves but requires experience. 90%+: extremely slack dough — almost impossible to shape by hand. High hydration requires more stretch-and-fold sets and confidence. Whole wheat absorbs more water — add 5% hydration per 10% whole wheat substitution.

Bulk Fermentation

After mixing, the dough ferments at room temperature for 4–12 hours (depending on temperature and starter strength). During this time, perform stretch-and-fold sets every 30 minutes for the first 2–3 hours. The dough should increase 50–75% in volume and feel airy, with bubbles visible on the sides. Warmer temperatures speed bulk; cooler temps slow it. Over-fermented dough becomes slack and doesn't hold shape.

Cold Proof (Retard)

After shaping, the loaf goes into a lined banneton or loaf pan, covered, into the fridge for 8–18 hours (or up to 36 hours). Cold slows fermentation and allows continued flavor development. The cold dough is also easier to score cleanly. Bake straight from the fridge — the thermal shock helps oven spring. Cold proofing is the most reliable way to develop complex flavor without over-fermenting.
