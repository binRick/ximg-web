Dairy Ferments

Yogurt

Thermophilic (heat-loving) bacteria — Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus — ferment lactose to lactic acid. Heat milk to 180°F (denatures whey proteins for thicker texture), cool to 110°F, add 2 tbsp live-culture yogurt as starter, incubate at 110°F for 6–12 hours. Longer = tangier and thicker. Strain through cheesecloth for Greek yogurt. Mesophilic yogurt (viili, filmjölk) ferments at room temperature.

Kefir

Milk kefir grains (polysaccharide-protein matrix colonized by bacteria and yeast) ferment milk at room temperature in 24–48 hours. More probiotic diversity than yogurt — 30–50 bacterial strains vs yogurt's 2–3. Slightly effervescent and thinner than yogurt. Grains grow with each batch — share the extras. Water kefir uses different grains to ferment sugar water or coconut water. Both are powerful probiotic sources.

Crème Fraîche & Sour Cream

Crème fraîche: heavy cream (36%+ fat) fermented with mesophilic culture (buttermilk works as starter) at room temperature for 12–24 hours. Thicker and richer than sour cream; fat content makes it heat-stable (won't curdle in hot sauces). Sour cream: lower fat cream with the same process. Commercial crème fraîche uses specific mesophilic cultures. The high fat buffers the acidity for a mild, tangy result.

Cultured Butter

Made by churning crème fraîche (fermented cream) instead of sweet cream. The fermentation adds lactic acid, creating a more complex, tangy flavor prized in European-style butter. Common in Denmark, France, and Germany. The same fat content as regular butter (~82%) but with the flavor complexity of the ferment. Exceptional on bread or finishing sauces. Worth making at home from your crème fraîche.

Labneh (Strained Yogurt Cheese)

Middle Eastern fresh cheese made by straining yogurt through cheesecloth for 12–24 hours. The result is thick, spreadable, and tangy — somewhere between cream cheese and goat cheese in texture. Season with olive oil, za'atar, and salt. Roll into balls and store in olive oil for months. The liquid whey drained off is nutritious — use in smoothies, bread, or soup stock. No rennet, no cooking needed.
