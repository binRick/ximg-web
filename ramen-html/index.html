Ramen

Japan's most beloved noodle soup — four broths, infinite variations

The Four Principal Broth Styles

Every bowl of ramen begins with two elements: the tare (seasoning concentrate) and the soup base (usually chicken, pork, or dashi). The tare defines the style; the base determines the richness. These four tare styles are the foundation of all Japanese ramen.

Shoyu — Soy Sauce

Tokyo · Classic Style

The original ramen of Tokyo's Shinjuku and Koenji. Clear, brown broth made from chicken or chicken-pork blend seasoned with a soy sauce tare. Complex umami from the soy, subtle sweetness, clean finish. Light to medium-bodied. The most delicate of the four.

Medium FatClear BrothTokyoWavy Noodles

Shio — Salt

Hakodate · Lightest Style

The oldest ramen style — salt-seasoned, typically with chicken or seafood base. Pale gold to clear broth. The lightest and most delicate of all styles, allowing the quality of the soup base to shine through. Hakodate in Hokkaido claims it as their own. Often finished with a wedge of lemon or yuzu.

Low FatPale BrothHokkaidoStraight Noodles

Miso

Sapporo · Rich & Robust

Sapporo's gift to ramen. Fermented soybean paste (miso) mixed with pork or chicken stock creates a rich, opaque, deeply savory broth. Often topped with corn, butter, and bean sprouts. Medium to heavy body. The miso adds probiotic complexity and a slightly funky, fermented depth no other style matches.

High FatOpaque BrothSapporoThick Wavy

Tonkotsu — Pork Bone

Fukuoka · Hakata Style

Pork bones boiled at a rolling boil for 12–18 hours until the collagen and marrow emulsify into a creamy white broth. Intensely rich, slightly sulfurous, unmistakably porcine. Fukuoka's Hakata style: thin straight noodles, chashu, soft egg, black garlic oil. Hardest style to make, most polarizing flavor.

Toppings — The art of the bowl

A ramen bowl is composed like a painting. Each topping contributes a different texture, flavor, or visual element. The combination of toppings signals the regional style as much as the broth does.

Chashu Pork
チャーシュー
Pork belly or shoulder rolled tight, tied, then braised or slow-roasted in soy, mirin, sake, and sugar. Sliced into tender rounds. The crown jewel of most ramen bowls.
Soft-Boiled Egg
味玉 · Ajitsuke Tamago
Egg boiled exactly 6–7 minutes, peeled, then marinated in soy sauce, mirin, and sake. Jammy, custard-like yolk with savory-sweet exterior. The most anticipated bite in the bowl.
Nori
海苔
Sheet of dried seaweed standing upright in the broth. As it softens, it absorbs the broth and becomes a delicious, umami-packed leaf. Visually iconic in the bowl.
Menma
メンマ · Bamboo Shoots
Fermented bamboo shoots — crunchy, slightly sour, soy-marinated. A classic ramen topping that provides textural contrast against the tender noodles and pork.
Green Onion
ネギ
Thinly sliced scallion sprinkled on the surface. Fresh, pungent, cutting through the richness of fatty broths. Some shops have a dedicated green onion station to customize.
Corn
コーン
Kernels of sweet corn — a Sapporo miso ramen signature. Adds bursts of sweetness against the savory miso base. Often added with a knob of butter that melts into the broth.
Butter
バター
A cube of butter placed on Hokkaido ramen — melts into the broth, enriching it further and adding a sweet dairy roundness. Divisive but deeply satisfying in cold-weather ramen.
Narutomaki
なると巻き
White fish cake with a pink spiral design — the classic ramen topping from illustrations. Named after the Naruto whirlpools. More decorative than flavorful; chewy, mild, nostalgic.
Bean Sprouts
もやし
Mung bean sprouts — crispy, fresh, adds lightness to rich broths. Standard in miso and tonkotsu bowls. Often stir-fried briefly in sesame oil before adding.
Black Garlic Oil
黒マー油
Charred garlic blended with sesame oil — swirled on tonkotsu ramen to add smoky, almost burnt garlic depth. The defining flavor note of Kumamoto-style ramen.
Tofu
豆腐
Silken or firm tofu cubes. Common in vegetarian ramen and some miso styles. Absorbs broth flavor. A cooling, protein-rich addition.
Sesame Seeds
ごま
Toasted white or black sesame sprinkled on top. Nutty aroma, slight crunch. Especially prominent in tantanmen (spicy sesame ramen) where sesame is a structural flavor.
Kikurage Mushroom
キクラゲ
Wood ear mushrooms — sliced thin, slippery, slightly crunchy. Classic tonkotsu topping. Almost tasteless alone but carries broth flavor beautifully.
Spinach or Bok Choy
青菜
Blanched greens placed neatly beside other toppings. Adds color, mild bitterness, and lightness to otherwise rich bowls. Standard in shoyu and shio styles.

Noodles — Matching noodle to broth

Ramen noodles are made from wheat flour, water, and kansui — an alkaline mineral water that gives ramen noodles their characteristic yellow color and springy texture. The thickness, waviness, and density of the noodle must be matched to the broth's viscosity.

Thin Straight Noodles
細ストレート麺
Standard for Hakata tonkotsu. The thin noodle surfaces let the rich broth cling tightly. Cooks in 60–90 seconds. Hakata shops offer kaedama — a refill of noodles into your remaining broth.
Wavy Medium Noodles
縮れ麺 (中)
The most versatile ramen noodle. Wavy texture traps broth in the folds. Standard for Tokyo shoyu, many regional styles. Coils in the bowl beautifully. Perfect medium between delicate and hearty.
Thick Wavy Noodles
太縮れ麺
Classic Sapporo miso noodle. Wide, heavily wavy, substantial chew. Matches the assertive, rich miso broth. Takes 3–4 minutes to cook. Stays al dente longer without going mushy.
Flat Wide Noodles
平打ち麺
Kitakata-style — unusually flat and wide, hand-cut appearance. Chewy, rustic texture. Pairs with a light shoyu broth. Visually distinctive; more similar to pasta than typical ramen noodles.
Curly Noodles
縮れ麺 (強)
Tightly curled, springy. Common in tsukemen (dipping ramen) where the noodle must hold its texture out of the broth. The curls create pockets that hold dipping sauce.
Egg Noodles (Chukamen)
中華麺
The original ramen noodle with a higher egg content — richer yellow color, more tender bite. The standard Chinese-style noodle adapted for Japanese ramen. Medium thickness.

Regional Ramen — Japan's noodle geography

Every major Japanese city and prefecture has developed its own distinct ramen style — different broths, noodles, toppings, and serving temperatures shaped by local ingredients, climate, and culture. The ramen map of Japan is staggering in variety.

Sapporo Ramen
札幌ラーメン · Hokkaido
Rich miso base with corn, butter, and bean sprouts. Thick wavy noodles. Born in 1955 at Aji no Sanpei restaurant. Designed for Hokkaido winters. The miso style that defined a genre.
Hakata Ramen
博多ラーメン · Fukuoka
Creamy tonkotsu broth, very thin straight noodles, minimal toppings. Kaedama (noodle refill) is standard. Fukuoka's Nakasu area is ramen paradise — open-air yatai stalls at midnight.
Tokyo Ramen
東京ラーメン
Classic shoyu style — chicken-pork broth with light soy tare. Slightly wavy noodles. Menma, chashu, nori, naruto. The "default" ramen that most of the world pictures. Clean and balanced.
Kitakata Ramen
喜多方ラーメン · Fukushima
Flat, wide, slightly wavy noodles in a clear pork-soy broth. A small city in Fukushima with over 120 ramen shops for 50,000 residents — the highest ramen density in Japan.
Kyoto Ramen
京都ラーメン
Thick, rich tonkotsu-shoyu hybrid. Darker and heavier than Tokyo shoyu. Pork back fat floating on the surface. Often served with chicken-garlic oil (menma butter). Surprisingly bold for refined Kyoto.
Kumamoto Ramen
熊本ラーメン
Tonkotsu base but lighter than Hakata — clearer, less cloudy. Defined by mayu (black garlic oil) and fried garlic chips on top. Medium-thick noodles. A balance between Hakata richness and Tokyo clarity.
Asahikawa Ramen
旭川ラーメン · Hokkaido
Double-dashi broth combining pork bones AND dried seafood. Soy-based, but seafood-forward. Wavy medium noodles. Fat floats on the surface to retain heat in Hokkaido's extreme winters.
Onomichi Ramen
尾道ラーメン · Hiroshima
Flat, wide noodles in a soy-chicken-pork broth with floating pork back fat. The back fat is the signature — rendering into the broth as you eat, continuously enriching it.

Tantanmen, Tsukemen & Beyond

Beyond the four classic styles, Japanese ramen culture has spawned entire sub-genres with their own devoted followings. These styles often push further in terms of spice, technique, or presentation.

Tantanmen
担々麺
Japanese adaptation of Chinese dan dan noodles. Spicy sesame-based broth (or sauce), ground pork, bok choy, sesame oil. Available with or without broth. Rich, nutty, fiery — a totally different profile from standard ramen.
Tsukemen
つけ麺 · Dipping Ramen
Noodles served cold (or room temp) separately from a thick, concentrated dipping broth. You dip each bite of noodles into the broth before eating. Popularized by Yamagishi Kazuo in Tokyo in the 1960s. Deep, complex broth — usually tonkotsu-shoyu concentrate.
Mazemen / Abura Soba
まぜ麺 · 油そば
Brothless ramen — noodles tossed with a sauce (soy + sesame oil + vinegar + tare) and toppings directly in the bowl. Mix everything together before eating. Higher noodle-to-sauce ratio; intensely flavored, no dilution from broth.
Spicy Miso Ramen
辛味噌ラーメン
Sapporo miso base with added togarashi (chili flakes), doubanjiang (fermented chili bean paste), or house chili oil. Heat builds with each sip. The miso base absorbs chili flavors differently than a clear broth would.
Vegetarian / Vegan Ramen
精進ラーメン
Kombu (kelp) and shiitake-based dashi with soy tare. Toppings: tofu, mushrooms, corn, nori, vegetable chashu. The umami from kombu and dried shiitake can match animal-based broths in depth when done well.
Curry Ramen
カレーラーメン
Japanese curry spices blended into the broth — turmeric, cumin, coriander. Sapporo-area specialty. Yellow-orange broth, mild spice, rich with coconut or pork base. A comfort food crossover between Japan's two great stewed dishes.

How to Eat Ramen — The full experience

01
Slurping is mandatory and respectfulIn Japanese food culture, slurping noodles is completely acceptable — it cools the noodles as they enter the mouth and aerates the broth across your palate, enhancing flavor. Silence while eating ramen is unusual; slurping is expected.
02
Eat the noodles first, within 3 minutesRamen noodles continue cooking in the hot broth. Most ramen experts say the window for perfect noodle texture is the first 3–5 minutes. Start with the noodles, then move to toppings, sipping broth throughout.
03
Drink the broth — it's the best partThe broth is what the chef spent hours or days creating. Drinking it directly from the bowl at the end is standard practice in Japan. The concentrated flavors at the bottom of the bowl are often the most intense sips.
04
Use the toppings as interval breaksChashu, soft egg, and nori can be eaten as standalone bites between noodle slurps, or dunked into the broth. The soft egg yolk mixed into the remaining broth is a traditional last-minute enrichment.
05
Customize with table condimentsMost ramen shops provide table condiments: sesame seeds, white pepper, vinegar, togarashi (chili flakes), and sometimes garlic press or extra tare. Apply after tasting the bowl as designed — then adjust. Never before.
06
Kaedama — request a noodle refill (Hakata only)In Fukuoka's Hakata-style shops, when your noodles are nearly finished but broth remains, you can shout "kaedama!" for a fresh serving of noodles dropped into your remaining broth. It's a specific Hakata tradition.
07
Ramen is not a shared dishRamen is an individual meal — each person has their own bowl, tailored to their order. Unlike many Japanese dishes served family-style, ramen is an intimate, personal experience. Counter seating facing the kitchen is traditional for solo diners.
