Regional Ramen — Japan's noodle geography

Every major Japanese city and prefecture has developed its own distinct ramen style — different broths, noodles, toppings, and serving temperatures shaped by local ingredients, climate, and culture. The ramen map of Japan is staggering in variety.

Sapporo Ramen
札幌ラーメン · Hokkaido
Rich miso base with corn, butter, and bean sprouts. Thick wavy noodles. Born in 1955 at Aji no Sanpei restaurant. Designed for Hokkaido winters. The miso style that defined a genre.
Hakata Ramen
博多ラーメン · Fukuoka
Creamy tonkotsu broth, very thin straight noodles, minimal toppings. Kaedama (noodle refill) is standard. Fukuoka's Nakasu area is ramen paradise — open-air yatai stalls at midnight.
Tokyo Ramen
東京ラーメン
Classic shoyu style — chicken-pork broth with light soy tare. Slightly wavy noodles. Menma, chashu, nori, naruto. The "default" ramen that most of the world pictures. Clean and balanced.
Kitakata Ramen
喜多方ラーメン · Fukushima
Flat, wide, slightly wavy noodles in a clear pork-soy broth. A small city in Fukushima with over 120 ramen shops for 50,000 residents — the highest ramen density in Japan.
Kyoto Ramen
京都ラーメン
Thick, rich tonkotsu-shoyu hybrid. Darker and heavier than Tokyo shoyu. Pork back fat floating on the surface. Often served with chicken-garlic oil (menma butter). Surprisingly bold for refined Kyoto.
Kumamoto Ramen
熊本ラーメン
Tonkotsu base but lighter than Hakata — clearer, less cloudy. Defined by mayu (black garlic oil) and fried garlic chips on top. Medium-thick noodles. A balance between Hakata richness and Tokyo clarity.
Asahikawa Ramen
旭川ラーメン · Hokkaido
Double-dashi broth combining pork bones AND dried seafood. Soy-based, but seafood-forward. Wavy medium noodles. Fat floats on the surface to retain heat in Hokkaido's extreme winters.
Onomichi Ramen
尾道ラーメン · Hiroshima
Flat, wide noodles in a soy-chicken-pork broth with floating pork back fat. The back fat is the signature — rendering into the broth as you eat, continuously enriching it.
