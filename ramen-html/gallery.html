Hakata Tonkotsu
Fukuoka's iconic creamy white pork-bone broth with thin straight noodles, chashu, soft egg, and nori.
Sapporo Miso Ramen
Hokkaido's signature — a rich miso broth topped with corn, a pat of butter, bean sprouts, and thick wavy noodles.
Tokyo Shoyu Ramen
Tokyo's classic soy-sauce ramen — a clear, amber chicken-pork broth with wavy noodles, menma, and narutomaki.
Shio Ramen
The lightest of the four styles — a pale gold salt-seasoned broth, delicate enough to let the quality of the dashi shine through.
Tsukemen (Dipping Ramen)
Noodles and a thick concentrated dipping broth served separately — you dip each tangle of noodles before eating.
Ichiran Solo Booth
Ichiran's famous individual dining booths in Fukuoka — designed to eliminate distractions and focus the diner entirely on the bowl.
Fresh Ramen Noodles
Kansui-alkaline wheat noodles give ramen its characteristic yellow tint and springy, slightly chewy texture.
Butter & Corn Ramen
A Hokkaido cold-weather classic — sweet corn and a melting cube of butter enrich the miso broth as you eat.
Tantanmen
Japanese adaptation of Chinese dan dan noodles — spicy sesame broth, ground pork, and bok choy in a rich, nutty bowl.
Tantanmen — Tokyo Style
A Tokyo shop's take on tantanmen: deep sesame and chili broth with a swirl of chili oil floating on top.
Abura Soba (Brothless)
Noodles tossed in a concentrated tare and sesame oil with no broth — intensely flavored, every bite rich with sauce.
Miso Ramen
Fermented soybean paste blended into rich pork stock — opaque, deeply savory, and warming in every sip.
Spicy Miso Ramen
Sapporo miso base fired up with doubanjiang and togarashi — heat builds with every sip as chili infuses the broth.
Kumamoto Ramen
A lighter tonkotsu than Hakata — topped with mayu (black garlic oil) and crispy fried garlic chips for smoky depth.
Kumamoto Bowl
The black garlic oil swirl is Kumamoto's signature — adding a charred, smoky note to the milky pork-bone base.
Kitakata Ramen
Flat, wide, slightly wavy noodles in a clear pork-shoyu broth — from the Japanese city with the highest ramen-shop density per capita.
Kitakata — Bannai Style
Bannai Shokudo is Kitakata's most famous shop — rustic, unfussy, and legendary for its hand-cut flat noodles.
Chashu Ramen
Generous slices of braised pork belly — slow-cooked in soy, mirin, and sake until meltingly tender — crown the bowl.
Ajitsuke Tamago
Marinated soft-boiled eggs — 6-minute cook, then bathed in soy-mirin overnight. The jammy, custard yolk is the most coveted bite.
Fukuoka Yatai Stall
Fukuoka's open-air yatai stalls — mobile food carts that line the Nakasu riverbank at night, serving tonkotsu until midnight.
Yatai Preparation
A Hakata yatai chef assembles bowls behind the counter — the tight workspace and theatre of preparation is part of the experience.
Ramen Shop Interior
The counter seat faces the kitchen — the traditional solo ramen experience, designed to focus attention entirely on the bowl.
Hand-Pulled Noodles
La mian technique — alkaline wheat dough stretched and folded repeatedly to develop the gluten network that gives ramen its signature springy bite.
Gyoza — The Classic Side
Pan-fried pork-and-cabbage dumplings are ramen's perfect companion — crispy on the bottom, juicy inside, dipped in rayu chili oil.
Gyoza Plate
A full plate of pan-fried gyoza — the standard side order at any ramen shop, especially in Fukuoka and Osaka.
