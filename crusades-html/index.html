The Crusades

Holy War for Jerusalem — Two Centuries of Conflict
1095 – 1291 AD · Christian & Islamic Worlds Collide
The Major Crusades
First Crusade (1096–1099)
Deus Vult — God Wills It
Called by Pope Urban II at the Council of Clermont. A combined force of 60,000 Europeans captures Jerusalem in 1099 after a brutal siege. The Crusader States — Jerusalem, Antioch, Edessa, and Tripoli — are established. Accompanied by the People's Crusade, which was annihilated.
Second Crusade (1147–1149)
Failure in the Holy Land
Called after Edessa falls to Zengi of Mosul. Led by King Louis VII of France and Emperor Conrad III of Germany. The disastrous siege of Damascus unified Islamic forces against the Crusaders. A humiliating failure that weakened the Crusader States.
Third Crusade (1189–1192)
Kings' Crusade
After Saladin recaptures Jerusalem in 1187, the three most powerful kings of Europe respond: Richard I (Lionheart), Philip II Augustus, and Frederick Barbarossa. Richard's leadership was brilliant — he won key battles but never retook Jerusalem. Ended in a truce allowing Christian pilgrimage.
Fourth Crusade (1202–1204)
The Crusade that Sacked Constantinople
Intended for Egypt but diverted by Venetian politics. Crusaders shockingly sacked Constantinople — a Christian city — in 1204, looting churches including the Hagia Sophia. Created the Latin Empire and permanently damaged Byzantine power. A defining moment of Christian division.
Children's Crusade (1212)
Tragic Innocence
Inspired by visions of children leading peaceful conversion, thousands of children from France and Germany marched to the Holy Land. Most never reached the Mediterranean; those who did were sold into slavery or died of disease. A cautionary tale of religious enthusiasm.
Ninth Crusade (1271–1272)
The Last Major Crusade
Led by Prince Edward (later Edward I of England). Achieved little militarily. The fall of Acre in 1291 — the last Crusader stronghold — ended 200 years of Crusader presence in the Holy Land and marked the definitive end of the Crusading era.
The Knightly Military Orders
Knights Templar
Founded 1119 · Dissolved 1312
The Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and of the Temple of Solomon. Founded to protect pilgrims, grew into a powerful military and banking organization. Created one of history's first banking systems. Suppressed by Philip IV of France in 1307; leaders burned at the stake.
Knights Hospitaller
Founded 1113 · Still Exists Today
Originally founded to provide care for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem. Evolved into a military order that defended Crusader territories. After losing the Holy Land, held Rhodes (1309–1522) then Malta (1530–1798). The Sovereign Military Order of Malta still operates today.
Teutonic Knights
Founded 1190 · Northern Crusades
German crusading order that shifted focus to the Baltic after the Crusades failed. Conquered and Christianized Prussia and the Baltic states through the Northern Crusades. Became a major power in medieval Germany; their legacy shaped modern Poland and Germany.
Saladin's Counter-Crusade
1187 · Reclaiming Jerusalem
Sultan Saladin (Salah ad-Din) united Egypt and Syria, then decisively defeated the Crusader army at the Battle of Hattin (1187), capturing Jerusalem. His chivalrous treatment of defeated enemies contrasted sharply with the First Crusade's massacres — admired even by European chroniclers.
Crusades Timeline
1095
Pope Urban II's Call
At the Council of Clermont, Urban II calls for a holy war to recapture Jerusalem from the Seljuk Turks. The crowd responds with "Deus Vult!" (God wills it!) — the First Crusade begins.
1099
Jerusalem Falls to the Crusaders
After a brutal 40-day siege, Crusaders enter Jerusalem. The sack is extraordinarily violent — contemporary accounts describe blood ankle-deep in the streets. The Kingdom of Jerusalem is established.
1187
Saladin Recaptures Jerusalem
After the disaster at Hattin (where Crusaders ran out of water and were slaughtered), Saladin takes Jerusalem. His merciful treatment of civilians — contrasting with 1099 — sets him apart as history's model of chivalry.
1204
Sack of Constantinople
The Fourth Crusade sacks the Christian city of Constantinople. The schism between Eastern and Western Christianity deepens permanently. Venice profits enormously from the loot — the four bronze horses now on St. Mark's Basilica came from here.
1291
Fall of Acre — End of the Crusades
The Mamluk Sultanate captures Acre, the last major Crusader stronghold. The remaining Crusader presence collapses. Two centuries of holy war end with the Holy Land firmly in Muslim hands.
