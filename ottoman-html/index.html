Ottoman Empire

The Sublime Porte — Bridging East and West for 600 Years
1299 – 1922 AD · Three Continents, One Empire
The Great Sultans
Osman I
1299–1326 · Founder of the Dynasty
A small Anatolian chieftain who founded the Ottoman dynasty by defeating the Byzantines at Bapheus. His name gave the empire its name: "Ottoman" is a corruption of "Osmanlı" — followers of Osman.
Mehmed II "the Conqueror"
1451–1481 · Fall of Constantinople
At age 21, Mehmed II achieved what Islamic rulers had attempted for 700 years — the conquest of Constantinople (1453). He built massive cannon and used innovative siege techniques. Renamed the city Istanbul and made it his imperial capital.
Suleiman the Magnificent
1520–1566 · The Lawgiver
The longest-reigning sultan, who brought the empire to its greatest extent. Known in the East as "the Lawgiver" for his legal reforms. His forces besieged Vienna (1529); his navy controlled the Mediterranean. A poet and patron of the arts.
Selim I "the Grim"
1512–1520 · Conqueror of the Middle East
Doubled the empire's size in just 8 years by conquering Egypt, Syria, Arabia, and the Hejaz. Became Caliph — guardian of the holy cities of Mecca and Medina — legitimizing Ottoman religious authority over all Sunni Muslims.
Ibrahim I "the Mad"
1640–1648 · Erratic Ruler
Known for his mental instability and expensive harem. Ordered the drowning of 280 of his concubines. His chaotic reign led to his deposition and execution by the Janissaries — one of history's most dramatic palace coups.
Mehmed VI
1918–1922 · Last Sultan
The last Ottoman sultan, who fled on a British warship in 1922 as Atatürk's forces took control. The sultanate was abolished; the Republic of Turkey proclaimed in 1923 — ending six centuries of Ottoman rule.
Ottoman Culture & Achievement
The Janissaries
Elite Infantry Corps
The Sultan's elite infantry — Christian boys taken through the devshirme system, converted, and trained from childhood as soldiers. Intensely loyal and technically advanced, they were the first professional standing army in Europe since Rome. Abolished in the 1826 "Auspicious Incident."
Hagia Sophia & the Mosques
Architecture of Faith
After conquering Constantinople, Mehmed II converted the Hagia Sophia into a mosque. The Ottoman architect Mimar Sinan then built the Süleymaniye and Selimiye mosques — engineering masterpieces that exceeded the Hagia Sophia's dome diameter.
The Harem
Topkapi's Inner World
The imperial harem at Topkapi Palace housed hundreds of women — consorts, wives, daughters, and slaves. Far from simple pleasure palaces, harems were complex political environments. Mothers of sultans (the Valide Sultan) wielded enormous behind-the-scenes power.
Ottoman Science & Medicine
Golden Age of Learning
Preserved and advanced Greek, Persian, and Arab knowledge. Produced advances in surgery, astronomy, and cartography. Piri Reis's 1513 world map showed the Americas with remarkable accuracy. The first smallpox inoculations in Europe came via Ottoman contacts.
Ottoman Timeline
1299
Founding of the Ottoman State
Osman I declares independence from the Seljuk Sultanate. His small principality in northwestern Anatolia begins expanding into Byzantine territory.
1453
Constantinople Falls
Mehmed II bombards the city's walls with massive cannon for 53 days. On May 29, 1453, Ottoman forces breach the walls. The Byzantine Empire ends after 1,100 years. The medieval world ends; the modern era begins.
1529
Siege of Vienna
Suleiman's forces besiege Vienna — the high-water mark of Ottoman expansion into Europe. The siege fails due to supply problems and weather. Europe breathes again; the Ottoman tide turns.
1571
Battle of Lepanto
The Holy League (Spain, Venice, Papacy) defeats the Ottoman fleet in the largest naval battle since antiquity. 400+ ships, 170,000 men. The battle ended Ottoman naval supremacy in the Mediterranean.
1683
Second Siege of Vienna
The Polish king Jan III Sobieski leads the largest cavalry charge in history, defeating the Ottoman army besieging Vienna. The Ottoman retreat marks the beginning of the empire's long decline in Europe.
1922
Fall of the Sultanate
After World War I defeat and the Turkish War of Independence, the Ottoman Sultanate is abolished. Mustafa Kemal Atatürk proclaims the Republic of Turkey — ending 623 years of Ottoman rule.

Ottoman Military Power
Janissaries
The Sultan's Elite Infantry
Founded by Murad I, the Janissaries were recruited through the devshirme — a levy of Christian boys who were converted to Islam, educated in Ottoman culture, and trained as professional soldiers. They became the most feared fighting force in the medieval world, but by the 18th century had become a praetorian guard, making and unmaking sultans.
Artillery
The Cannons of Constantinople
Mehmed II commissioned the Hungarian engineer Urban to cast massive cannons — the "Basilica" weighed 19 tons and fired stone balls weighing 544 kg. These bombards reduced the walls of Constantinople in 54 days in 1453, ending a 1,000-year empire. The Ottomans became the premier artillery power of the 15th century.
Sipahi
The Cavalry of the Timar System
Provincial cavalry granted land (timar) in exchange for military service. They formed the bulk of the Ottoman army outside the Janissaries. At peak power, the Ottoman Empire could mobilize 200,000 troops within weeks, drawn from across three continents and dozens of ethnic groups.
Ottoman Architecture
Sinan
Mimar Sinan — Master Architect (1489–1588)
Chief Ottoman architect under Suleiman the Magnificent, Sinan designed over 300 structures including the Süleymaniye Mosque and his masterpiece the Selimiye Mosque in Edirne. He solved the engineering problem that had defeated Byzantine architects: creating a stable, soaring dome larger than the Pantheon, using a revolutionary system of half-domes and flying buttresses.
Blue Mosque
Sultan Ahmed Mosque, 1616
The only mosque in Istanbul with six minarets (provoking outrage in Mecca, which also had six), the Blue Mosque is named for its 20,000 hand-painted blue Iznik tiles. Architect Sedefkâr Mehmed Ağa designed the massive cascade of domes to compete visually with the Hagia Sophia next door.
Topkapi Palace
Heart of the Ottoman World
For 400 years the administrative and residential center of the Ottoman Empire. The Harem contained up to 300 women, the Treasury held jewels from three continents, and the Divan (council chamber) was where sultans listened invisibly behind a grille while ministers debated state affairs — the sultan seen but not seeing was the Ottoman ideal of power.
